Wikipedia is not governed like a normal company product with a central editorial team. It is closer to a **constitutional commons**: millions of pages are editable by the public, but editing is constrained by policies, social norms, elected/trusted user roles, dispute processes, technical permissions, and a nonprofit legal owner/host.

The simplest model is:

> **Editors write. Community norms govern. Administrators enforce. ArbCom resolves severe conduct disputes. The Wikimedia Foundation hosts and intervenes only in legal/safety/system-level cases.**

## 1. Who “owns” Wikipedia?

Wikipedia is hosted by the **Wikimedia Foundation**, a nonprofit. The Foundation owns/operates the infrastructure, trademarks, legal policies, privacy framework, fundraising, software support, and global safety/legal processes. But it usually does **not** decide what an article should say. Wikipedia’s own policy page says Wikipedia is operated by the Foundation, which reserves certain legal rights, but “normally Wikipedia is a self-governing project run by its community.” ([Wikipedia][1])

That distinction is central:

| Layer                                        | What it controls                                                          |
| -------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Volunteer editors**                        | Article content, sourcing, wording, cleanup, discussions                  |
| **Local community**                          | Local policies, guidelines, consensus, dispute processes                  |
| **Administrators/bureaucrats/functionaries** | Technical enforcement tools                                               |
| **Arbitration Committee**                    | Serious conduct disputes on some Wikipedias                               |
| **Stewards/global functionaries**            | Cross-wiki emergency or global permissions work                           |
| **Wikimedia Foundation**                     | Legal, hosting, privacy, safety, Terms of Use, trademarks, office actions |
| **Board of Trustees**                        | Strategic and organizational oversight of the Foundation                  |

The Wikimedia Foundation Board of Trustees guides the Foundation’s work, provides oversight, and consults on long-term strategy; its trustees are selected partly by Wikimedia communities/affiliates and partly appointed for expertise. ([Meta-Wiki][2])

## 2. Wikipedia is many communities, not one single Wikipedia

There is not just “Wikipedia governance.” There is **English Wikipedia governance**, **Turkish Wikipedia governance**, **German Wikipedia governance**, and so on. Each language edition has its own local community, rules, admin processes, conventions, and dispute culture.

For example, English Wikipedia has its own policy system, administrator process, arbitration committee, deletion process, protection process, and noticeboards. Other Wikipedias may have different procedures. The Arbitration Committee page on Meta-Wiki explicitly says ArbCom rulings, policies, and procedures differ between projects depending on local and cultural contexts. ([Meta-Wiki][3])

So when people say “Wikipedia decided X,” the accurate question is often:

> Which Wikipedia? Which local community? Which policy? Which discussion? Which administrator or committee?

## 3. The core constitution: the Five Pillars and core content policies

Wikipedia governance starts with content rules. The famous summary is the **Five Pillars**, but the operational core is usually these three content policies:

1. **Neutral point of view**
2. **Verifiability**
3. **No original research**

Neutral point of view is described as a fundamental principle and one of the three core content policies; Wikipedia says these policies jointly determine what kind and quality of material is acceptable in articles. ([Wikipedia][4])

### Neutral point of view

Wikipedia articles should not argue like an essay, advertisement, activist pamphlet, company profile, or fan page. They should represent significant published views fairly and proportionately.

Important nuance: neutrality does **not** mean “give every view equal space.” A fringe view does not get the same weight as a mainstream scholarly consensus. Neutrality is about **proportionate representation according to reliable sources**.

### Verifiability

The basic rule is not “is this true?” but:

> Can a reader verify this from a reliable published source?

This is why a true but unpublished personal memory does not belong in Wikipedia. A false claim from a weak source also may not belong. A strong article is built from reliable, independent, published sources.

Wikipedia’s reliable-sources guideline says the verifiability policy requires inline citations for any material challenged or likely to be challenged and for all quotations, and that this is especially strict for biographies of living persons. ([Wikipedia][5])

### No original research

Wikipedia is not supposed to publish new ideas, new syntheses, unpublished theories, or original conclusions. The no-original-research policy says Wikipedia is built on research collected and organized from reliable sources, and if someone discovers something new, Wikipedia is not the place to announce it. ([Wikipedia][6])

This is extremely important. Wikipedia editors are not supposed to be “researchers” in the academic sense. They are more like **source-based summarizers**.

## 4. The real decision-making mechanism: consensus

Wikipedia is governed less by voting and more by **consensus**.

Consensus is Wikipedia’s fundamental decision-making method. It does not require unanimity and is not simply the result of a vote; it involves addressing legitimate concerns through compromise while following policies and guidelines. ([Wikipedia][7])

This means an argument like:

> “I have 12 votes, you have 8 votes, therefore I win”

is weaker than:

> “My version is better supported by the reliable sources, follows NPOV, avoids original research, and addresses the objections raised.”

Consensus is usually formed through:

1. **Bold editing**
   - Someone changes an article.

2. **Reversion or modification**
   - Someone else changes it, improves it, or reverts it.

3. **Talk page discussion**
   - Editors debate sources, wording, weight, article structure, neutrality.

4. **Request for Comment**
   - If stuck, a broader community discussion may be opened.

5. **Noticeboards**
   - Specialized boards handle source reliability, neutrality, biographies of living persons, fringe theories, original research, conflicts of interest, etc. ([Wikipedia][8])

6. **Administrative enforcement**
   - If editors violate conduct rules, edit-war, vandalize, harass, or ignore restrictions, admins may intervene.

7. **Arbitration**
   - For severe, chronic conduct disputes, ArbCom may impose binding remedies.

So consensus is not “everyone agrees.” It is more like:

> the current best stable outcome after policy-based argument, source evaluation, and community discussion.

## 5. Policies, guidelines, essays, and norms

Wikipedia has different levels of rules.

### Policies

Policies are high-authority standards that all editors are normally expected to follow. Wikipedia says policies have wide acceptance among editors and describe standards users should normally follow. ([Wikipedia][1])

Examples:

- Neutral point of view
- Verifiability
- No original research
- Biographies of living persons
- Deletion policy
- Protection policy
- Blocking policy
- Consensus
- Civility
- Edit warring
- Vandalism

### Guidelines

Guidelines are best practices supported by consensus. They are important, but usually more contextual than policies. Wikipedia says guidelines are sets of best practices supported by consensus, and editors should try to follow them with common sense. ([Wikipedia][1])

Examples:

- Reliable sources guideline
- Notability guidelines
- Manual of Style
- Conflict of interest guideline
- Article title guidelines

### Essays

Essays are advice or opinion from editors or groups of editors. They can be influential, but they do not necessarily represent community consensus. Wikipedia explicitly distinguishes essays from policies and guidelines. ([Wikipedia][1])

Examples of essay-like concepts include:

- “Assume good faith”
- “Don’t bite the newcomers”
- “Wikipedia is not therapy”
- “There is no deadline”

Some essays become culturally powerful even if they are not formal law.

## 6. Editors: the basic citizens of Wikipedia

Most governance begins with ordinary editors. Anyone can edit many pages, although restrictions vary by page and account status.

Editors can:

- create articles
- improve articles
- add citations
- remove unsourced claims
- revert vandalism
- discuss on talk pages
- nominate pages for deletion
- comment in deletion discussions
- request page protection
- participate in Requests for Comment
- join WikiProjects
- patrol recent changes
- review new pages
- help detect copyright violations
- participate in policy discussions

Power in Wikipedia is not supposed to come from job title. It comes from:

- demonstrated judgment
- policy knowledge
- edit history
- trust from other editors
- ability to persuade through sources and policy
- technical permissions granted by the community

## 7. Administrators: not bosses, but trusted tool-holders

Administrators are volunteer editors with extra technical tools. English Wikipedia describes administrators as volunteer editors trusted with certain tools, expected to use them fairly, and never to gain advantage in a dispute. ([Wikipedia][9])

Admins can typically:

- delete and undelete pages
- protect and unprotect pages
- block and unblock users
- edit fully protected pages
- close certain discussions
- enforce sanctions
- handle vandalism/spam
- perform maintenance tasks

But admins are **not** supposed to be editorial rulers. English Wikipedia’s RfA page says admins are not final arbiters in content disputes and do not have special powers to decide content matters, except to enforce community consensus or Arbitration Committee decisions. ([Wikipedia][10])

This is why the classic metaphor is “the mop”: admins are janitors with keys, not kings. Wikipedia itself uses this janitor/mop metaphor and says admins are not more important than other editors. ([Wikipedia][9])

## 8. How someone becomes an administrator

On English Wikipedia, there are two processes: public community discussion or secret-ballot administrator elections. In the traditional **Requests for adminship** process, candidates self-nominate or are nominated, editors ask questions and support/oppose/neutral-comment, and then a bureaucrat evaluates whether consensus exists. The page says most nominations remain active for at least seven days and that the discussion is not a simple vote. ([Wikipedia][10])

The community evaluates things like:

- long-term editing history
- temperament
- policy knowledge
- anti-vandalism work
- content work
- judgment under conflict
- civility
- trustworthiness
- ability to use tools conservatively

A person can be a brilliant content editor and still fail adminship if the community does not trust their judgment with blocking/deletion/protection tools.

## 9. Bureaucrats, functionaries, CheckUsers, Oversighters

Beyond ordinary admins, some users have narrower but more sensitive roles.

### Bureaucrats

Bureaucrats can usually close adminship discussions and assign certain user rights. They do not decide article truth; they implement community outcomes around permissions.

### CheckUsers

CheckUsers can access limited technical data to investigate abuse such as sockpuppetry. Because this touches privacy, access is tightly controlled.

### Oversighters

Oversighters can suppress material from public view and even from normal administrators in cases like severe privacy violations, nonpublic personal information, certain legal issues, or grossly inappropriate content.

### Functionaries

“Functionaries” is often used for trusted users with advanced permissions such as CheckUser or Oversight.

The key point: higher technical access means **more constrained use**, not more editorial authority.

## 10. Stewards and global permissions

Above local projects, Wikimedia has cross-wiki roles.

**Stewards** are global users with broad technical ability across public Wikimedia wikis. Meta-Wiki says stewards can modify local and global user rights, change global account status/names, and access permissions available to administrators and bureaucrats, but their use of rights is restricted by policy; they do not act when local users can handle something, except in emergencies. ([Meta-Wiki][11])

**Global sysops** are trusted cross-wiki users with maintenance and anti-vandalism tools on many public wikis, but Meta-Wiki stresses that they do not have extra editorial control over content or local communities. ([Meta-Wiki][12])

So global governance exists mostly for:

- small wikis without enough local admins
- cross-wiki vandalism
- global spam
- compromised accounts
- global bans/locks
- emergency technical intervention

## 11. Arbitration Committee: the court of last resort

Some Wikipedias have an **Arbitration Committee**, often called ArbCom. Meta-Wiki describes ArbComs as small groups of trusted users that serve as a means of dispute resolution on some Wikimedia projects. ([Meta-Wiki][3])

On English Wikipedia, ArbCom mainly handles serious **conduct** disputes, not ordinary content disagreements.

ArbCom can impose remedies such as:

- topic bans
- interaction bans
- site bans
- editing restrictions
- administrator desysopping
- discretionary sanctions / contentious topic restrictions
- appeal structures
- enforcement rules

Important distinction:

| Problem                                           | Usually handled by                |
| ------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| “Which wording is more neutral?”                  | Talk page / RfC / noticeboards    |
| “Is this source reliable?”                        | Reliable sources noticeboard      |
| “Is this article notable?”                        | Deletion discussion               |
| “This editor keeps harassing people across pages” | Admin noticeboards / ArbCom       |
| “This whole topic area is chronically chaotic”    | ArbCom / contentious topic regime |

ArbCom is not supposed to decide “the truth” of an article. It governs editor behavior when normal community processes break down.

## 12. Deletion governance: how pages are removed

Wikipedia has formal deletion processes. The deletion policy says pages that do not meet encyclopedia criteria are identified and removed through outlined processes; only administrators can perform deletion, view deleted pages, and undelete them, and those actions are logged. ([Wikipedia][13])

Main deletion paths include:

### Speedy deletion

For obvious cases:

- vandalism
- spam
- attack pages
- nonsense
- copyright violations
- recreated deleted material
- pages by banned users in some circumstances
- empty or test pages

This is fast and admin-handled.

### Proposed deletion

For uncontroversial deletion. An editor proposes deletion; if nobody objects within the period, an admin may delete it. If someone objects, the page usually must go to a fuller discussion.

### Articles for deletion

This is the deliberative process. Editors discuss whether the article should be:

- kept
- deleted
- merged
- redirected
- moved
- draftified

The closer evaluates policy-based consensus, not just vote count.

The deletion policy lists common reasons for deletion, including copyright violations, vandalism, spam, content forks, articles lacking reliable sources, failed notability, biography-of-living-persons violations, and content unsuitable for an encyclopedia. ([Wikipedia][13])

## 13. Page protection: how open editing is limited

Not all pages are equally open. Administrators can protect pages to prevent disruption.

Wikipedia’s protection policy governs when pages can be protected and unprotected. Search snippets from the official policy summarize that it covers protection types, procedures, and when protection should or should not be applied. ([Wikipedia][14])

Common reasons:

- persistent vandalism
- edit warring
- high-risk templates
- biographies under attack
- breaking news pages receiving chaotic edits
- heavily transcluded pages where mistakes affect many pages

Common protection types include:

- **semi-protection**: blocks anonymous/new users from editing
- **extended-confirmed protection**: only more established accounts can edit
- **full protection**: only admins can edit
- **move protection**: prevents page-title wars
- **pending changes**: edits by less-established users require review before appearing

Protection is supposed to prevent disruption, not to endorse one content version as “true.”

## 14. Blocking and banning: user-level enforcement

Wikipedia has several ways to restrict users.

### Blocks

A block is a technical restriction. It may prevent a user or IP from editing. Blocks are usually used for:

- vandalism
- harassment
- edit warring
- sockpuppetry
- spam
- legal threats
- disruption
- repeated policy violations

### Topic bans

A user may be allowed to edit Wikipedia generally but forbidden from editing a specific topic area.

Example:

> You may edit articles about mathematics, but not articles related to Israel-Palestine.

### Interaction bans

Two users may be forbidden from interacting.

### Site bans

A user is prohibited from editing the site.

### Global bans / global locks

For cross-wiki abuse, restrictions may apply across Wikimedia projects.

The governance logic is:

> edit the encyclopedia collaboratively, or lose access to the collaborative space.

## 15. Talk pages: the deliberative layer

Every article has a talk page. Talk pages are where editors discuss:

- source reliability
- wording
- neutrality
- article structure
- whether material is undue
- whether a claim is original research
- whether a biography claim is too weakly sourced
- whether to merge/split/rename an article

Talk pages are not forums for general debate. You are not supposed to argue “what I think about Napoleon.” You are supposed to argue:

> What should the article say, based on reliable sources and policy?

That is the key difference between Wikipedia and Reddit.

## 16. Noticeboards: specialized governance courts

Wikipedia has many noticeboards, each handling a class of dispute.

Examples:

| Noticeboard                               | Purpose                                         |
| ----------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Reliable sources noticeboard              | Is this source usable?                          |
| Neutral point of view noticeboard         | Is this article biased?                         |
| No original research noticeboard          | Is this synthesis/original analysis?            |
| Biographies of living persons noticeboard | Is this living-person content policy-compliant? |
| Conflict of interest noticeboard          | Is someone editing with improper self-interest? |
| Administrators’ noticeboard/incidents     | Does this require admin intervention?           |
| Edit warring noticeboard                  | Did someone violate revert rules?               |
| Arbitration enforcement                   | Is an ArbCom restriction being violated?        |

The official dispute-resolution noticeboard index lists boards for neutrality, source reliability, original research/synthesis, biographies of living persons, fringe theories, conflicts of interest, and external links. ([Wikipedia][8])

This is one of Wikipedia’s hidden strengths: governance is distributed into many specialized forums.

## 17. Requests for Comment: broad community input

When local talk page discussion fails, editors can open a **Request for Comment**. This invites wider community input.

RfCs are useful when:

- a small group is deadlocked
- a question affects many articles
- a policy interpretation is uncertain
- a controversial wording needs broader consensus
- a recurring dispute needs a stable answer

Again, an RfC is not a raw vote. Closers evaluate strength of arguments against policy and evidence.

## 18. WikiProjects: soft governance through expertise and maintenance

WikiProjects are groups of editors interested in a topic area, such as medicine, military history, philosophy, mathematics, Turkey, video games, or biographies.

They usually do not have binding authority, but they influence:

- article quality standards
- templates
- style conventions
- notability interpretations
- maintenance backlogs
- review practices
- article assessment
- collaborative editing drives

Think of WikiProjects as **guilds**, not governments. They coordinate labor and norms.

## 19. Bots and automated governance

Bots are automated accounts approved to perform repetitive tasks.

They can:

- revert obvious vandalism
- fix formatting
- tag pages
- update templates
- detect dead links
- archive discussions
- notify users
- maintain categories
- detect copyright problems
- revert known spam patterns

Bots are governance infrastructure because they make rule enforcement scalable. But bots usually need approval, are monitored, and can be stopped if they malfunction.

## 20. Recent changes patrol and new page patrol

Wikipedia survives openness through patrol mechanisms.

### Recent changes patrol

Editors monitor new edits in real time to catch:

- vandalism
- nonsense
- spam
- unsourced controversial claims
- test edits
- malicious edits

### New page patrol

New articles are reviewed for:

- notability
- sourcing
- copyright issues
- promotional tone
- attack content
- duplication
- suitability as an encyclopedia article

This is why many bad edits disappear quickly, while subtle biased edits can survive longer.

## 21. Biographies of living persons: stricter governance

Living people get special protection. The logic is:

> Bad Wikipedia content about a living person can cause real-world harm.

So claims about living people require especially careful sourcing. Unsourced or poorly sourced contentious material can be removed immediately. This applies not only to biography articles but also to mentions of living people elsewhere.

This is one of the strongest examples of Wikipedia governance being risk-sensitive.

## 22. Notability: why not everything gets an article

Wikipedia is not a database of everything. A topic usually needs significant coverage in reliable, independent sources.

Notability is not the same as importance. A person or company can be important to themselves, customers, employees, or fans, but still not meet Wikipedia’s notability standard.

Typical notability questions:

- Are there independent reliable sources?
- Is the coverage significant, not trivial?
- Is the topic covered beyond press releases and self-published material?
- Is there enough source material to write a neutral article?
- Does a specialized notability guideline apply?

This is why many startup, artist, musician, software, and local business pages get deleted: they are often promotional or sourced mainly to themselves.

## 23. Conflict of interest and paid editing

Wikipedia strongly discourages editing where you have a conflict of interest, especially editing about:

- yourself
- your company
- your employer
- your clients
- your political group
- your product
- your competitors

Paid editing has disclosure rules under the Wikimedia Terms of Use. The broader idea is: Wikipedia should not become reputation management or marketing.

COI editors are usually expected to suggest changes on talk pages rather than directly edit sensitive article content.

## 24. Wikimedia Foundation policies: the legal/global layer

The Wikimedia Foundation maintains official policies that apply across projects. Its governance wiki lists policies covering privacy, Terms of Use, licensing, trademarks, DMCA, data retention, office actions, Universal Code of Conduct, IP information tools, human rights, and more. ([Wikimedia Foundation][15])

This is a different layer from English Wikipedia community policy.

Community policy decides:

> Should this source be used in this article?

Foundation policy decides:

> What are the legal terms, privacy obligations, safety rules, and organizational responsibilities of the whole platform?

## 25. Terms of Use

The Terms of Use are the legal agreement for using Wikimedia projects. They interact with community rules but are not the same thing. The Terms of Use summary says users must follow the Terms, the Universal Code of Conduct, and applicable community policies when using the websites/projects. ([Wikimedia Foundation][16])

The Terms of Use matter for:

- paid editing disclosure
- harassment
- misuse of services
- legal compliance
- user contributions and licensing
- privacy/security constraints
- global behavior expectations

## 26. Universal Code of Conduct

The **Universal Code of Conduct** is a global baseline for acceptable behavior across Wikimedia projects. It was created to address harassment and abuse across the movement, especially where local governance was uneven. The Wikimedia policy list shows the Universal Code of Conduct was adopted in December 2020 and its enforcement guidelines in March 2023. ([Wikimedia Foundation][15])

This is one of the more “centralized” layers compared with older Wikipedia culture, because it creates a movement-wide behavioral baseline. But enforcement still interacts with local communities and processes.

## 27. Office actions: when the Foundation overrides local process

Normally, local community governance is primary. But the Foundation can take **office actions**.

The office actions policy says the Foundation may delete, protect, blank a page, or take actions against individuals without normal community processes in certain cases; these may be temporary or permanent and should not be undone by users. ([Wikimedia Foundation][17])

Reasons can include:

- privacy violations
- child protection
- copyright infringement
- systematic harassment
- legal demands
- court orders
- threats to safety
- severe public or community harm

The policy explicitly says local policies remain primary, but rare cases may require the Foundation to override local policy, such as valid court orders or protecting community/public safety. ([Wikimedia Foundation][17])

So Foundation intervention is the emergency/legal override layer.

## 28. Licensing: why content can be reused

Wikipedia content is freely licensed, mostly under Creative Commons Attribution-ShareAlike. This means people can reuse and adapt content if they follow attribution and share-alike rules. This licensing structure is part of governance because it determines what contributors agree to when they publish.

It also means Wikipedia cannot casually copy copyrighted text from elsewhere. Copyright enforcement is a major governance function.

## 29. The Board, Foundation staff, affiliates, and movement governance

The Foundation is governed by its Board of Trustees. The Board sets high-level organizational direction and oversees the Foundation’s work. ([Meta-Wiki][2])

But the Wikimedia movement also includes:

- local chapters
- thematic organizations
- user groups
- affiliates
- volunteer communities
- technical contributors
- grant-funded initiatives
- events like Wikimania
- movement strategy processes

Affiliates can organize outreach, edit-a-thons, partnerships, education programs, GLAM work, and local community activity. But affiliates do not “own” article content.

This creates a weird but powerful separation:

| Entity                      | Has authority over article text?                  |
| --------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| Wikimedia Foundation        | Usually no                                        |
| Board of Trustees           | Usually no                                        |
| Wikimedia chapters          | No                                                |
| Donors                      | No                                                |
| Editors/community consensus | Yes, within policy                                |
| Courts/legal orders         | Sometimes indirectly, via Foundation legal action |

## 30. Money and governance

Wikipedia is funded mainly by donations through the Wikimedia Foundation. Donors do not buy editorial control. The Foundation funds servers, software, staff, legal, safety, grants, research, fundraising, communications, and movement support.

This separation is important: the fundraising organization supports the infrastructure, but the encyclopedia’s content is governed mostly by volunteer editorial communities.

## 31. Software governance: MediaWiki, Phabricator, technical decisions

Wikipedia runs on MediaWiki. Technical governance happens through:

- Wikimedia Foundation product/engineering teams
- volunteer developers
- MediaWiki maintainers
- Phabricator tasks
- technical RFCs
- interface administrators
- gadget/script maintainers
- community wishlists
- deployment processes

Technical changes can affect governance. For example:

- page protection tools shape who can edit
- watchlists enable patrol
- revision history enables accountability
- abuse filters prevent spam/vandalism
- bots automate maintenance
- edit filters enforce local rules
- user rights define power boundaries

So Wikipedia governance is partly social, partly legal, partly software architecture.

## 32. Revision history and public accountability

Every article has a public edit history. This is central.

It means people can see:

- who changed what
- when it changed
- edit summaries
- reversions
- talk page arguments
- protection logs
- deletion logs
- block logs
- move logs

This makes governance transparent compared with most platforms. Administrative actions are logged; the RfA page notes admin actions are publicly logged and can be reverted by other administrators. ([Wikipedia][10])

The public history is one reason Wikipedia can allow open editing: bad edits are not invisible.

## 33. Vandalism handling

Vandalism is handled through a stack:

1. recent changes patrol
2. watchlists
3. automated filters
4. anti-vandalism bots
5. rollbackers
6. warnings
7. blocks
8. page protection
9. range blocks
10. global locks for cross-wiki abuse

Most obvious vandalism is reverted quickly. Harder problems are subtle POV-pushing, source manipulation, COI editing, and long-term agenda editing.

## 34. Contentious topics

Some topics are chronically difficult:

- wars
- nationalism
- religion
- ethnic conflict
- biographies of politicians
- gender/sexuality disputes
- pseudoscience
- fringe medicine
- current politics
- active legal disputes

In these areas, Wikipedia may use special restrictions:

- extended-confirmed protection
- discretionary sanctions
- topic bans
- one-revert rules
- stricter sourcing rules
- ArbCom-authorized enforcement
- page-level sanctions

The idea is that open editing works well in many domains, but highly contested domains need extra friction.

## 35. The practical lifecycle of an article

A typical article evolves like this:

1. Someone creates a stub.
2. Others add sources and sections.
3. Patrol checks whether it is spam, notable, or copyright-infringing.
4. If weak, it may be tagged, draftified, merged, or nominated for deletion.
5. If it survives, editors improve citations, structure, categories, infoboxes.
6. Disputes move to the talk page.
7. If quality rises, it may become Good Article or Featured Article.
8. If the topic becomes controversial, it may receive protection or sanctions.
9. Over years, the article becomes a layered sediment of many editors’ decisions.

Wikipedia is therefore not “written” once. It is **continuously negotiated**.

## 36. Quality assessment: Good Articles and Featured Articles

High-quality articles can go through review processes.

### Good Article

A single or small group of reviewers checks whether the article meets criteria for broad quality.

### Featured Article

A more rigorous community review determines whether the article is among Wikipedia’s best work.

Featured articles are judged on prose, comprehensiveness, sourcing, neutrality, stability, structure, media, and style.

This is a voluntary quality layer, not a requirement for publication.

## 37. Why Wikipedia sometimes works surprisingly well

Wikipedia’s governance has several stabilizers:

- edits are reversible
- history is public
- sources are required
- consensus is policy-bound
- experienced editors patrol changes
- admins can block disruption
- contentious pages can be protected
- bots handle repetitive abuse
- disputes have escalation paths
- bad actors can be banned
- no one “owns” an article
- policies are living documents updated by consensus

Wikipedia’s policies and guidelines are described as living documents that reflect actual experienced-editor practice and are updated when consensus changes. ([Wikipedia][1])

## 38. Why Wikipedia still fails sometimes

Its governance also has weaknesses:

- editor demographics are not representative of humanity
- some topics attract organized advocacy
- subtle bias is harder to catch than vandalism
- reliable-source rules can reproduce media/systemic bias
- obscure topics may be neglected
- expert participation varies
- talk page debates can become exhausting
- deletion/notability standards can be harsh to underdocumented communities
- administrator and editor communities can develop internal cultures hard for newcomers
- policies are complex and intimidating
- consensus can favor whoever has more time, stamina, and policy fluency

So Wikipedia is not “pure wisdom of crowds.” It is more like:

> an open-source bureaucracy for producing encyclopedia articles from published sources.

## 39. The most important governance principle

The most important thing to understand is this:

> Wikipedia does not govern truth directly. It governs **what can be included as encyclopedic content based on reliable published sources, neutrality, verifiability, no original research, and community consensus.**

That is why Wikipedia can say something like:

- “This claim is true but cannot be included yet.”
- “This claim is widely believed but needs better sourcing.”
- “This source exists but is unreliable.”
- “This viewpoint exists but is fringe and should not dominate.”
- “This person is notable, but this detail about them is not appropriate.”
- “This article should be deleted, not because the subject is fake, but because independent coverage is insufficient.”

## 40. Compressed mental model

Imagine Wikipedia as a city:

| Wikipedia thing      | City analogy                                    |
| -------------------- | ----------------------------------------------- |
| Editors              | Citizens/builders                               |
| Articles             | Public buildings                                |
| Sources              | Building materials                              |
| Policies             | Constitution/laws                               |
| Guidelines           | Building codes                                  |
| Talk pages           | Town halls                                      |
| Consensus            | Civic decision-making                           |
| Admins               | Police/janitors/permit officers                 |
| ArbCom               | Supreme conduct court                           |
| Bots                 | Street-cleaning machines                        |
| Foundation           | Landowner/infrastructure/legal authority        |
| Board                | Nonprofit board overseeing infrastructure owner |
| Office actions       | Emergency/legal override                        |
| Revision history     | CCTV/public records                             |
| Deletion discussions | Zoning/removal hearings                         |
| Page protection      | Temporary barricades                            |

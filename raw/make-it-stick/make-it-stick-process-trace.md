## Running notes
- The book itself practices spaced repetition and interleaving — key ideas recur across chapters in different contexts. This means later chapters will deepen rather than introduce entirely new concepts.
- Arose from a 10-year James S. McDonnell Foundation research grant (2002) "Applying Cognitive Psychology to Enhance Educational Practice" — Roediger as PI. Strong empirical grounding.
- Heavy overlap expected with [[learning-how-to-learn]] on retrieval practice, spaced repetition, interleaving, and illusions of competence — but this source goes deeper into the research evidence.
- Ch. 2 confirmed: the testing effect research is extensive (1917-present) and the evidence is overwhelming. The chapter effectively splits "recall-as-learning" (the strategy) from "testing-effect" (the empirical phenomenon). The generation effect and feedback timing are both instances of desirable difficulty — the unifying principle is holding strong.
- First application note created (low-stakes-classroom-quizzing). Sobel's story is a useful cautionary tale: implementation matters as much as the underlying principle. Pop quizzes vs. scheduled quizzes produce opposite student reactions despite leveraging the same mechanism.
- Ch. 3 introduces the "momentary strength vs. habit strength" distinction — this is the most precise framing yet for *why* desirable difficulties work and why massed practice feels effective. Should inform how we frame all future discussion of practice strategies.
- The practice strategy triad (spacing, interleaving, variation) is now well-established. Each addresses a different dimension: consolidation, discrimination, and flexibility. They are complementary, not redundant.
- Doug Larsen's medical education work is a bridge between research and pedagogy — tracks closely with the instructional design thread. His "practice like you play" insight (the most effective retrieval mirrors what you'll do with the knowledge) may be an important concept in its own right.
- The painting attribution finding (learners persist in preferring massed practice even after experiencing evidence against it) is a powerful extension of the illusion-of-competence concept — the illusion operates at the practice-strategy level, not just individual study sessions.
- Ch. 4 provides the theoretical scaffolding that earlier chapters lacked: the three-step model (encoding → consolidation → reconsolidation) explains *how* every desirable difficulty works. This is the most important chapter so far for understanding mechanism rather than just evidence.
- Two questions from Ch. 1 are now answered: the generation effect relates to desirable difficulty as a specific instance (struggling before instruction primes learning), and Bjork defines the desirable/undesirable boundary precisely (must be overcomeable through effort and must strengthen relevant skills).
- Retrieval cues emerge as a major theme: it's not enough to store knowledge — you need the routes to find it. This reframes forgetting as "lost keys, not lost house." Connects well to varied-practice rationale (more contexts = more cues).
- The errorless-learning debunking (Skinner) connects fear-of-failure psychology to the mechanics of working memory. This is a practical bridge: teaching about desirable difficulty is itself an intervention that improves learning by reducing anxiety.
- Bonnie Blodgett's story is the longest narrative in the book so far (4+ pages) — emphasizing generative learning as a sustained practice, not just a test-taking strategy.
- Ch. 5 completes the argument arc of the first half: Ch. 1-4 established *what* works and *why*; Ch. 5 explains *why we resist what works and prefer what doesn't.* The answer is poor metacognition — our subjective sense of what we know is systematically wrong.
- The cockpit instrument metaphor is the chapter's organizing image and connects powerfully to calibration as a concept: just as pilots had to learn to trust instruments over vestibular sensation, learners must develop the habit of checking objective measures over the feeling of knowing.
- The Dunning-Kruger finding that domain-specific training (not mere exposure to superior work) improves metacognition is crucial: you can't teach self-assessment as a generic skill - you improve metacognition by building competence in the relevant domain.
- Student-directed learning is explicitly critiqued: the same metacognitive failures that make students choose poor strategies (rereading, cramming, dropping flashcards too soon) make them poor judges of what and how to study. Instructor guidance + structured calibration tools are necessary.
- The "practice like you play" principle, introduced via Doug Larsen in Ch. 3, gets its most vivid illustration here with the police gun-handback story and Catherine Johnson's simulation accounts. This principle connects calibration to mental models: your training builds the models you'll deploy in the field.
- Ch. 6 rejects learning-styles matching without flattening all learner differences. The replacement is more precise: what matters are capabilities like reading fluency, [[structure-building]], and [[rule-learning]], plus whether assessment is treated developmentally through [[dynamic-testing]].
- Bruce Hendry's "winding stair" is the chapter's central image for expertise: a person keeps climbing by extracting rules from experience and fitting them into a larger structure, not by discovering an innate style.
- Sternberg's dynamic testing extends the calibration thread from Ch. 5: tests are not just instruments for judging present competence, but tools for deciding the next round of focused effort.
- Ch. 7 grounds the book's optimistic message in mechanism: ability is malleable because the brain remains [[neuroplasticity|plastic]], but improvements are largely local to the circuits being trained. This gives us a principled way to support growth without buying into generalized brain-training hype.
- [[growth-mindset]] and [[dynamic-testing]] now form a strong pair in the wiki: one is the learner's interpretation of difficulty, the other is the instructional stance toward performance. Both push against static labels and toward developmental feedback.
- The chapter sharpens a useful distinction for future notes: mnemonic systems are retrieval organizers for already-mastered material, not substitutes for understanding. This should shape how we talk about memorization tools across the repo.
- Ch. 8 is prescriptive rather than theoretical — it consolidates all seven prior chapters into actionable tips for students, lifelong learners, teachers, and trainers. The most substantive new contribution is *high-structure teaching* (Wenderoth): embedding desirable difficulties into every class meeting, teaching learning science explicitly, and using Bloom's taxonomy for calibration. The Thayer method at West Point is a 200-year-old system that independently arrived at the same principles. The chapter's core tension: the principles are universal, but people need scaffolding to persist because effective techniques feel less productive than the ones they naturally prefer.

## Running notes
- The preface ("A Note to Our Teacher-Readers") introduces the authors' backgrounds and frames the book as bridging neuroscience, psychology, and teaching practice. Beth Rogowsky is the third co-author alongside Oakley and Sejnowski. The preface mentions declarative and procedural pathways as two distinct neural routes to learning — a key structural distinction for the book.
- Heavy overlap with existing wiki material from [[learning-how-to-learn]] and [[make-it-stick]] expected, since two of the three authors created the MOOC and the book covers similar neuroscience-of-learning territory. The new contribution is the teacher-facing, classroom-practical framing.
- Reingesting chapter 1 — 2026-04-15.
- Ch. 2 introduces the race-car/hiker framework that will likely recur throughout the book. The declarative/procedural pathway distinction previewed in Ch. 1 may map onto race-car (declarative) and hiker (procedural) approaches — to be confirmed in Ch. 3 and Ch. 6.
- Expertise reversal effect connects back to John Sweller's cognitive load theory (Ch. 1) — same researcher.
- Ch. 2 explicitly confirms the WM-creativity link from [[learning-how-to-learn]]: tiredness/lessened WM heightens creative insight.
- Friedrich Hayek mentioned as an example of a hiker thinker — his breakthroughs came from slow, muddling engagement with material. Not given a full entity note as he is tangential to the learning-science focus.
- Ch. 3 introduces the declarative learning system (WM → hippocampus → neocortex) as the first of two learning pathways. The procedural pathway (basal ganglia) is previewed but deferred to Ch. 6.
- The choir parable (conductor/Hip/Neo) is the book's central teaching metaphor for how information moves into long-term memory. Hip's dual-direction constraint (can't learn and rehearse simultaneously) is the key neuroscience rationale for brain breaks.
- Freeman meta-analysis caveat: active learning time in the studied classrooms ranged from 10-100% — the benefit may come from breaks with retrieval, not from maximizing active time.
- Natalie Wexler (The Knowledge Gap) cited for the argument that comprehension skills without content knowledge are hollow — connects to the "knowledge party" idea.
- Jennifer Gonzalez (Cult of Pedagogy) quoted on "Grecian urn" activities — flashy but educationally empty.
- Erin Wamsley quoted on seconds-long rest breaks triggering memory-related neural activity — consolidation during wakeful rest is underappreciated.
- Pooja Agarwal & Patrice Bain (Powerful Teaching) cited again for the emphasis on getting information OUT of students' heads rather than IN.
- Ch. 4 connects procrastination directly to the focused/diffuse framework introduced in [[learning-how-to-learn]] and deepened in this chapter. The insular cortex mechanism grounds what was previously described as "pain centers" with specific neuroanatomy.
- Motivation is teased but deferred to Ch. 7 — the "commonsense" approach of making learning fun can backfire. This may connect to the extrinsic/intrinsic motivation distinction.
- Race-car procrastination vulnerability at school transitions (middle→high, high→college) is a practical insight for teachers that connects to the scaffolded-instruction work from Ch. 2.
- The rubric discussion notes that rubrics primarily assess declarative knowledge — Ch. 6 (procedural pathway) will address the limitation that mouthing correct words doesn't mean understanding.
- Ch. 5 establishes the evolutionary framework: primary vs. secondary knowledge (Geary) and neuronal recycling (Dehaene) explain *why* direct instruction matters for hard subjects. This is the theoretical spine for the practical advice in the rest of the book.
- The direct instruction ↔ learn-it/link-it mapping is now explicit: I do + we do = learn it; you do + extend it = link it. Teacher-directed and student-directed are phases, not competing philosophies.
- David Geary, Stanislas Dehaene, and Barak Rosenshine are created as entity notes. Currently single-source but their frameworks are central enough to warrant standalone pages; they will likely accumulate links from later chapters.
- Ch. 6 fully develops the procedural pathway via basal ganglia. Michael Ullman's declarative-procedural theory confirms both systems can learn the same material. The seesaw metaphor and front-door/back-door architecture are introduced. Interleaving and spaced repetition emerge as the best procedural-learning strategies. Schemas are introduced as the integrative structure for both systems. The verbal-explanation-vs-understanding insight has significant implications for assessment equity. Jim Knight's coaching quote supports the procedural value of goal-directed practice.
- The dyslexia sidebar and the ADHD/WM-capacity note connect Ch. 6 to the race-car/hiker framework from Ch. 2 — race-car → declarative, hiker → procedural is now explicit.
- Handwriting vs. typing (Audrey van der Meer) is a brief but important application note not given its own page; the evidence is cited within the chapter's broader argument about sensorimotor engagement.
- Ch. 7 preview: habits and motivation will be the next layer — the "commonsense" approach of making learning fun may backfire.
- Ch. 7 confirms the preview: the dopamine mechanism explains WHY unexpected rewards enhance learning (synaptic strengthening) and why expected rewards only drive motivation (no new dopamine). The "commonsense" backfire is now explained: constant positivity becomes expected, eliminating the dopamine learning-boost while providing only motivation.
- Richard Felder quoted on impostor syndrome — engineering professor's perspective on how common it is even among top students. Not given an entity note (single mention, tangential).
- Szu-Han Wang and Richard Morris cited for the schema-motivation link ("what interests us takes time to develop") — a powerful counterargument to "make learning fun first" approaches.
- Ch. 8 preview: collaborative learning — the next chapter will address how teams and group structures leverage the neuroscience covered so far.
- Ch. 8 confirms the dopamine-bullying connection: each unexpected opportunity to bully sends a dopamine rush to the bully, reinforcing the behavior. Without negative consequences, the loop never extinguishes.
- The empathy-boundaries pairing is an important practical insight: excessive empathy without wisdom can enable manipulation and codependency. This is relevant to SEL curriculum design.
- Suparna Rajaram's research on group size vs. creativity is a clean empirical finding: larger groups → fewer ideas per person, less receptive to outside ideas. Brainwriting is the proposed solution.
- Ch. 9 preview: online teaching — how to translate the neuroscience framework to online and flipped classroom settings.
- Ch. 9 is the most applied chapter so far — heavy on practical tips, lighter on new neuroscience. The theoretical contributions are multimedia learning theory (Mayer), the top-down/bottom-up attention distinction, and instructor presence as an empirically validated engagement driver. The learn-it/link-it framework extended to hybrid course design is the structural contribution.
- Doug Lemov referenced again (cold-calling online); Rhonda Bondie (Harvard) mentioned for breakout group note-taking strategy; Aaron Johnson quoted on instructor presence and absenteeism.
- Ch. 10 preview: lesson plans — the final instructional chapter will address course design and planning.

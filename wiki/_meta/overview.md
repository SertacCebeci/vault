# Knowledge Map

High-level view of the wiki's emerging structure and key threads.

## Domains

### Learning Theory & Neuroscience
The foundation of the wiki. From [[learning-how-to-learn]], we have a comprehensive framework covering how the brain learns at the neural level ([[neuromodulators]], synaptic plasticity, [[long-term-memory]] consolidation, [[sleep-and-learning]]) and how to leverage that understanding for practical study.

**Core framework:** The brain operates in two modes — [[focused-and-diffuse-modes]] — and learning requires alternating between them. [[chunking]] is the fundamental unit of expertise, compressing information into reusable patterns that fit within [[working-memory]]'s ~4 slots. [[mental-models]] are the higher-order structures built from interconnected chunks — they enable expert performance under pressure.

**The three-step learning model** (from [[make-it-stick]] Ch. 4): Learning occurs through [[encoding]] (perceptions → memory traces in short-term memory), [[consolidation]] (brain reorganizes and stabilizes traces into [[long-term-memory]]), and retrieval (recalling from long-term storage triggers [[reconsolidation]], making learning pliable again for updating and strengthening). This model explains *why* desirable difficulties work at each stage.

**[[neuroplasticity]] as the bridge between mechanism and hope** (from [[make-it-stick]] Ch. 7): The brain remains modifiable well past childhood. Experience can strengthen or refine neural pathways, increase myelination along heavily used circuits, and gradually recode repeated action sequences into faster, more automatic patterns. This matters because it grounds the wiki's practical advice in a developmental claim: ability is not merely revealed by practice, it is partly built by practice. At the same time, plasticity is not magical generality. Changes tend to be specific to the circuits being exercised, which is why [[deliberate-practice]] produces domain-specific expertise and why generic "brain training" claims remain weak.

**The desirable difficulty principle** (coined by Elizabeth and Robert Bjork): Learning is deeper and more durable when it requires effort. Easy learning creates [[illusions-of-competence]]; [[desirable-difficulty]] shatters them. Difficulties are desirable because "they trigger encoding and retrieval processes that support learning, comprehension, and remembering" — but they become *undesirable* if the learner lacks the background knowledge to respond. The key inverse relationship: the easier knowledge is to retrieve, the less benefit from retrieval practice; the harder the retrieval, the stronger the learning. Scientists distinguish **momentary strength** (visible acquisition gains) from **underlying habit strength** (durable retention) — the techniques that build habit strength actively impede momentary strength, which is why [[massed-practice]] *feels* superior despite producing inferior long-term results.

**The retrieval practice ecosystem:** The [[testing-effect]] is the empirical backbone — retrieval strengthens memory more than additional study, with a research lineage from 1917 to modern classroom experiments. Around it orbit several related mechanisms: the [[forgetting-curve]] explains *why* retrieval is needed (without it, ~70% is lost quickly), the [[generation-effect]] shows that even minimal active production strengthens memory (and even *wrong* answers help, provided corrective feedback follows), [[feedback-timing]] reveals that delayed correction outperforms immediate, and [[reflection-as-retrieval]] shows that nightly reflection on experience is a naturally occurring form of retrieval practice. [[retrieval-cues]] are the overlooked second task of learning — not just storing knowledge but building the routes to find it; forgetting is usually lost cues, not lost knowledge. Practical applications: [[low-stakes-classroom-quizzing]] and [[write-to-learn]].

**The practice strategy triad** (from [[make-it-stick]] Ch. 3): Three alternatives to [[massed-practice]], each contributing something distinct:
- **[[spaced-repetition|Spacing]]** — allows consolidation; the effort of retrieval after some forgetting retriggerers consolidation (surgical residents study)
- **[[interleaving]]** — develops [[discrimination-learning|discrimination skills]] — the ability to classify problems and select the right solution (geometric solids: 215% improvement; painting attribution; bird classification)
- **[[varied-practice|Variation]]** — builds flexible schemas that transfer to novel conditions (beanbag toss study: never-practiced-target group outperformed target-only)

These are implemented practically through tools like the [[leitner-box]] (spaced flashcard system) and in domains from medical education to elite athletics.

**Failure and error** (from [[make-it-stick]] Ch. 4): The [[errorless-learning-myth]] (Skinner's 1950s-60s approach) has been thoroughly debunked - errors with corrective feedback produce stronger learning than error-free instruction. Fear of failure is itself harmful, consuming [[working-memory]] capacity for self-monitoring rather than problem-solving. Teaching learners that errors are natural (not signs of failure) is an intervention that directly improves performance. [[priming-for-learning]] - struggling with a problem before being shown the solution - is a specific application: the struggle creates fertile ground for encoding, even when the attempt fails.

**Learning differences that matter** (from [[make-it-stick]] Ch. 6): The book rejects the [[learning-styles-myth]] while taking other differences seriously. What matters is not whether a learner prefers visual versus verbal presentation, but whether the learner can access the material, extract its main structure ([[structure-building]]), abstract governing principles across cases ([[rule-learning]]), and use performance data to guide growth ([[dynamic-testing]]). This extends [[mental-models]] from a description of expertise into an account of how expertise is gradually assembled.

**[[growth-mindset]] as interpretation of difficulty** (from [[make-it-stick]] Ch. 7): Carol Dweck's work adds a motivational layer to the repo's model of learning. A learner's beliefs about ability shape whether difficulty is read as a threat to identity or as evidence that growth is happening. This turns out to matter because many of the repo's best-supported techniques feel worse than the ineffective ones. [[growth-mindset]] therefore connects directly to [[desirable-difficulty]], [[dynamic-testing]], and [[calibration]]: it is the stance that lets a person use hard feedback and repeated failure without collapsing into helplessness.

### Study Techniques (Evidence-Based)
A rich set of interconnected techniques grounded in research:
- **What works:** [[recall-as-learning]], [[spaced-repetition]], [[interleaving]], [[varied-practice]], [[deliberate-practice]], [[elaboration]], [[memory-techniques]], [[metaphor-and-analogy]], [[pomodoro-technique]], [[reflection-as-retrieval]], [[priming-for-learning]]
- **What doesn't:** Re-reading, highlighting, cramming, [[massed-practice]], practicing only easy material → [[illusions-of-competence]], [[overlearning]], [[errorless-learning-myth|errorless learning]]
- **Why it works:** [[desirable-difficulty]] — the effort required by effective strategies is the mechanism, not a side effect; the three-step model ([[encoding]] → [[consolidation]] → [[reconsolidation]]) explains the mechanism at each stage

**Technique distinctions sharpened by Ch. 7:** [[deliberate-practice]] is not just "do hard things"; it is feedback-rich, weakness-targeted, and specific to a domain. [[memory-techniques]] are not substitutes for understanding; they are retrieval scaffolds that keep already-mastered material usable under pressure. This chapter therefore improves the wiki's ability to separate durable study advice from plausible-sounding but weakly supported shortcuts.

### Self-Regulation & Metacognition
[[procrastination]] as a habit loop (cue → routine → reward → belief), defeated through [[process-vs-product]] thinking and the [[pomodoro-technique]]. [[einstellung]] as a cognitive bias that blocks creative problem-solving.

**Metacognition as the master skill** (from [[make-it-stick]] Ch. 5): [[metacognition]] — the ability to accurately monitor your own thinking — is the root skill governing all self-directed learning. Without it, you cannot allocate effort wisely, choose effective strategies, or recognize when you've gone astray. The chapter reveals a comprehensive taxonomy of why metacognition fails:

- **[[system-1-and-system-2]]** (Daniel Kahneman): System 1 (automatic/intuitive) provides fast but unreliable self-assessments; System 2 (controlled/analytical) can check them but must be deliberately engaged — like a pilot scanning instruments rather than trusting vestibular sensation.
- **[[illusions-of-competence]]** (deepened): fluency illusions, feeling-of-knowing, "the big lie" technique, flashbulb memory overconfidence — a full taxonomy of how familiarity masquerades as knowledge.
- **[[memory-distortion]]**: memory is reconstruction, shaped by [[narrative-bias]], suggestion (Loftus car crash study), imagination inflation, interference (Donald Thomson case), and social influence (memory conformity).
- **[[dunning-kruger-effect]]** ([[david-dunning]]): incompetent people overestimate competence because they lack the skill to recognize their deficiency — but domain-specific training fixes it.
- **[[curse-of-knowledge]]** ([[eric-mazur]]): experts forget what it's like not to know; the knuckle-tapping experiment shows a 20x overestimation of communication success.
- **[[calibration]]**: the prescriptive toolkit — testing as primary instrument, [[peer-instruction]] (Mazur), cumulative quizzing, corrective feedback, apprenticeship, teams, [[simulation-based-training]], and the cardinal rule: practice like you play ([[police-gun-handback]] as cautionary tale).

### Pedagogy & Instructional Design
Emerging from [[make-it-stick]]: The book's final chapter (Ch. 8) translates learning science into institutional practice. Three models stand out:

- **[[high-structure-teaching]]** (Mary Pat Wenderoth, University of Washington): Daily and weekly low-stakes exercises, transparent teaching of learning science, Bloom's taxonomy calibration, and growth-mindset framing. Empirically reduces failure rates and closes achievement gaps compared to lecture-only "low-structure" classes. The key finding: even minimal grade consequences for practice exercises improve outcomes compared to ungraded exercises.

- **[[thayer-method]]** (West Point, ~200 years old): Clear learning objectives, pre-class generation, daily quizzing, and board-based collaborative recitation. Works across wide ability ranges (from elite cadets to high school students at Riverside Military Academy). Mike Matthews's metaphor of "shooting an azimuth" — using quizzes to check whether you're on course — is a vivid operational definition of [[calibration]].

- **[[low-stakes-classroom-quizzing]]** (expanded): McDermott's implementation at Washington University (four-item daily quizzes, cumulative scope, 20% of grade) and the Columbia IL expansion into high school history and science. Both illustrate how retrieval practice can be embedded institutionally.

Earlier chapters laid the groundwork: Douglas Larsen's medical education work (Ch. 3), Andrew Sobel's quiz evolution (Ch. 2), and [[eric-mazur]]'s [[peer-instruction]] (Ch. 5). The key insight across all of them: the most effective retrieval practice mirrors what you'll do with the knowledge later — "practice like you play."

### Inclusive Teaching & Differentiation
From [[uncommon-sense-teaching]] Ch. 2: The [[race-car-and-hiker-brains]] framework reframes [[working-memory]] capacity differences as qualitative variations in learning experience. Students range from ~3 to 6+ items of WM capacity, and this variation — not effort or attention — explains much of the observed classroom diversity. The pedagogical response is [[scaffolded-instruction]]: differentiated support for both ends of the spectrum. The [[expertise-reversal-effect]] governs when to add and remove scaffolding. The deeper insight is [[long-term-memory-boosts-working-memory]]: education's transformative power is not in changing WM capacity (which is fixed) but in building LTM through [[chunking]], [[recall-as-learning]], and practice, which effectively extends apparent WM in each domain. Cajal's story ([[cajal-hiker-learner]]) illustrates that hiker-type processing can produce deeper, more flexible understanding than race-car speed.

### Active Learning & Brain Architecture
From [[uncommon-sense-teaching]] Ch. 3: The [[declarative-learning-system]] reveals the three-part neural architecture underlying conscious learning — [[working-memory]] (conductor), the [[hippocampus-as-index|hippocampus]] (fast but superficial indexer), and the neocortex (vast permanent store). The "choir parable" ([[choir-parable-hip-neo]]) personifies these as characters: the forgetful Conductor, quick but shallow Hip, and slow but deep Neo. The critical constraint — Hip can only face one direction at a time (learning or rehearsing) — provides the neuroscience rationale for [[brain-breaks]] during instruction.

[[active-learning-declarative|Active learning]] is redefined as cognitive, not physical, engagement: activities that trigger retrieval, connection, and [[consolidation]] of neural links. The Freeman meta-analysis (1.5x failure rate in lecture) supports active learning, but the fine print reveals that even modest breaks with retrieval may suffice. Practical applications include [[think-pair-share]] and [[whip-around]]. [[bdnf-and-exercise|Exercise and BDNF]] add a physiological dimension: physical activity primes the neural substrate for learning by growing new dendritic spines.

### Evolution, Brain Development & Direct Instruction
From [[uncommon-sense-teaching]] Ch. 5: The [[biologically-primary-and-secondary-knowledge]] framework (David Geary) provides the evolutionary explanation for why some learning is effortless and some is hard. Primary abilities (face recognition, language) exploit neural architecture shaped by natural selection; secondary abilities (reading, math, formal reasoning) require the brain to repurpose those circuits — a process described by Stanislas Dehaene's [[neuronal-recycling-hypothesis]]. Letters colonize the occipitotemporal cortex, numbers the intraparietal cortex, regardless of culture.

This carries a direct pedagogical implication: [[direct-instruction]] is not a preference but a neurological necessity for secondary material. The "I do, we do, you do, extend it" model maps onto [[learn-it-link-it]] — teacher-directed phases build initial neural links, student-directed phases strengthen and extend them. Barak Rosenshine's principles of effective instruction operationalize this. Student-directed approaches are valuable *after* foundation-building, not before. The [[brain-development-timeline]] (neuropil → pruning → back-to-front maturation, prefrontal cortex last) adds a developmental dimension to why and when different instructional approaches are needed.

### The Procedural Pathway & Dual-System Learning
From [[uncommon-sense-teaching]] Ch. 6: The [[procedural-learning-system]] is the brain's second major pathway to [[long-term-memory]], operating through the basal ganglia largely outside conscious awareness. Where the [[declarative-learning-system]] is fast to learn but slow to use, the procedural system is slow to learn but fast to deploy — once mastered, skills like driving, language fluency, and mathematical intuition operate with uncanny swiftness.

The [[declarative-procedural-seesaw]] captures the key constraint: engaging one system de-emphasizes the other during learning, but knowledge acquired through both systems is complementary. Learning through both is like having two legs — more stable and more mobile than either alone. The [[procedural-front-and-back-door]] architecture reveals two entry points: a goal-directed front door (from prefrontal cortex/WM) and a habitual back door (from sensory cortex). Michael Ullman's declarative-procedural theory of language learning shows both systems can learn the same material in different ways.

[[interleaving|Interleaving]] and [[spaced-repetition]] emerge as the best methods for building procedural knowledge. Interleaving forces pattern discrimination — the kind of subtle, intuitive learning the procedural system excels at. Spaced repetition allows cross-system link connections to form. [[schemas|Schemas]] integrate both systems' contributions into "neural shelves" that enable rapid learning and [[transfer]]. The chapter's most provocative insight: [[verbal-explanation-vs-understanding|verbal explanation does not prove conceptual understanding]], because procedural knowledge resists articulation. Assessment must include application, not just explanation. Practical applications include [[concept-attainment]] (guided pattern discovery) and [[judicious-homework]] (short, interleaved sets).

### Procrastination & Self-Regulation (Teaching Perspective)
From [[uncommon-sense-teaching]] Ch. 4: [[procrastination]] is deepened with its neurological basis — the insular cortex generates genuine pain signals when a student merely *thinks* about an unpleasant task. The chapter reveals differential vulnerability: [[race-car-and-hiker-brains|race-car students]] can be the worst procrastinators because early-years speed masked the need for disciplined habits, while motivated hikers learn discipline earlier. The remedies connect back to the mode-switching framework: [[focused-and-diffuse-modes]] explains why deep learning *requires* time (creative neural connection-building happens in diffuse mode), making procrastination not just a time problem but a neuroscience problem. Practical applications include the [[pomodoro-technique]] (adapted for classroom use with age-adjusted timing), the [[hard-start-technique]] (leveraging diffuse-mode background processing during tests), and [[intermediate-task-scaffolding]] (structuring assignments with staged deadlines to make procrastination structurally impossible).

### Classroom Habits, Dopamine & Motivation
From [[uncommon-sense-teaching]] Ch. 7: The [[procedural-learning-system]] extends beyond academic skills to classroom management. Everyday routines — entering the room, lining up, transitioning between activities — are habits built through the same procedural pathway that encodes mathematical intuition or language fluency. Teaching procedures follows the [[learn-it-link-it]] / [[direct-instruction]] sequence: tell (I do) → show and practice (We do) → reinforce to automaticity (You do). Once the basal ganglia have encoded a routine, students execute it without conscious effort, freeing [[working-memory]] for learning. Consistency is paramount — inconsistent enforcement prevents procedural consolidation.

[[dopamine-and-learning|Dopamine]] is the neurochemical bridge between reward and learning. Unexpected rewards trigger dopamine surges that bathe forming synapses, directly strengthening neural connections — not just making students feel good, but physically facilitating the rewiring that learning requires. Critically, dopamine also temporarily halts the [[declarative-procedural-seesaw]], enabling the two systems to work together rather than competing. The flip side: punitive responses trigger dopamine crashes that signal neurons to *disconnect*, actively impairing learning in progress.

The expected/unexpected distinction matters for motivation. Expected rewards (grades, stickers, breaks) drive motivation through [[temporal-discounting]] — brains discount delayed rewards, so near-term rewards are more potent. But expected rewards do not enhance learning directly, because the brain is already predicting them. [[classroom-procedures|Classroom procedures]] leverage this: teach through direct instruction, then reinforce with unexpected praise during practice to get both the motivational and the learning-enhancement effects of dopamine. The schema-motivation dynamic adds another layer: students often dislike subjects not because of inherent disinterest but because they lack the [[schemas]] that would make the material accessible — practice builds schemas, schemas generate interest.

### Collaborative Learning, Stress & Social Dynamics
From [[uncommon-sense-teaching]] Ch. 8: [[eustress-and-learning|Eustress]] reframes stress as a learning tool, not an obstacle. Moderate transient stress triggers glucocorticoid release that strengthens neural connections, following an inverted-U curve — too little produces boredom, too much causes burnout. [[social-buffering|Social buffering]] is the neurochemical mechanism through which supportive teammates moderate stress hormones into the productive zone, making it one of the strongest neuroscience-based arguments for [[collaborative-learning]].

Effective collaborative learning requires deliberate structure: teacher-assigned groups of 3-4, defined roles (previewer, clarifier, connector, summarizer), behavioral expectations taught as [[classroom-procedures]], and periodic [[collaboration-clinics]] to troubleshoot group dysfunction. The five components of cooperative learning (positive interdependence, individual accountability, face-to-face interaction, social skills, group processing) operationalize what "well-structured group work" means. [[socioemotional-learning]] — partly biologically primary but requiring explicit instruction for sophisticated skills — develops naturally through well-designed group practice.

Important nuances: empathy must be paired with boundary-setting to prevent manipulation and codependency; moderate competition is a form of eustress, not something to eliminate; and group size involves a trade-off — larger groups produce bigger accomplishments while smaller groups (2-3) produce more creative results. [[brainwriting]] (individual brainstorming first, then group augmentation) bridges this gap.

### Online Teaching & Multimedia Design
From [[uncommon-sense-teaching]] Ch. 9: The book's neuroscience framework extends to online instruction with remarkably little modification — the brain is the same whether learning online or in person. [[multimedia-learning-theory]] (Richard Mayer) provides the design rationale: [[working-memory]]'s dual channels (auditory-verbal and visual-spatial) can be loaded simultaneously, but reading text aloud duplicates the verbal channel rather than complementing it. The [[top-down-and-bottom-up-attention]] framework explains why static talking-head videos lose students (no bottom-up attention capture) and why small editing tricks — camera switches, animated arrows, brief humor — sustain engagement without taxing willpower. [[instructor-presence]] emerges as one of the strongest predictors of student engagement and course completion online; even a low-quality amateur video builds the social connection that hiker students need to stay motivated.

The practical culmination is the [[flipped-classroom-online]] model: a Mon/Wed/Fri structure mapping [[learn-it-link-it]] across the week — asynchronous "I do" (Monday), synchronous "We do" (Wednesday), student-directed "You do" (Friday). This extends the direct instruction framework from Ch. 5 into hybrid course design. Online-specific [[classroom-procedures]] (ground rules, chat-room protocols, Simon Says for platform familiarization) parallel the procedural-system work from Ch. 7.

### Small Teaching & Practical Faculty Development
From [[small-teaching]] (James M. Lang, 2nd ed. 2021): A fourth source enters the wiki with a distinctive practitioner angle — Lang is an English professor and teaching center director, not a psychologist or neuroscientist. His contribution is the [[small-teaching-approach]]: translating learning science into small, immediately implementable modifications that busy faculty can adopt without overhauling their courses. The book's three-part structure — Knowledge, Understanding, Inspiration — mirrors a progression from cognitive foundations to affective dimensions.

**Ch. 1 "Predicting"** establishes [[prediction-as-learning]] as the first small teaching strategy: asking students to predict outcomes before instruction enhances learning even when predictions are wrong. The mechanism operates through [[prior-knowledge-activation]] (forcing students to surface what they know), the [[generation-effect]] (active production strengthening memory), and emotional investment (Brod 2021: pupil dilation/surprise during prediction reveal). The Part I introduction argues for [[knowledge-as-foundation]]: the widespread misreading of Bloom's taxonomy as a hierarchy to be climbed past has led instructors to skip knowledge-building, when in fact factual knowledge is the prerequisite for all higher-order thinking (Willingham). This reinforces the wiki's existing thread on the importance of [[long-term-memory]] and connects directly to thread #23 (education changes LTM, not WM).

## Emerging threads

1. **The chunking-as-Zettelkasten parallel**: Chunking in the brain mirrors Zettelkasten note-taking — both compress information into atomic, reusable, densely-linked units. This wiki itself is an externalized chunk library.
2. **Anti-patterns are as important as techniques**: Both sources give equal weight to what *not* to do (illusions of competence, overlearning, Einstellung, rereading) as to what to do. This is a pedagogical pattern worth preserving.
3. **The neuroscience-to-practice bridge**: Every practical technique is grounded in a neuroscience mechanism (spaced repetition ← synaptic consolidation, Pomodoro ← focused/diffuse cycling, recall ← reconsolidation). This grounds the advice in mechanism, not just correlation.
4. **Working memory as creativity lever**: The counterintuitive finding that smaller working memory may enhance creativity (via reduced Einstellung) is a thread worth tracking as more sources arrive.
5. **Desirable difficulty as unifying principle**: [[make-it-stick]] provides the organizing framework that [[learning-how-to-learn]] lacked — *why* retrieval, spacing, and interleaving work is because they're effortful, and effort during encoding/retrieval is the mechanism that produces durable learning. This reframes the entire toolkit.
6. **Convergence across sources**: Two independent sources (a neuroscience-based MOOC and a cognitive psychology book) arrived at nearly identical conclusions about what works and what doesn't. The convergence strengthens confidence in the core claims.
7. **The illusion extends to practice strategy**: Ch. 3 reveals that the illusion of competence is not limited to study techniques — it applies to how we *practice* as well. Even after experiencing evidence that interleaving produces better test results, learners cling to massed practice. The myth is self-reinforcing because momentary strength feels like real learning.
8. **Practice strategy as a triad**: Spacing, interleaving, and variation are distinct but complementary. Spacing allows consolidation. Interleaving develops discrimination. Variation builds flexible schemas. Each addresses a different dimension of durable learning.
9. **The three-step model as unifying mechanism**: Ch. 4 provides the theoretical scaffolding that earlier chapters lacked. The encoding → consolidation → reconsolidation model explains *how* every desirable difficulty works at the neural level: generation and interleaving deepen encoding; spacing allows consolidation; effortful retrieval triggers reconsolidation. This connects the cognitive psychology evidence to the neuroscience framework from [[learning-how-to-learn]].
10. **Retrieval cues as the overlooked dimension**: The distinction between stored knowledge and the ability to retrieve it is fundamental. Forgetting is usually lost cues, not lost knowledge. This reframes how we think about learning: it's not just about putting things in memory, but about building the routes to find them. Varied practice and interleaving multiply cues; massed practice narrows them.
11. **Fear of failure as a learning tax**: The French sixth-graders study shows that framing matters — teaching learners that errors are natural directly improves working memory use. This connects the psychology of learning to the sociology of education and achievement culture.
12. **Metacognition as the keystone**: Ch. 5 completes the argument arc of the book's first half. Ch. 1-4 established *what* works and *why*; Ch. 5 explains *why we resist what works and prefer what doesn't*. The answer is poor metacognition — our subjective sense of what we know is systematically wrong, and the mechanisms that corrupt it (narrative bias, fluency illusions, Dunning-Kruger, curse of knowledge) are deeply wired into human cognition. This reframes the entire learning problem: the obstacle isn't ignorance of effective strategies, it's the illusion that our current strategies already work.
13. **Calibration tools as "flight instruments"**: The cockpit metaphor is powerful - just as pilots learned (through crashes) to trust instruments over vestibular sensation, learners must develop the habit of checking objective measures (testing, feedback, peer review) rather than trusting the subjective feeling of knowing. This connects retrieval practice (Ch. 2) to metacognition (Ch. 5): testing isn't just a learning tool, it's the primary calibration instrument.
14. **Static labels versus developmental feedback**: Ch. 6 pushes the wiki away from fixed learner categories. The important contrast is not "visual learner vs. auditory learner" but static labeling versus developmental feedback. [[dynamic-testing]] treats poor performance as information about the next learning move, while [[learning-styles-myth]] encourages identity-based explanations that can narrow effort.
15. **Student-directed learning skepticism**: The book argues against "unschooler" and fully student-directed approaches - not because students shouldn't take control of their learning, but because their metacognition is too poor to self-direct effectively without instructor guidance and structured calibration.
16. **Ability is trainable, but mostly locally**: Ch. 7 strengthens the repo's anti-essentialist stance while keeping it precise. Practice can change the brain and increase performance, but usually in the circuits and judgments being exercised. This helps reconcile optimism about improvement with skepticism toward generalized brain-training claims.
17. **Motivation depends on interpretation, not just willpower**: Growth mindset matters because it changes what difficulty *means*. If hard learning is read as evidence of stupidity, learners retreat; if it is read as the mechanism of development, they persist. This gives the repo a tighter account of why many effective techniques are abandoned too early.
18. **Mnemonics are organizers, not substitutes**: The memory-palace material clarifies an important distinction. Retrieval structures can make large bodies of knowledge usable under pressure, but they do not by themselves create conceptual understanding. This sharpens how the wiki should talk about memorization going forward.
19. **Three-source convergence on core claims**: *Uncommon Sense Teaching* is the third independent source to arrive at the same core conclusions about retrieval practice, the dangers of passive study, and the working-memory/long-term-memory distinction. The convergence across a neuroscience MOOC, a cognitive psychology book, and a teacher-focused neuroscience guide further strengthens confidence in these claims.
20. **"Learn it, link it" as the neural story beneath chunking**: The two-phase framework from [[uncommon-sense-teaching]] provides the neuron-level narrative for what [[chunking]] describes at the cognitive level. "Learn it" = initial synaptic connections; "link it" = strengthening and extending those connections into durable, interconnected networks. This gives the wiki a more complete vertical stack from synapse to study strategy.
21. **Test anxiety as a memory-system diagnosis**: Reframing "I have test anxiety" as "I reach into long-term memory and nothing's there" is a powerful diagnostic tool for teachers. It shifts the intervention from emotional support (calming anxiety) to instructional change (teaching retrieval practice).
22. **WM capacity as the real individual difference**: While [[learning-styles-myth]] was debunked, working memory capacity is a genuine, measurable individual difference that shapes learning trajectories. The [[race-car-and-hiker-brains]] framework provides a non-deficit framing: speed is not necessarily an advantage, and slowness enables depth, flexibility, and error correction (Cajal, Hayek). This connects to thread #4 (WM as creativity lever) — Ch. 2 confirms that lessened WM heightens creative insight.
23. **Education changes LTM, not WM**: The [[expertise-reversal-effect]] and [[long-term-memory-boosts-working-memory]] together establish that the goal of teaching is not to change students' processing speed but to build the long-term memory structures that make processing speed less important. This is a profound reframing of what "closing the gap" means.
24. **The hippocampus as learning bottleneck**: Ch. 3 reveals that the hippocampus can only face one direction at a time — receiving new information OR rehearsing existing connections to the neocortex. This single constraint explains why nonstop instruction undermines learning, why brain breaks are essential, and why cramming produces fragile knowledge. The choir parable (conductor/Hip/Neo) is the book's central teaching metaphor.
25. **Active ≠ physically active**: The "Grecian urn" fallacy — equating hands-on projects with genuine learning — is debunked. What matters is cognitive engagement (retrieval, connection, consolidation), not physical activity. The civil-war/civil-rights confusion illustrates that lively discussion without retrieval produces nothing durable.
26. **Teaching as interval training**: The dual demand on teachers — make the mind work hard (retrieval) AND let it rest (hippocampal offloading) — mirrors athletic interval training. This reframes the tension between direct instruction and student activity not as competing philosophies but as complementary phases of a single process.

27. **Primary vs. secondary as the master variable for instructional design**: The biologically-primary/secondary distinction may be the most fundamental axis for choosing pedagogy. Primary material → immersion, discovery, play. Secondary material → direct instruction, scaffolding, deliberate practice. The false dichotomy between teacher-directed and student-directed approaches dissolves when viewed through this lens: both are appropriate at different points on the primary/secondary gradient.
28. **The teacher-directed → student-directed pipeline**: Direct instruction is not opposed to student autonomy — it *produces* it. The "I do, we do, you do, extend it" sequence maps onto learn-it/link-it, showing that teacher-led phases (learn it) create the neural foundation that makes student-led phases (link it) productive rather than frustrating. This reframes the instructional debate from "which is better?" to "when does each apply?"

29. **The procedural system as the "hidden" learner**: The procedural system is critically important for learning complex ideas — not just motor skills — yet it operates below conscious awareness. This means a significant portion of what students learn is invisible to both the student and the teacher. The challenge of teaching to both systems simultaneously is the central pedagogical insight of Ch. 6.
30. **The seesaw constraint on instructional design**: Teachers must alternate between declarative instruction (I do, We do) and procedural practice (You do, active exercises) because the two systems trade off during learning. This resolves the false teacher-directed vs. student-directed dichotomy at a deeper level than the primary/secondary framework (thread #27) — it explains *why* the alternation works neurologically.
31. **Assessment must match learning pathway**: If verbal explanation tests only the declarative system, then traditional assessment systematically undervalues students who learned procedurally. This connects to the race-car/hiker framework (thread #22) and has equity implications: hiker/procedural learners — who may include students with autism spectrum traits or low WM — are disadvantaged by explanation-only assessment.
32. **Schemas as the integration point**: Schemas are where the declarative-procedural, chunking, interleaving, and transfer threads converge. They are the neural architecture of expertise, integrating knowledge from both systems into structures that enable rapid learning and flexible application. The schema concept may eventually subsume or reorganize several existing threads.

33. **Dopamine as learning facilitator, not just motivator**: Ch. 7 reveals that dopamine does double duty — it drives motivation (expected rewards, temporal discounting) AND directly strengthens synaptic connections (unexpected rewards). The distinction between expected and unexpected rewards is critical: expected rewards keep students working but don't enhance learning; unexpected rewards enhance both. This means being "too positive all the time" is counterproductive — constant positivity becomes expected, and its dopamine-boosting effect disappears. The dopamine crash from punitive responses is equally important: yelling at students doesn't just make them feel bad, it physically undermines the neural consolidation in progress.
34. **Practice precedes motivation, not the other way around**: The schema-motivation dynamic inverts the commonsense assumption. Teachers often wait for students to become interested before assigning practice, but schemas must be built before interest can develop. "What interests us takes time to develop" (Wang & Morris). This argues for structured practice even in the absence of student enthusiasm, trusting that competence will generate its own motivation.
35. **Eustress as a pedagogical tool**: Moderate transient stress enhances learning, not just motivation. The inverted-U curve (glucocorticoids vs. memory performance) means the goal is not to eliminate stress but to keep it in the productive zone. Social buffering from supportive group members is the mechanism that moderates stress — making collaborative learning a biochemical intervention, not just a social one.
36. **Structure is the prerequisite for collaborative freedom**: Veronique Mintz's account of dysfunctional group work and the chapter's detailed procedural remedies establish a pattern: the more structured the collaborative framework (roles, behavioral expectations, collaboration clinics, countdown timers), the more genuine the social and cognitive benefits. Unstructured "put them in groups" approaches produce the opposite of their intent.
37. **Group size shapes output type**: Research (Suparna Rajaram) shows that larger groups produce bigger accomplishments but suppress individual creativity, while smaller groups and individuals produce more innovative results. Brainwriting bridges this gap. Good teaching develops abilities in both modes.

38. **Online teaching as neuroscience translation, not a new domain**: Ch. 9 demonstrates that online teaching does not require new learning science — the same principles (multimedia dual channels, brain breaks, retrieval practice, learn-it/link-it, direct instruction in short bursts) apply directly. What changes is the *delivery mechanism*, not the underlying neuroscience. This makes the chapter more of an application guide than a theoretical advance, but the top-down/bottom-up attention framework and instructor presence as an empirical construct are genuine additions to the wiki's conceptual toolkit.

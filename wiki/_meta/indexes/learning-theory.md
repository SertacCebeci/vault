## Concepts (63)

- [[active-learning-declarative]] — Active learning from a neuroscience perspective: cognitive engagement that builds and consolidates neural links via the declarative pathway
- [[biologically-primary-and-secondary-knowledge]] — Evolutionary framework: primary abilities (faces, language) are pre-wired; secondary abilities (reading, math) require deliberate neural repurposing
- [[brain-breaks]] — Strategic pauses during instruction enabling hippocampal offloading and micro-consolidation
- [[chunking]] — Binding information into compact neural patterns through focused attention, understanding, and context
- [[consolidation]] — Brain reorganizes and stabilizes memory traces into long-term memory; essay-writing analogy
- [[covert-retrieval]] — Silent retrieval practice produces essentially the same retention gains as overt retrieval
- [[curse-of-knowledge]] — Experts forget what it's like not to know; expertise makes teaching harder
- [[declarative-learning-system]] — The three-part neural architecture for conscious learning: working memory (conductor) → hippocampus (indexer) → neocortex (store)
- [[declarative-procedural-seesaw]] — The two learning systems act like a seesaw; learning through both produces the most powerful knowledge
- [[deliberate-practice]] — Focusing effort on difficult material rather than repeating what's already mastered
- [[desirable-difficulty]] — Learning is deeper and more durable when it requires effort; easy learning creates illusions
- [[discrimination-learning]] — Interleaving's key benefit: developing the ability to classify problems and select solutions
- [[dunning-kruger-effect]] — Incompetent people overestimate competence; the skill to perform well is the skill to recognize performance
- [[dynamic-testing]] — Test-improve-retest assessment cycle that treats performance as developmental rather than fixed
- [[einstellung]] — Existing neural patterns blocking better solutions; the "mindset" trap
- [[elaboration]] — Giving new material meaning by expressing it in your own words and connecting to prior knowledge
- [[encoding]] — First step of learning: converting perceptions into memory traces in short-term memory
- [[errorless-learning-myth]] — Debunking Skinner; errors + corrective feedback strengthen learning
- [[eustress-and-learning]] — Moderate transient stress enhances cognition and learning via glucocorticoids; inverted-U curve
- [[expertise-reversal-effect]] — Guidance that helps novices can impede experts as LTM takes over the load scaffolding once provided
- [[feedback-timing]] — Delayed feedback produces better long-term retention than immediate feedback
- [[focused-and-diffuse-modes]] — The brain's two complementary thinking modes: tight/concentrated vs. relaxed/big-picture
- [[forgetting-curve]] — Ebbinghaus (1885): ~70% lost quickly without retrieval; the central challenge learning must overcome
- [[generation-effect]] — Active production of answers strengthens memory more than passive reception
- [[hebbian-learning]] — "Neurons that fire together wire together" — the synaptic mechanism underlying all learning
- [[hippocampus-as-index]] — The hippocampus functions as an index to the neocortex, not as storage; limited capacity, dual-direction constraint
- [[interleaving]] — Mixing different problem types/approaches to build flexibility and creativity
- [[knowledge-as-foundation]] — Factual knowledge is the prerequisite for higher-order thinking, not the bottom rung of Bloom's pyramid to skip
- [[learn-it-link-it]] — Two-phase learning framework: initial neural connections (learn it) → strengthening and extending through practice (link it)
- [[learning-styles-myth]] — Matching instruction to a preferred modality is not supported by evidence
- [[long-term-memory]] — Vast distributed storage requiring consolidation via hippocampus; reconsolidation on recall
- [[long-term-memory-boosts-working-memory]] — LTM links extend apparent WM capacity through chunking without changing the underlying neural limit
- [[massed-practice]] — The myth of focused repetitive drilling; momentary strength vs. underlying habit strength
- [[memory-distortion]] — Memory is reconstruction, not playback; distorted by narrative, suggestion, imagination, interference
- [[mental-models]] — Interconnected webs of knowledge enabling expert performance and rapid decision-making
- [[metaphor-and-analogy]] — Visual metaphors as powerful cognitive tools for learning and understanding
- [[multimedia-learning-theory]] — Mayer's dual-channel theory: combining visual and verbal leverages WM's hearing and seeing components
- [[narrative-bias]] — Deep compulsion to impose coherent stories on ambiguous information; shapes memory and perception
- [[neuronal-recycling-hypothesis]] — Dehaene's theory: secondary skills repurpose brain regions evolved for related primary functions; constrained plasticity
- [[neuroplasticity]] — The brain remains modifiable through experience, practice, injury, and use
- [[overlearning]] — Continued practice beyond mastery; useful for automaticity, wasteful for regular learning
- [[prediction-as-learning]] — Making predictions before instruction enhances learning even when predictions are wrong; emotional investment drives encoding
- [[priming-for-learning]] — Struggling with a problem before being shown the solution deepens encoding
- [[prior-knowledge-activation]] — Deliberately surfacing existing knowledge before new material strengthens encoding by creating cognitive hooks
- [[procedural-front-and-back-door]] — Two entry points to the procedural system: goal-directed (front) and habitual (back)
- [[procedural-learning-system]] — The brain's second learning pathway via basal ganglia; nonconscious, slow to learn, fast to deploy
- [[race-car-and-hiker-brains]] — Working memory capacity differences: fast processors (race cars) vs. slow-but-deep processors (hikers); both reach the destination
- [[recall-as-learning]] — Active retrieval as one of the most powerful learning techniques (Karpicke research)
- [[reconsolidation]] — Retrieval makes learning pliable again for updating and strengthening
- [[reflection-as-retrieval]] — Reflection on experience as a compound form of retrieval practice, elaboration, and mental rehearsal
- [[retrieval-cues]] — Mental triggers for locating stored knowledge; forgetting = lost cues, not lost knowledge
- [[rule-learning]] — Abstracting underlying principles instead of memorizing examples; crucial for transfer
- [[schemas]] — Neural shelves integrating procedural and declarative knowledge; enable transfer and rapid learning
- [[spaced-repetition]] — Reviewing at increasing intervals for durable retention; "let the neural mortar dry"
- [[structure-building]] — Extracting the main ideas from new material and organizing them into a coherent framework
- [[system-1-and-system-2]] — Kahneman's dual-process framework: automatic/intuitive vs. controlled/analytical thinking
- [[temporal-discounting]] — Brains discount delayed rewards; steeper discounting in teenagers linked to the striatum
- [[testing-effect]] — The act of retrieval strengthens memory more than additional study; research from 1917 to present
- [[transfer]] — Chunks from one domain helping learn in surprisingly different domains
- [[varied-practice]] — Practicing the same skill under different conditions builds broader, more flexible schemas
- [[verbal-explanation-vs-understanding]] — Verbal explanation does not prove understanding; procedural knowledge may resist articulation
- [[worked-example-effect]] — Studying sample problems forms mental templates; necessary for initial learning but insufficient without independent practice
- [[working-memory]] — The ~4-slot prefrontal cortex system for immediate conscious processing

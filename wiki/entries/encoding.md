# Encoding

Encoding is the first step of learning: the brain converts sensory perceptions into mental representations called *memory traces*. When you watch a demonstration, listen to an explanation, or practice a new skill, your brain transforms what you perceive into chemical and electrical changes that form a pattern you can later reconstruct. These traces are initially held in short-term memory — fragile notes on a scratchpad — and most are soon forgotten. Only the experiences that matter get promoted to durable storage through [[consolidation]].

## Elaboration

Much of what we encode is ephemeral by design. How you jiggled the broken locker latch this morning, the route you took around a puddle — these perceptions clutter short-term memory briefly and are discarded. This is adaptive: if everything were retained with equal strength, you'd drown in irrelevant detail and struggle to find the knowledge you actually need.

The experiences and learning we want to keep must be made stronger and more durable. This requires active processing — not just passive exposure. The Mia Blundetto jump school example illustrates this well: standing in a gravel pit watching a parachute landing fall demonstrated, your brain encodes the visual pattern of the instructor's body position, the sequence of ground contact, the feeling of gravel underfoot. But *watching* alone produces a weak trace. It's when you attempt the fall yourself — receiving corrective feedback, adjusting your posture — that encoding deepens, because you're now generating motor memory traces alongside the visual ones.

The quality of initial encoding matters, but it is not sufficient. Even well-encoded material will fade without [[consolidation]] and periodic [[recall-as-learning|retrieval]]. Encoding is the necessary first step, but durable learning requires all three stages of the process.

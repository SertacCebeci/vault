# Neuroplasticity

Neuroplasticity is the brain's capacity to change its structure and functioning in response to experience, practice, injury, and use. Learning is not simply the accumulation of information inside a fixed organ; it is the gradual rewiring, strengthening, pruning, and repurposing of neural pathways so new kinds of perception, judgment, and action become possible.

## Elaboration

Genes help determine the broad architecture of a brain, but experience shapes much of its usable fine structure. Practice can strengthen the circuits a skill depends on, increase myelination along well-used pathways, and reorganize sequences of thought or action into more efficient units. This is one reason expertise can look effortless from the outside: repeated work has changed the underlying circuitry that supports performance.

Crucially, the brain is plastic but not super-plastic. [[stanislas-dehaene|Dehaene]]'s [[neuronal-recycling-hypothesis]] demonstrates this constraint: when the brain learns [[biologically-primary-and-secondary-knowledge|biologically secondary]] skills like reading or math, it does not grow entirely new circuits — it repurposes existing regions with functions evolutionarily similar to the new skill. Letters colonize the occipitotemporal cortex (evolved for object recognition), numbers colonize the bilateral intraparietal cortex (evolved for quantity estimation). This constrained recycling is universal across cultures, demonstrating that plasticity operates within anatomical boundaries.

This constrained plasticity explains why some brain damage can be repaired by shifting functions to nearby related areas, while other damage is irreparable because the required rewiring exceeds the brain's limits. It also explains why secondary learning is genuinely hard: the brain must painstakingly retrain regions evolved for different purposes, which requires sustained [[deliberate-practice]] and [[direct-instruction]].

The [[brain-development-timeline]] shapes the landscape of plasticity. In early infancy, a dense nest of intertwined axons and dendrites called a *neuropil* forms, producing a surge of new synaptic connections peaking around age two. Then pruning begins, decreasing synaptic density as the brain matures from back (vision, hearing) to front (prefrontal cortex — planning and judgment). But the ability to form and prune connections persists throughout life — the ease and speed change, not the fundamental capacity.

Neuroplasticity also helps explain why ability is neither infinitely general nor perfectly fixed. The benefits of training are often specific to the networks being exercised. A pianist's practice refines the circuits involved in musical perception and finger control, not every form of cognition at once. The same principle underlies recovery after injury, habit formation, and the long-term effects of [[deliberate-practice]]: the brain remains modifiable, but it changes in relation to what it repeatedly does.

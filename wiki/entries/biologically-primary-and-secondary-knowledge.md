# Biologically Primary and Secondary Knowledge

## Lead

Some knowledge is easy for human brains to acquire because evolution has pre-wired the neural architecture to absorb it. Recognizing faces, learning a first language, basic social interaction — these are *biologically primary* abilities. Other knowledge — reading, writing, mathematics, formal reasoning — requires the brain to repurpose circuits that evolved for different purposes. This culturally transmitted material is *biologically secondary* and demands deliberate instruction to learn.

## Elaboration

The distinction was formalized by evolutionary educational psychologist [[david-geary]]. Primary abilities emerge without formal instruction: put a baby near native speakers and language acquisition happens automatically; facial recognition is so deeply wired that the inability to recognize faces (prosopagnosia) is exceedingly rare. These abilities were honed over hundreds of thousands of generations of natural selection — infants who could recognize caregivers and communicate survived.

Secondary abilities are different in kind, not just degree. Reading and mathematics are recent cultural inventions — writing is only about 5,000 years old, and for most of human history even basic arithmetic was a specialized skill. Because evolution has not had time to build dedicated circuits for these tasks, the brain must stretch and repurpose existing neural architecture to handle them. This repurposing is the mechanism described by [[stanislas-dehaene]]'s [[neuronal-recycling-hypothesis]].

The primary/secondary distinction carries a critical implication for teaching: the more biologically secondary the material, the more students need [[direct-instruction]] — carefully scaffolded, teacher-directed guidance. Primary learning is fun and feels effortless, which can mislead teachers, parents, and policymakers into expecting *all* learning to be easy and enjoyable. Secondary learning requires focused effort and often involves [[desirable-difficulty]]. Students who struggle with reading or math are not deficient — they are grappling with material the human brain was never designed to learn on its own.

The boundary between primary and secondary is not sharp. There are in-between skills — drinking from a glass, tying shoes — that are not purely instinctive but are far easier to acquire than algebra. The two categories overlap, and individual differences in [[working-memory]] capacity affect how readily a student can tackle secondary material.

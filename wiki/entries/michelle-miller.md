# Michelle Miller

Cognitive psychologist (Northern Arizona University) and author of *Minds Online: Teaching Effectively with Technology* (2014) — one of the first book-length attempts to apply learning-science findings specifically to online and hybrid course design.

## Key contributions

- **The "vast closet" metaphor for long-term memory.** Miller's framing that long-term memory is essentially unlimited in storage but constrained in *retrieval* — "a vast amount of closet space" where the hard part is finding what you need in a timely fashion — is one of the cleanest pedagogical summaries of the research consensus. Retrieval practice matters because it paves the neural pathways that let us find material when we need it. See [[long-term-memory]] and [[testing-effect]].
- **Online retrieval design principles.** In [[small-teaching]] Ch. 2 Miller's specific recommendations drive the **Online Retrieval** model: (1) conditional release — require short quizzes at the end of each online reading section before students can advance; (2) frequent quizzing from large question banks (mitigates cheating and provides many retrieval reps); (3) **spacing due dates** across the week rather than bunching them, which yields both retrieval practice and [[interleaving]] as a by-product.
- Observation that frequent quiz breaks paradoxically *reduce* test anxiety — students who did interspersed quizzes reported less anxiety about the cumulative test, not more.
- Defense of multiple-choice when time is limited: "The best quiz is the one that students will actually do." Short-answer is better; multiple-choice you will actually assign beats short-answer you won't.

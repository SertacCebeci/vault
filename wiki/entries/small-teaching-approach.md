# Small Teaching

Small teaching is an approach to improving higher education through small, evidence-based modifications to course design, classroom practice, and communication with students — rather than requiring wholesale transformation of one's pedagogy. Like "small ball" in baseball (bunting, stealing bases, sacrifice flies), small teaching achieves significant cumulative impact through individually modest moves.

## Elaboration

The approach rests on the premise that most college instructors are too busy to undertake radical pedagogical overhauls. They teach multiple courses, serve on committees, do research, and manage personal lives. Small teaching meets them where they are: each strategy can be implemented with minimal preparation and grading, often in five to ten minutes of class time. Yet the research shows these small interventions can raise student performance by the equivalent of a full letter grade.

Small teaching takes three forms: (1) brief classroom or online learning activities lasting 5-10 minutes that capture attention and boost engagement; (2) one-time interventions in a course, like a Minute Thesis exercise, that occupy a full class period but only need to happen once or twice per semester; and (3) small modifications to course design or communication that don't translate into specific activities but shape the overall learning environment.

To qualify as a small teaching strategy, a technique must meet three criteria: it must have a foundation in learning science research, it must have demonstrated positive impact in real educational settings (not just laboratory conditions), and it should be personally observable in teaching practice. The book organizes these strategies into three progressive domains: knowledge acquisition ([[prediction-as-learning]], retrieval, interleaving), understanding (connecting, practicing, explaining), and inspiration (belonging, motivating, continued learning).

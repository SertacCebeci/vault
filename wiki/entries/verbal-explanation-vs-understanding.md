# Verbal Explanation vs. Understanding

Being able to explain a concept verbally does not necessarily prove deep conceptual understanding, and being unable to explain a concept does not prove lack of understanding. This is because verbal explanation is a [[declarative-learning-system|declarative]] skill, while genuine understanding may reside partly or wholly in the [[procedural-learning-system|procedural system]], which operates below conscious awareness.

## Elaboration

Teachers often assume that if a student can articulate an explanation, they truly understand the material. Research by Kevin Dunbar and colleagues shows this is unreliable: adults who can explicitly state the correct pattern of Newtonian motion show the same brain activation patterns as adults who do not understand it. Reciting a correct verbal formula may reflect declarative memorization rather than genuine comprehension.

The reverse is equally important. Students who understand a concept through their procedural system — intuitively grasping grammar rules, simplifying fractions, or recognizing patterns — may struggle to translate that understanding into words. Students with poor [[working-memory]] who learn primarily through hiker-like procedural pathways can become frustrated and disengaged when teachers insist that verbal explanation is the only valid way to demonstrate knowledge. This can be an instance of the [[expertise-reversal-effect]]: the student genuinely understands the content but is demotivated by being forced through a declarative demonstration that does not match how they learned it.

The practical implication is that assessment should include opportunities for students to demonstrate understanding through application and performance, not only through verbal or written explanation. Sometimes good teaching means accepting that if a student knows how to get the right answer and is enjoying the material, that is sufficient evidence of understanding.

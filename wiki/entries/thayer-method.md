# Thayer Method

The Thayer method is an instructional system used at the U.S. Military Academy at West Point, developed almost two hundred years ago by superintendent Sylvanus Thayer. It provides clear learning objectives for every course, places the responsibility for meeting those objectives on the student, and incorporates quizzing and recitation into every class meeting.

## How it works

The method reverses the typical classroom dynamic. Instead of the instructor lecturing and students absorbing, students arrive having wrestled with the material on their own, and class time is spent testing, applying, and discussing it:

1. **Pre-class generation** — Students read assigned material before class, guided by specific learning objectives. The point is not to passively absorb but to engage actively: start with questions and read for answers. This is a form of [[generation-effect|generation]] — struggling with new material before instruction deepens encoding.

2. **Daily quizzing** — Each class opens with a quiz on the assigned reading's learning objectives, ensuring [[testing-effect|retrieval practice]] is built into the structure rather than left to student initiative.

3. **Board work and recitation** — Students "take to the boards" (slate panels on all four walls) in small groups to collaborate on higher-order questions that require integrating ideas from the reading and applying them at a conceptual level. A student from each group gives a recitation explaining their group's answer, and the work is critiqued. This combines [[recall-as-learning|retrieval]], [[generation-effect|generation]], and [[peer-instruction]].

4. **Constructs over facts** — All class meetings focus on constructs and larger concepts, not specific facts. When students don't take to the boards, they engage in exercises, demonstrations, or group work aimed at understanding and articulating the concepts underlying the material.

The method works even for students not prone to working hard on their own outside of class, because the daily structure of quizzing and participation makes effort non-optional. It has been adopted in various forms beyond West Point, including at Riverside Military Academy for high school students with a much wider range of ability.

## Concepts applied

- [[testing-effect]] — daily quizzes ensure retrieval practice is routine, not optional
- [[generation-effect]] — pre-class reading with learning objectives forces students to wrestle with material before instruction
- [[recall-as-learning]] — board work and recitation require recall without open materials
- [[calibration]] — quizzes function as regular azimuth checks: "are you gaining the mastery you need?"
- [[peer-instruction]] — small-group board work and recitation combine peer discussion with public demonstration

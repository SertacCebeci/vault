# Spaced Repetition

Spaced repetition is the practice of reviewing material at increasing intervals over time rather than cramming it all at once. It works because each retrieval and reconsolidation cycle strengthens the synaptic connections in [[long-term-memory]], and the time gaps between sessions allow the brain's connections to rearrange themselves into simpler, better, deeper, stronger configurations — just as you must let each layer of a brick wall set before adding the next.

## Elaboration

Repeating something 20 times in one evening is far less effective than repeating it the same number of times spread over several days. The research is clear: 10 minutes per month over a semester produces better retention than an hour of cramming the night before an exam. This is because cramming builds fragile, short-term connections — you may retrieve the information for tomorrow's test, but it will quickly fade.

When you first learn a concept, your brain struggles to make connections any way it can, and the initial configuration is often not optimal. The best way to allow those connections to rearrange into stronger forms is to take a break, then return repeatedly over time. This returning over time underlies the brain's ability to [[consolidation|consolidate]] information. The revisiting may even allow links created by the [[procedural-learning-system]] and links created by the [[declarative-learning-system]] to find ways to connect if they relate to the same concept — one reason why learning with both systems produces more powerful, flexible knowledge.

The practical implementation involves starting with short intervals (review the same evening, then the next day) and gradually extending them as mastery increases. Flash card systems like Anki have built-in algorithms for this, automatically adjusting intervals from days to weeks to months. The technique pairs naturally with [[recall-as-learning]] — each spaced repetition session should involve active recall, not passive re-reading. Writing and saying material aloud helps set auditory hooks alongside visual ones. [[sleep-and-learning|Sleep]] between sessions is particularly valuable as it consolidates the day's learning.

Spacing guidelines remain imprecise. For an upcoming test a week away, daily review in the preceding week is reasonable. For year-long retention, review once every three weeks is a good rule of thumb. But everyone is different — information sticks better with some students than others, as a function of their preexisting [[schemas]], their internal motivation, and the facility with which their brains create mental links. [[interleaving]] itself appears to be a form of spaced repetition, which may partly explain its effectiveness. Conversely, efforts to make shortened, condensed, intensive study courses may be counterproductive because they leave less time for spacing and consolidation.

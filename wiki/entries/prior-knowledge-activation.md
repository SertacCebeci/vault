# Prior Knowledge Activation

New learning is built on the scaffolding of what a learner already knows. Deliberately surfacing and engaging existing knowledge before introducing new material strengthens encoding by giving incoming information a structure to attach to. Without activation, prior knowledge remains inert — present in [[long-term-memory]] but not connected to the new material being taught.

## Elaboration

When instructors ask students what they already know about a topic — through knowledge dumps, brainstorming activities, or simple questions — they achieve several things simultaneously. Students retrieve and strengthen existing knowledge (a form of [[recall-as-learning]]). They make their current mental models visible, which helps instructors identify misconceptions before those misconceptions interfere with new learning. And they create cognitive "hooks" that new information can latch onto, improving the depth and durability of encoding.

Prior knowledge activation matters even when the activated knowledge is incomplete or partly wrong. The process of surfacing what you think you know, and then encountering new information that confirms, extends, or corrects that knowledge, produces deeper learning than encountering the new information cold. This is why [[prediction-as-learning]] works: the prediction forces activation regardless of whether the prediction itself is correct.

The research is unambiguous that prior knowledge "plays a critical role in learning" and that instructors need to assess and build upon the knowledge students bring into their courses. Ignoring prior knowledge — or assuming students will activate it on their own — leaves the most powerful learning scaffold unused.

# Multimedia Learning Theory

A picture paired with a verbal explanation helps students grasp a concept faster and more deeply than either channel alone. This is because [[working-memory]] has two largely independent components — an auditory-verbal channel and a visual-spatial channel. Presenting information through both channels simultaneously lets learners encode more without exceeding either channel's limited capacity.

## Elaboration

The theory, developed primarily by educational psychologist [[richard-mayer]], yields several practical design principles for both online and face-to-face instruction. Complex images should appear incrementally — part by part — so that each element can be processed as the instructor explains it. Extraneous material (decorative graphics, dense bullet-point text, busy backgrounds) competes for limited channel capacity and should be stripped away. Crucially, reading a paragraph of on-screen text aloud *undermines* learning: when the same verbal information enters through both eye and ear, the two signals interfere rather than complement each other. Brief phrases on screen that *emphasize* what is being said work; long passages that *duplicate* it do not.

These principles connect to [[direct-instruction]]'s emphasis on clear, structured presentation and to the broader concept of [[chunking]] — multimedia design is essentially chunking for the eyes and ears. The theory also provides the neuroscience rationale for the "five-minute rule" in synchronous online teaching: short, focused segments prevent either channel from becoming overloaded.

# Discrimination Learning

Discrimination learning is the ability to assess context, identify what kind of problem or situation you're facing, and select the appropriate response from your repertoire. It is one of the most significant advantages that [[interleaving]] and [[varied-practice]] confer over [[massed-practice]]. In real life, problems and opportunities arrive unpredictably and out of sequence — you must first figure out *what kind of problem this is* before you can apply a solution. Massed practice never exercises this critical sorting process; interleaved and varied practice make it central.

## Elaboration

In math education, massed practice is built into the textbook structure: each chapter covers one problem type, and the homework problems all require the same technique. Students can solve them without ever asking "which algorithm do I use?" But on the final exam, the problems are mixed — and students who have only massed their practice flounder because they've never practiced the discrimination step.

Research on painting attribution demonstrates this vividly. When students studied one artist's works in a block before moving to the next artist, they learned the *commonalities* within each artist's style. But when students studied paintings interleaved across multiple artists, they learned the *differences* between artists — which proved far more useful for correctly identifying both studied and novel works. The interleaving group even outperformed on paintings they had never seen, demonstrating genuine conceptual understanding rather than memorized associations.

Bird classification studies reinforce this finding. Because bird species within a family share many traits but no single trait is universal, classification requires learning "characteristic" rather than "defining" traits — a judgment call, not a rule lookup. Interleaved practice proved far superior to massed practice for developing this conceptual skill. The studies suggest that strategies building discrimination help learners reach beyond memorization into higher spheres of comprehension.

Douglas Larsen draws a direct parallel to medical diagnosis: the skills required for bird classification are similar to those required for diagnosing patients. Variety in clinical experience helps doctors build the implicit pattern-matching that allows them to discriminate between conditions and select appropriate treatments.

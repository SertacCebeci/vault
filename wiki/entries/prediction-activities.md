# Prediction Activities

Brief, low-preparation classroom activities that ask students to make predictions about course material before, during, or after instruction. These leverage [[prediction-as-learning]] to deepen encoding, activate [[prior-knowledge-activation]], and orient students toward key content — all in five minutes or less.

## How it works

Prediction activities exploit the brain's natural prediction-error-correction cycle. By asking students to commit to an answer before receiving instruction, the activity forces engagement with prior knowledge, creates emotional investment in learning the correct answer, and produces a productive surprise response that strengthens memory. Four models cover the range:

**Activating Prior Knowledge:** Before introducing new content, ask students what they already know. This can be a knowledge dump ("list everything you know about X"), a brainstorming session, or a brief written pretest. The goal is to surface existing knowledge — correct or not — so new material has something to attach to.

**Polling Predictions:** Use clickers, Poll Everywhere, index cards, or raised hands to pose conceptual prediction questions mid-lecture. Students predict outcomes, vote, and then see the correct answer. The anonymity of polling technology allows students to commit to predictions without fear of public embarrassment.

**Prediction-Exposure-Feedback:** The simplest model — ask students to predict what will happen next in a case, experiment, or narrative; expose them to the actual outcome; then invite reflection on why they were right or wrong. Follows a "pause, predict, ponder" rhythm adaptable to any discipline.

**Closing Predictions:** End class by asking students to predict answers to questions about the next session's material. This primes their reading or studying by activating [[prior-knowledge-activation]] before they encounter the new content on their own. Revisit predictions at the opening of the next class to close the loop.

## Concepts applied
- [[prediction-as-learning]] — the core mechanism: generating predictions strengthens subsequent encoding
- [[prior-knowledge-activation]] — predictions force students to surface and organize what they already know

# Interleaving

## Lead

Interleaving is the practice of mixing different problem types, approaches, or concepts during practice rather than repeating the same thing in blocks. While [[chunking]] builds individual pieces of knowledge, interleaving builds the flexibility to know *when* to use each chunk — which is equally important. It is one of the most powerful methods for developing [[procedural-learning-system|procedural knowledge]] and building [[schemas]].

## Elaboration

A textbook chapter typically focuses on one technique, so when you flip to the practice problems you already know which approach to use. This undermines learning. The best way to study is to jump back and forth between problems that require different techniques or strategies — even from different chapters. This feels harder and less fluent than blocked practice, but the difficulty is productive.

Interleaving may boost procedural memory specifically because it gives students practice picking up on patterns — detecting the subtle differences between somewhat similar items or techniques. Detecting and intuitively mastering patterns after seeing, hearing, and doing something many times is precisely the kind of learning the [[procedural-learning-system]] excels at. Somewhat similar categories (seals vs. sea lions, preterite vs. imperfect tenses in Spanish) are perfect fodder for interleaving. However, categories with obvious differences (dogs vs. cats) do not benefit from interleaving because the distinctions are too apparent to require practice.

Interleaving is extraordinarily important for creativity. When you interleave within a single subject, you build solid neural patterns to draw on. When you interleave between multiple subjects or disciplines, you can more easily make novel connections between chunks in different fields, enhancing creativity further. Philosopher Thomas Kuhn discovered that paradigm shifts in science are often brought about by people originally trained in a different discipline — they're not trapped by [[einstellung|Einstellung]]-like blocked thoughts.

Students often think they are not learning when interleaving because the answers do not flow as easily as during blocked practice. As Peter Brown and his coauthors note in *Make It Stick*: "Massed practice gives us the warm sensation of mastery because we're looping information through short-term memory without having to reconstruct the learning from long-term memory." But this laborious quality is exactly what makes interleaving a [[desirable-difficulty]]. Robert Bjork's research confirms that interleaving produces worse practice performance yet dramatically better long-term retention and [[transfer]].

A certain amount of blocked practice is often necessary first. Students need basic declarative links in place before they can benefit from interleaving. Starting interleaving before the fundamentals are established can overwhelm [[working-memory]] and slow learning. As Daisy Christodoulou has warned, teachers can misuse interleaving by bombarding students with questions about concepts they do not yet understand.

Interleaving also helps build [[schemas]] by forcing students to discriminate between related concepts, creating mini-schemas that eventually merge into larger, more robust knowledge structures. The connection to [[transfer]] is direct: interleaving practice across domains strengthens the ability to apply chunks from one field to surprisingly different contexts. Interleaving itself appears to be a form of [[spaced-repetition]], which may partly explain its ability to help students retain information.

## Connections

- Builds on: [[chunking]] — interleaving develops the ability to select the right chunk for each situation
- Contrasts with: [[overlearning]] — overlearning deepens one pattern; interleaving develops flexibility across patterns
- Contrasts with: [[massed-practice]] — massed practice blocks by type, creating momentary strength but weak long-term retention
- Related to: [[varied-practice]] — interleaving mixes different skills; varied practice mixes conditions of the same skill
- Related to: [[discrimination-learning]] — interleaving's most powerful benefit: developing the ability to classify problems and select solutions
- Related to: [[einstellung]] — interleaving helps prevent Einstellung by training pattern flexibility
- Related to: [[transfer]] — interleaving strengthens the ability to apply knowledge across domains
- Related to: [[deliberate-practice]] — interleaving is a form of deliberate practice that focuses on selection, not just execution
- Related to: [[desirable-difficulty]] — interleaving feels harder and slower than blocked practice, which is precisely why it works
- Related to: [[procedural-learning-system]] — one of the best methods for building procedural knowledge through pattern discrimination
- Related to: [[schemas]] — interleaved practice helps construct schemas by forcing comparison between related concepts
- Related to: [[spaced-repetition]] — interleaving itself appears to be a form of spaced repetition

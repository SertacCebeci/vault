# Rule Learning

Rule learning is the habit of abstracting the underlying principle that explains a set of examples rather than memorizing the examples themselves. It matters because principles transfer: once a learner sees the rule that makes a problem type what it is, the learner can recognize new cases and choose an appropriate response even when the surface details change.

## Elaboration

Rule learners look across cases to infer what governs them. Example learners tend to remember the last or most similar case and then generalize from that memory, even when the new case differs in crucial ways. The practical result is that rule learners transfer more successfully to unfamiliar situations.

Classic convergence problems show the mechanism clearly. Students struggle to solve the castle-and-moat problem or the radiation-and-tumor problem when they treat each as an isolated story. When they compare the two and look for structural similarity, many notice the same principle: a large force cannot travel through one route safely, but smaller forces can converge on the target from multiple routes. Once the rule becomes visible, it can be applied to many other problems.

Rule learning is therefore closely connected to [[interleaving]] and [[discrimination-learning]]. Seeing differences and similarities across multiple cases helps learners move beyond surface resemblance. It is also a key ingredient in [[mental-models]], because a model becomes operational only when it contains the principles that tell you what to do, not just the memory of what happened last time.

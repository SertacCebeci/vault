# Direct Instruction

Direct instruction is a teacher-directed approach in which new content is broken into small, digestible segments, each followed by active practice with corrective feedback. It follows an "I do, we do, you do, extend it" progression that maps onto the [[learn-it-link-it]] framework: the teacher models (I do), guides practice (we do), releases responsibility to students (you do), and then students extend their learning through varied application (extend it). Direct instruction is not lecture — it embeds active learning throughout.

## Elaboration

The distinction between direct instruction and lecture is critical and often confused. In a lecture, the teacher talks for most of the period and assigns practice for homework. In direct instruction, presentation segments are short — interspersed with retrieval, partner discussion, guided practice, and formative assessment. The classroom alternates between brief presentation and active learning in a rhythm that keeps students cognitively engaged rather than passively listening.

The "I do" phase is where the teacher presents new concepts or skills with examples and demonstrations. The "we do" phase is guided practice — students work the material while the teacher circulates, offers corrective feedback, and reteaches where needed. Only when students demonstrate proficiency does the "you do" phase begin, where they practice independently. The "extend it" phase moves into student-directed exploration: inquiry, problem-based learning, and other approaches that deepen and broaden the neural links formed during the earlier phases.

Direct instruction is especially effective for [[biologically-primary-and-secondary-knowledge|biologically secondary material]] — the hard stuff that evolution did not prepare the brain to absorb on its own. The more secondary the material, the more students need the teacher's scaffolding before they can work independently. Student-directed approaches are valuable but should come *after* students have a foundation, not before. Introducing discovery learning too early overloads [[working-memory]], especially for [[race-car-and-hiker-brains|hiker-type learners]] with lesser WM capacity.

[[barak-rosenshine]]'s principles of effective instruction provide a checklist for high-quality direct instruction: begin with review of prior learning, present in small steps with practice after each, limit information load, ask many questions, provide guided practice with feedback, think aloud and model steps, and prepare students for independent practice.

The teacher-directed and student-directed approaches form a continuum, not a dichotomy. Teacher-directed instruction (learn it) prepares students for student-directed learning (link it). Countries that primarily use direct instruction show higher achievement scores, but the goal is independence — direct instruction builds the foundation for autonomous learning.

# Make It Stick: The Science of Successful Learning

## Summary
*Make It Stick* synthesizes over a decade of cognitive psychology research into a practical guide for how people actually learn and retain knowledge. Written by two leading memory researchers (Roediger and McDaniel, Washington University) and a narrative writer (Brown), the book's central argument is that the most effective learning strategies are deeply counterintuitive — and that the strategies most people rely on (re-reading, massed practice, cramming) create illusions of mastery without durable learning.

The book grew out of a 10-year collaboration funded by the James S. McDonnell Foundation's research grant "Applying Cognitive Psychology to Enhance Educational Practice." It uses storytelling — real cases from pilots, surgeons, athletes, and students — to illustrate principles grounded in controlled experiments. The book itself practices its own principles: key ideas are spaced and interleaved across chapters rather than treated in isolation.

The core evidence-based strategies advocated are retrieval practice, spaced practice, interleaving, elaboration, generation, and calibration of one's own knowledge. The book challenges popular beliefs including learning styles theory and the idea that effortful learning signals failure rather than growth.

## Key ideas

### Ch. 1: Learning Is Misunderstood (pp. 1-22)
- [[desirable-difficulty]] — Learning is deeper when it's effortful; easy learning creates illusions of mastery
- [[elaboration]] — Giving new material meaning by expressing it in your own words and connecting it to prior knowledge; no known ceiling
- [[mental-models]] — Interconnected webs of knowledge that enable expert performance under pressure
- [[recall-as-learning]] (strengthened) — Testing is a learning tool, not just a dipstick; Columbia IL study: A- on quizzed material vs C+ on reviewed-only
- [[illusions-of-competence]] (strengthened) — Fluency from rereading masquerades as mastery; poor metacognition means we don't know what we don't know
- Prior knowledge is necessary for higher-level skills (analysis, synthesis, creativity) — the creativity-vs-knowledge dichotomy is false

### Ch. 2: To Learn, Retrieve (pp. 23-45)
- [[testing-effect]] — The act of retrieval strengthens memory more than additional study; research from 1917 to present; crammers forgot 50% in 2 days vs 13% for retrieval-practicers
- [[reflection-as-retrieval]] — Reflection on experience is a compound learning activity: retrieval + elaboration + visualization + generation
- [[forgetting-curve]] — Ebbinghaus (1885): ~70% lost quickly without retrieval; retrieval "ties the knot" that holds memory
- [[generation-effect]] — Even minimal active production (fill-in-the-blank) strengthens memory vs. passive study; stronger when delayed
- [[feedback-timing]] — Delayed feedback produces better long-term retention than immediate; like training wheels, immediate feedback creates dependency
- [[transfer]] (strengthened) — Retrieval practice facilitates transfer to new contexts, not just rote recall
- Columbia IL study expanded: 8th grade science, 92% (A-) quizzed vs 79% (C+) non-quizzed, effect persisted 8 months
- Supply-format tests (essay, short-answer) more effective than recognition (multiple choice) — more cognitive effort = stronger retention
- Only 11% of college students use self-testing as a study strategy; most who do use it only for diagnosis, not as a learning tool itself

### Ch. 3: Mix Up Your Practice (pp. 46-66)
- [[massed-practice]] — The myth of focused, repetitive practice; "momentary strength" (visible acquisition gains) vs. "underlying habit strength" (durable retention); cramming as binge-and-purge
- [[varied-practice]] — Practicing the same skill under different conditions builds broader, more flexible schemas; beanbag study: never-practiced-target group outperformed target-only group
- [[interleaving]] (strengthened) — Geometric solids: 215% improvement on delayed test despite 60% vs. 89% during practice; painting attribution and bird classification studies show interleaving develops discrimination
- [[discrimination-learning]] — A major advantage of interleaving: developing the ability to classify problems and select correct solutions; connects to medical diagnosis and conceptual knowledge
- [[spaced-repetition]] (strengthened) — Surgical residents study: spaced microsurgery training dramatically outperformed single-day format; consolidation requires time and sleep; the effort of retrieval after some forgetting retriggerers consolidation
- [[desirable-difficulty]] (strengthened) — Scientists distinguish "momentary strength" from "habit strength"; techniques building habit strength slow visible acquisition, which is why massed practice feels superior
- [[leitner-box]] — Sebastian Leitner's file-card box system as practical implementation of spaced practice; the better your mastery, the less frequent the practice, but never disappear completely
- Blocked practice ≠ varied practice — moving through stations in a fixed order is still massed-like; true variation requires shuffling
- Douglas Larsen — Medical education: bird classification skills parallel diagnostic skills; structured reflection as integral training component; critique of passive conference/lecture format
- Vince Dooley — Football training cycle naturally embodies spacing, interleaving, variation, reflection, and mental rehearsal

### Ch. 4: Embrace Difficulties (pp. 67-101)
- [[encoding]] — First step of learning: converting perceptions into memory traces held in short-term memory
- [[consolidation]] — Second step: brain reorganizes and stabilizes traces into long-term memory; essay-writing analogy; prior knowledge as prerequisite; sleep's role
- [[reconsolidation]] — Retrieval makes learning pliable again, enabling updating and strengthening; the mechanism through which retrieval practice works
- [[retrieval-cues]] — The overlooked second task of learning: building cues to find stored knowledge; forgetting = lost cues, not lost knowledge; cue competition (Italian vs. French, military vs. smokejumping)
- [[desirable-difficulty]] (deepened) — Bjork's precise definition and the desirable/undesirable boundary; inverse relationship: easier retrieval = less benefit; three-step model (encoding → consolidation → retrieval) explains *why* difficulties work
- [[mental-models]] (deepened) — "Brain apps" formed through effortful practice; complex skills fuse into meaningful wholes; expert performance = vast library of mental models
- [[priming-for-learning]] — Struggling with a problem before being shown the solution deepens encoding; even wrong attempts create fertile ground for the answer
- [[generation-effect]] (deepened) — Even incorrect answers help, provided corrective feedback follows; Bonnie Blodgett as extended example of generative learning
- [[errorless-learning-myth]] — Debunking Skinner's "errorless learning"; errors + corrective feedback strengthen learning; fear of failure consumes working memory
- [[reflection-as-retrieval]] (deepened) — "Write to learn" study: 800+ students, half-letter-grade improvement on written-about concepts; robust at 2-month follow-up
- Cal Poly batting practice: interleaved (random) pitch practice dramatically outperformed massed (blocked) pitch practice in already-skilled hitters
- Elizabeth and Robert Bjork — coined "desirable difficulties"; defined the boundary between desirable and undesirable

### Ch. 5: Avoid Illusions of Knowing (pp. 102-130)
- [[metacognition]] - the master skill for learning: monitoring your own thinking; poor metacognition is the root cause of ineffective study, not lack of effort
- [[system-1-and-system-2]] - Kahneman's dual-process framework: automatic/intuitive (System 1) vs. controlled/analytical (System 2); System 1 is powerful but deeply susceptible to illusion; learning to override System 1 with System 2 is central to expertise
- [[memory-distortion]] - memory is reconstruction, not playback; distorted by narrative, suggestion, imagination inflation, interference, social influence; flashbulb memories (9/11) are vivid but drift substantially while confidence remains high
- [[narrative-bias]] — deep hunger for coherent explanatory stories; we impose causal order on ambiguous events and fill gaps with plausible-but-invented details; narrative and memory become one
- [[dunning-kruger-effect]] — incompetent people overestimate competence because they lack the skill to detect their deficiency; 12th-percentile students rated themselves at 68th; domain-specific training (not exposure to superior work) fixes it
- [[curse-of-knowledge]] — experts forget what it was like to not know; [[eric-mazur]]: "The better you know something, the more difficult it becomes to teach it"; knuckle-tapping experiment: 50% predicted vs. 2.5% actual
- [[calibration]] - the chapter's prescriptive section: testing as primary calibration tool, [[peer-instruction]], cumulative quizzing, corrective feedback, apprenticeship, teams, [[simulation-based-training]]; the cardinal rule: practice like you play
- [[illusions-of-competence]] (deepened) - fluency illusions, feeling of knowing, "the big lie" technique (familiarity mistaken for truth), flashbulb memory overconfidence; comprehensive taxonomy of how and why we misjudge what we know
- [[mental-models]] (deepened) - danger of model misapplication: familiar-looking problems that are actually different; experts sort by principles while novices sort by surface features; Ebersold brain tumor story
- Student-directed learning critique - students need instructor guidance because metacognition is too poor for fully autonomous study; even students who know retrieval practice is superior often fail to persist long enough

### Ch. 6: Get Beyond Learning Styles (pp. 131-161)
- [[learning-styles-myth]] - people may have preferences, but there is no credible evidence that matching instruction to a preferred modality improves learning; instruction should match the content instead
- [[dynamic-testing]] - Sternberg and Grigorenko's alternative to static labeling: test, focus on weaknesses, improve, and retest; low performance is a developmental signal, not a fixed identity
- [[structure-building]] - strong learners identify the main ideas in new material and organize them into a coherent framework; low structure-builders retain too much noise to form a usable model
- [[rule-learning]] - successful transfer depends on abstracting underlying principles rather than memorizing examples; comparing cases helps learners discover the rule
- [[mental-models]] (deepened) - Bruce Hendry's "winding stair" shows expertise as self-built templates that combine multiple rules into operational judgment
- [[calibration]] (deepened) - dynamic testing turns assessment into ongoing developmental feedback rather than a one-time verdict
- Not all difficulties are desirable - reading deficits such as dyslexia are real impediments unless met with support and specific training; effort helps only when it strengthens the right skill
- Intelligence is broader than static IQ - successful performance depends on analytical, creative, and practical intelligence, plus the opportunity to keep developing expertise

### Ch. 7: Increase Your Abilities (pp. 162-199)
- [[neuroplasticity]] - the brain remains modifiable through experience, practice, and recovery; learning changes circuits rather than merely revealing a fixed capacity
- [[growth-mindset]] - believing ability can grow changes how setbacks are interpreted and whether effort persists
- [[deliberate-practice]] (deepened) - expert performance comes from goal-directed, feedback-rich work beyond current ability, not from talent alone
- [[memory-techniques]] (deepened) - mnemonics are retrieval structures that organize already-learned material for recall under pressure, not substitutes for understanding
- [[working-memory]] (qualified) - commercial "brain training" claims are much weaker than claims about learning specific material or skills
- [[mental-models]] (deepened) - know-how emerges when facts, rules, and rehearsed sequences become an organized structure for action
- Environment matters alongside genes - nutrition, language-rich interaction, schooling, and opportunity all influence measured intellectual performance

### Ch. 8: Make It Stick (pp. 200-256)
- Consolidation chapter translating all prior research into actionable advice for students, lifelong learners, teachers, and trainers
- Three keystone student strategies: retrieval practice (self-quizzing replaces rereading), [[spaced-repetition|spaced practice]] (let forgetting happen, then reload from long-term memory), [[interleaving|interleaved practice]] (mix problem types to build discrimination)
- Additional strategies with specific guidance: [[elaboration]] (find metaphors, relate to prior knowledge), [[generation-effect|generation]] (attempt problems before instruction), [[reflection-as-retrieval|reflection]] (what went well, what could improve), [[calibration]] (use objective tests, not feelings of knowing), [[memory-techniques|mnemonic devices]] (retrieval organizers for already-mastered material)
- [[high-structure-teaching]] — Wenderoth's high-structure biology classes (daily exercises, growth mindset, Bloom's taxonomy calibration) significantly reduced failure rates and closed achievement gaps vs. traditional lecture format
- [[thayer-method]] — West Point's instructional system embedding daily quizzing, pre-class generation, board recitation, and peer instruction into every class; effective across wide ability ranges
- [[low-stakes-classroom-quizzing]] (deepened) — McDermott's implementation: four-item quiz in last 3-5 minutes, cumulative scope, 20% of grade, clear ground rules; Columbia IL expanded to high school
- The chapter's central message: the principles are the same across all settings — what differs is the scaffolding needed to get people to persist with techniques that feel less productive than the ones they naturally prefer

## Notable claims
- Rereading is the #1 study strategy of 80%+ of college students, yet multiple readings in close succession provide negligible benefit over a single reading (Washington University, 2008)
- Endel Tulving's 1960s experiments: reading paired items 6 times produced no recall advantage over seeing items for the first time
- Every time you learn something new, you change the brain - intellectual ability is not hardwired
- The Pashler/McDaniel/Rohrer/Bjork review found virtually no rigorous evidence supporting the core "meshing" claim of learning styles, and several studies contradict it
- Effective instruction should match the subject matter being taught, not a learner's preferred modality
- Static tests reveal current performance, not a learner's future potential for developing expertise
- Average IQ in the United States has risen 18 points over the last sixty years (the Flynn effect), implying that ability reflects environmental change as well as inheritance
- Nutritional support, preschool, and language-rich interaction can raise measured IQ in early childhood, especially in low-resource settings
- The influential 2008 Swiss working-memory study behind many brain-training claims was small, narrowly sampled, and had not replicated by the time of the book's publication
- Expert performance is highly domain-specific - training one skill does not automatically produce general intellectual gains elsewhere

## Questions raised
- ~~How does the "generation effect" (trying to solve before being taught) relate to the desirable difficulty framework?~~ → Answered in Ch. 4: generation is a form of desirable difficulty; see [[priming-for-learning]]
- ~~What is the boundary between "desirable" and "undesirable" difficulty?~~ → Answered in Ch. 4: Bjork defines desirable difficulties as those triggering encoding/retrieval processes; undesirable if learner lacks background to respond; see [[desirable-difficulty]]
- ~~How do the learning styles theory and individual differences interact?~~ → Answered in Ch. 6: learning-style preferences exist, but the matching hypothesis lacks evidence; differences that matter include reading fluency, [[structure-building]], and [[rule-learning]]
- How do individual differences (intelligence, learning disabilities, personality) interact with desirable difficulty strategies?
- How does the three-step model (encoding → consolidation → retrieval) map onto the neuroscience framework from [[learning-how-to-learn]]?
- Is there an overarching rule that determines which kinds of impediments make learning stronger? The authors note this is still an open research question.
- Are [[structure-building]] and [[rule-learning]] teachable skills, stable differences, or some combination of both?
- How should formal education incorporate [[dynamic-testing]] without losing the administrative simplicity of static grading systems?
- How durable are growth-mindset interventions once they leave a motivated classroom or workshop setting?
- To what extent can [[deliberate-practice]] be self-directed without a coach or other reliable external feedback?
- When do mnemonic systems deepen retrieval of conceptual knowledge, and when do they encourage shallow memorization instead?

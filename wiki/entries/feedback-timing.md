# Feedback Timing

Counterintuitively, delayed feedback after a test often produces better long-term learning than immediate feedback. While immediate feedback feels more helpful and produces better performance on the spot, slightly delayed corrective feedback — given after the learner has had time to sit with their answer — creates more durable retention. This is another instance of [[desirable-difficulty]]: the extra cognitive effort involved in revisiting and correcting a now-fading response strengthens the corrected memory.

## Elaboration

The evidence comes from multiple lines of research. In one study, students read science passages and either took an open-book test (continuous immediate feedback) or a closed-book test followed by delayed feedback (seeing the passage afterward to check their answers). The open-book group performed best on the immediate test, but the delayed-feedback group retained the learning better on a later test.

In motor learning, the pattern is even clearer. Immediate feedback functions like training wheels on a bicycle — the learner quickly comes to depend on the continued presence of the correction rather than developing their own internal sense of correct performance. Delayed feedback forces the learner to develop an internal model of what "right" feels like, which is more robust when the feedback is eventually removed (as it always is in real-world performance).

One theory for motor skills is that when immediate feedback becomes part of the practiced task, its absence in real-world settings creates a gap in the learned pattern that disrupts performance. Another perspective is that frequent interruptions for feedback prevent the learner from establishing a stabilized performance pattern.

The practical takeaway is nuanced: feedback is essential (it corrects errors and prevents mislearned material from solidifying), but its timing matters. For long-term retention, a brief delay between the test and the feedback is beneficial — it spaces out the practice and requires more effort to process the correction.

# Brain Development Timeline

The human brain develops in a back-to-front wave of maturation. In early infancy, neurons migrate to their final positions like migratory birds, extending axons to find target neurons. A dense nest of intertwined axons and dendrites called a *neuropil* forms, producing a surge of new synaptic connections that peaks around age two. Then pruning begins — axons are snipped away, decreasing synaptic density as the brain matures through childhood and adolescence. The last region to mature is the prefrontal cortex, responsible for planning and judgment.

## Elaboration

The developmental trajectory has two phases with distinct educational implications. The growth phase (birth to roughly age two) is a period of exuberant connection-building. The environment matters profoundly during this phase: a healthy, varied environment preserves more neural connections, while a restricted or stressful environment causes excessive pruning — like plants with their foliage sheared off.

The maturation phase (childhood through adolescence) involves progressive pruning and myelination, moving from the visual and auditory areas at the back of the brain toward the prefrontal cortex at the front. This back-to-front pattern explains why middle and high school students can sometimes behave in surprisingly immature ways — their planning and judgment circuitry is literally the last to come online.

Crucially, the brain's ability to form and prune connections does not stop at maturity. New synaptic connections and pruning continue throughout life, which is the basis of lifelong [[neuroplasticity]]. However, the ease and speed of connection-building changes with age, which interacts with the distinction between [[biologically-primary-and-secondary-knowledge|primary and secondary learning]]: primary abilities (language, face recognition) exploit the exuberant connection-building of early childhood, while secondary abilities (reading, math) require the deliberate repurposing of circuits that maturation has already shaped.

# Overlearning

Overlearning is continuing to study or practice after you've mastered material within the same session. It has a place — it can produce automaticity important for high-stakes performance (tennis serves, piano concertos, public speaking) — but in regular learning it can be a waste of valuable time and can create [[illusions-of-competence]].

## Elaboration

Once you've grasped the basic idea during a session, continuing to hammer away at it doesn't strengthen the kinds of [[long-term-memory]] connections you want. It's like learning carpentry by only practicing with a hammer. The subsequent study session is the right time to revisit and deepen a pattern through [[spaced-repetition]], not the current one.

The main danger of overlearning is that repeating easy material feels productive (it's easy and fluent) while actually creating the illusion that you've mastered the full range of material. This is where [[deliberate-practice]] differs: it targets what you find *difficult*, not what you've already mastered. The balance is: use [[interleaving]] to mix different approaches within a session, focus deliberate practice on weaknesses, and let spaced repetition handle the long-term strengthening.

Overlearning can be valuable for specific automaticity needs — expert speakers practice the order of 70 hours for a 20-minute TED Talk. But for most learning, the better strategy is to move on once you've got the basics and return later.

## Connections

- Contrasts with: [[deliberate-practice]] — overlearning repeats what's easy; deliberate practice targets what's hard
- Related to: [[illusions-of-competence]] — overlearning the easy stuff creates the illusion of mastery
- Related to: [[spaced-repetition]] — better to repeat in future sessions than to overlearn in the current one
- Related to: [[interleaving]] — interleaving different material within a session prevents overlearning on one type

# Katina's Working Memory Trap

**Concepts illustrated:** [[illusions-of-competence]], [[working-memory]], [[learn-it-link-it]], [[recall-as-learning]], [[worked-example-effect]]

Katina begins to tear up looking at her exam score — she barely passed. She maintains that she understands the material fine when she is at home or in class, but freezes on tests. She wonders if she suffers from test anxiety, or maybe she just cannot do math. Her mother says she is "just like her — bad at math."

By all appearances Katina is a good student. Testing has ruled out reading or math disorders. She does her homework, finishes craft projects, has many friends. The problem is not effort, intelligence, or ability.

When Katina studies algebra at home, she quickly looks through the chapter and finds the example problems clear, so she skips straight to the homework. She solves problems by following the example with her finger on the worked solution while writing her answers. When a homework problem does not match any example, she reshapes it to fit. The night before a test she rereads her class notes. At no point in this process does she work a problem independently, without looking at the solution.

Her classmate Jared shows an identical pattern with Spanish vocabulary. He looks at the word list and thinks he knows it — *it's right there in front of him!* When completing homework exercises, he fills in the blanks by looking back at the examples. Done? Time to knock off.

The diagnosis: both students are using [[working-memory]] as their sole study tool. The information feels solid while it is being actively juggled — Katina genuinely follows the algebra logic, Jared genuinely recognizes the Spanish words. But none of it has been transferred to [[long-term-memory]] through [[recall-as-learning|retrieval practice]]. They have completed the "learn it" phase of [[learn-it-link-it]] but never entered the "link it" phase. On test day, when the working-memory crutch is removed, there is nothing in long-term memory to draw on.

The chapter makes an important compassionate observation: in all probability, no one has ever taught Katina or Jared how to study effectively. They are doing as well as they can, given what little they know about how their brains work. The problem is systemic, not personal.

Katina's situation is not rare. Perhaps a third of a typical class has boxed themselves in with an "I can't" mentality in one subject or another. The teacher worries about standardized test scores, school averages drop, morale drops. The fix is not more studying of the same kind — it is a fundamentally different study approach centered on [[recall-as-learning|retrieval practice]] and independent problem-solving.

## Why it matters

This vignette reframes a pervasive classroom problem — students who seem to understand but fail tests — from a character or ability issue into a memory-system issue. It shows teachers that the student is not lazy, anxious, or unintelligent; the student has simply never been taught to move material from working memory to long-term memory. The "test anxiety" label is often a misdiagnosis: what the student experiences is the panic of reaching into long-term memory and finding it empty. The fix is teaching retrieval practice explicitly, not prescribing more of the same ineffective study habits.

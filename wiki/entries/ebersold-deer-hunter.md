# Ebersold's Deer Hunter Surgery

**Concepts illustrated:** [[reflection-as-retrieval]], [[mental-models]], [[testing-effect]]

Late in 2011, neurosurgeon Mike Ebersold was called to a Wisconsin emergency room to examine a deer hunter found unconscious in a cornfield with blood at the back of his head. The injury turned out to be a gunshot wound — a 12-gauge shotgun slug fired from a distance, fracturing the skull and lodging about an inch into the brain.

Ebersold knew from experience that beneath the wound lay the venous sinus, a large vein draining the brain cavity, and that it was very likely torn. He ran through a mental checklist: arrange blood transfusion, review surgical steps A through D, set up the operating room, brief the team on what to expect. In the OR, he worked deliberately around the edges of the wound, freeing bone and tissue so he would be ready for what could go wrong when he pulled the bullet.

When he removed the bullet and bone fragments, the vein tore open and blood poured out at a rate of hundreds of cubic centimeters per minute. At that point, his thinking shifted from deliberate reasoning to reflex. He fell back on a technique he had invented himself through years of reflective practice — cutting small muscle plugs from the patient's own tissue and stitching them into the torn vein ends. It was a solution "you won't find written anywhere," developed through nightly reflection on surgical difficulties, mentally rehearsing improvements, and testing adjusted techniques the next day until the maneuver became automatic.

The patient survived, leaving the hospital a week later with only some lost peripheral vision.

## Why it matters

This case demonstrates that expert performance under extreme pressure depends on a foundation of [[reflection-as-retrieval]] — Ebersold's nightly habit of mentally replaying surgeries and generating improvements created the very technique that saved this man's life. It also shows how [[mental-models]] work in practice: the surgeon moved fluidly between deliberate checklist thinking and reflexive action, deploying knowledge that had been consolidated through thousands of retrieval cycles. His own words capture the core lesson: "Unless you keep recalling this maneuver, it will not become a reflex."

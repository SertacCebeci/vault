# Dopamine and Learning

Dopamine is a neurochemical that plays a dual role in learning: it makes experiences feel rewarding and it directly strengthens the synaptic connections that constitute new learning. When an unexpected reward arrives, dopamine surges into brain areas where new neural links are forming, strengthening the connections that were developing before, during, and after the reward. This makes dopamine uniquely powerful — it does not merely motivate, it physically facilitates the rewiring that learning requires.

## Elaboration

The distinction between expected and unexpected rewards is critical. An *unexpected* reward triggers a dopamine surge that bathes synapses across learning-related brain regions, making neural connections stronger and easier to form. An *expected* reward — one the brain has already predicted — produces little or no additional dopamine, because the brain is already correctly modeling its environment. This means expected rewards can drive motivation (the anticipation of reward keeps students working) but do not directly enhance the neural rewiring process.

A further effect of dopamine is that it temporarily suspends the competitive [[declarative-procedural-seesaw]]. Normally the declarative and procedural learning systems inhibit each other, but a dopamine surge allows both systems to work together simultaneously, amplifying learning.

Dopamine also produces a dramatic improvement in [[working-memory]] capacity. This means that when students experience positive surprise — unexpected praise, a novel activity, a spontaneous game — they temporarily become better at holding and manipulating information, not just happier.

The flip side is equally important: a sudden *drop* in dopamine (from expected reward being withdrawn, or from negative surprise like being yelled at) signals neurons to *disconnect* rather than connect. This is why punitive reactions to student mistakes can actively impair learning — the dopamine crash does not merely make students feel bad, it undermines the neural consolidation that was in progress.

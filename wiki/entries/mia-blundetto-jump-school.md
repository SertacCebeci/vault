# Mia Blundetto's Jump School Training

**Concepts illustrated:** [[desirable-difficulty]], [[encoding]], [[consolidation]], [[reconsolidation]], [[interleaving]], [[varied-practice]], [[spaced-repetition]]

Mia Blundetto, a twenty-three-year-old first lieutenant in the U.S. Marine Corps, was terrified of falling. She wouldn't even go down a water slide until middle school. But when billeted to logistics in Okinawa, she had to earn her place commanding an air delivery platoon — one of the most sought-after billets — by completing army jump school at Fort Benning, Georgia.

Jump school is a model of learning through desirable difficulty. You are not allowed to carry a notebook or take notes. You listen, watch, rehearse, and execute. Testing is the principal instructional medium, and the test is in the doing. The difficulty escalates systematically:

First, you stand in a gravel pit learning the parachute landing fall (PLF) — falling along different planes of the body in six possible directions. Then you jump from a two-foot platform. Then a zip line a dozen feet up, where the instructor drops you the last few feet at random. Then a thirty-four-foot tower practicing mass exits and equipment malfunctions. Finally, in week three, you jump for real from a C130 troop transport at 1,250 feet.

The training is spaced and interleaved by design — as you wait your turn at staging areas, platforms, and harness mechanisms, time elapses and skills interleave naturally. On Mia's third jump, she plummeted onto another soldier's inflated parachute moments after exiting the aircraft. Thanks to her training, she handled it calmly — "just sort of swam out" — an incident that amounted to nothing precisely because the escalating, varied, spaced practice had built the reflexive knowledge she needed.

## Why it matters

This story is the chapter's anchor example because jump school embodies *every* principle of desirable difficulty simultaneously: spacing between practice sessions, interleaving of different skills, escalating variation in conditions, testing as the primary learning method, and the impossibility of relying on notes or passive review. It also demonstrates that confidence is not about *feeling* confident — it's about having demonstrated mastery through repeated performance under increasingly realistic conditions.

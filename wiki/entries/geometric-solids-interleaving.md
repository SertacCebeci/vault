# The Geometric Solids Interleaving Study

**Concepts illustrated:** [[interleaving]], [[massed-practice]], [[desirable-difficulty]], [[discrimination-learning]]

Two groups of college students learned to calculate the volumes of four obscure geometric solids (wedge, spheroid, spherical cone, and half cone). One group worked practice problems clustered by type — all wedge problems, then all spheroid problems, and so on (massed). The other group worked the same problems in a mixed, interleaved sequence.

During practice, the massed group appeared to learn far better: they averaged 89 percent correct, compared to only 60 percent for the interleaved group. But on the final test a week later, the results flipped dramatically. The massed group averaged only 20 percent correct, while the interleaved group averaged 63 percent — a 215 percent improvement in test performance. The strategy that *impeded* practice performance *boosted* final test performance by more than three times.

## Why it matters

This study captures the central paradox of [[desirable-difficulty]]: the techniques that make practice feel harder and less successful are the ones that produce the most durable and transferable learning. The 89% vs. 60% during practice makes massed practice look overwhelmingly superior — which is exactly why students and teachers prefer it. But the 20% vs. 63% on the delayed test reveals the truth. The interleaved group had to practice the critical [[discrimination-learning|discrimination]] step — identifying which formula to use — on every single problem, while the massed group never had to think about it during practice and were helpless when they encountered it on the test.

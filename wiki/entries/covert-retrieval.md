# Covert Retrieval

Covert retrieval is retrieval practice performed silently, in one's head, without writing or speaking the answer. The finding — from Pyc, Agarwal, and Roediger (2014) — is that **covert retrieval produces essentially the same retention gains as overt retrieval**. Subjects who studied a map and then silently recalled its features did as well on a later reproduction test as subjects who wrote or spoke the features aloud.

## Why it matters

The practical teaching implication is large: when an instructor throws a question to the class, it is easy to worry that only the hand-raisers are benefiting — a handful of vocal students practicing retrieval on behalf of the silent majority. The covert-retrieval finding says this worry is misplaced, **provided the instructor leaves a genuine pause** before taking an answer. Every student who used that pause to think has done real retrieval work; only the students who checked out entirely missed the benefit.

This has knock-on consequences for how to run oral Q&A in class:

- **Pause.** Several seconds of silence is not awkward, it is the work happening. Calling on the first hand shortcuts retrieval for everyone else.
- **Don't short-circuit the question.** Once the answer is out, the window for covert retrieval has closed.
- **Use it to scale retrieval cheaply.** A single well-posed oral question with a long enough pause is retrieval practice for the entire room.

Covert retrieval doesn't make written retrieval superfluous. Writing forces the learner to commit to one specific answer, which tends to surface gaps that silent thinking glosses over — and written retrieval leaves a paper trail the instructor can respond to. But when time is tight or the exercise is informal, silent retrieval is not a consolation prize; it is most of the benefit.

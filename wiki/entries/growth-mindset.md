# Growth Mindset

A growth mindset is the conviction that ability can be developed through effort, strategy, and feedback rather than treated as a fixed trait. This belief changes how people interpret difficulty: setbacks become information about what to try next instead of verdicts on what kind of person they are.

## Elaboration

The core difference is not optimism versus pessimism but interpretation. When people assume intelligence is fixed, they are more likely to choose safe tasks that protect the image of being smart, avoid situations that expose weakness, and treat failure as proof of deficiency. When they assume ability can grow, they are more likely to take on stretch challenges, revise strategy after mistakes, and persist through frustration because the effort itself is part of development.

This mindset is not a license for vague encouragement. Productive growth depends on accurate feedback, better strategies, and repeated effort directed at something learnable. In that sense, a growth mindset works best when paired with [[calibration]], [[dynamic-testing]], and [[deliberate-practice]]. Its psychological power comes from being aligned with [[neuroplasticity]]: effortful learning can genuinely change what a person is capable of doing.

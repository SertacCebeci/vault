# Veronique Mintz on Collaborative Learning Dysfunction

**Concepts illustrated:** [[collaborative-learning]], [[socioemotional-learning]]

Thirteen-year-old Veronique Mintz wrote in *The New York Times* about her school's heavy emphasis on collaborative learning: approximately 80 percent of work was done in teacher-assigned groups of three to five students. The result, she reported, was that students who wanted to complete their assignments were forced into the position of having to discipline peers who would not behave and coax reluctant group members into contributing.

## Why it matters
Veronique's account is a vivid counterpoint to idealized visions of group work. It shows what happens when collaborative learning is implemented without adequate structure — students end up managing social dynamics they are not equipped to handle, turning the learning experience into an exercise in frustration. The chapter uses this as a springboard to argue that well-designed collaborative learning (with roles, behavioral expectations, and collaboration clinics) can transform this experience into one that builds self-control, patience, social problem-solving, and emotional intelligence.

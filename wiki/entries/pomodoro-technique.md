# Pomodoro Technique

The Pomodoro Technique is a time-management method invented by Francesco Cirillo in the early 1980s that combats [[procrastination]] by breaking work into focused intervals (typically 25 minutes) followed by short relaxation breaks. Named after the tomato-shaped kitchen timer Cirillo used, the technique works because it leverages both [[focused-and-diffuse-modes]]: concentrated work followed by a relaxed break that allows diffuse-mode processing and hippocampal offloading.

## Elaboration

The Pomodoro is the primary tool recommended against [[procrastination]]. Its power lies in the [[process-vs-product]] distinction: instead of focusing on finishing the homework (product, which triggers pain in the insular cortex), you focus on spending 25 minutes working (process, which is painless). Most people can focus for 25 minutes. The key insight is that the reward at the end is not optional — it trains the brain's dopamine system to associate focused work with pleasure, gradually rewiring the procrastination habit. It's like "an intense 25-minute workout at a mental gym" — brief enough to be tolerable, structured enough to be effective.

The relaxation break is not wasted time — it is when diffuse-mode processing and hippocampal offloading occur. If students use break time to grab their phones and check social media, that focused intrusion can overwrite what they just loaded into their hippocampus, like new passengers crowding onto a subway car and pushing others off at the wrong stop. The best breaks involve genuinely restful activities: closing eyes, walking, stretching, drawing, or listening to music.

The technique is adaptable for classroom use. Teachers can model it by having students work silently for a timed period, then enjoy a short relaxation break. For younger students, the interval can be adjusted to the child's age plus one minute — so a nine-year-old might do a ten-minute Pomodoro. Gamification apps like Forest can make the process more engaging. Teachers can also apply the technique to their own work — such as grading stacks of essays — using the same 25-minute focused sessions with breaks between piles.

# WWII Essay Intermediate-Task Schedule

**Concepts illustrated:** [[intermediate-task-scaffolding]], [[procrastination]]

A teacher wraps up an intense week on major battles of World War II and assigns a two-week essay: choose two events that influenced the outcome of the war and analyze them. The assignment is deliberately open-ended. But when the essays come in, the teacher is dismayed — students wrote generalities, repeated random fragments, and produced "rushed, haphazard fluff."

The problem is procrastination. Most students thought "I've got plenty of time," then waited until the night before to scramble. The teacher redesigns the assignment with a detailed intermediate-task schedule: thesis statement (March 6), reference citations (March 7), outline with think-aloud modeling (March 10), introduction draft (March 12), body paragraphs for each event (March 14, 17), summary (March 19), final copy (March 21). Each stage includes both a specific deliverable and a partner-based collaborative component.

The transformation is striking. Not only do students stop procrastinating, but the staged process produces dramatically better essays. The teacher has modeled a lifelong skill: breaking large, intimidating projects into manageable daily tasks.

## Why it matters
This scenario demonstrates that student procrastination is often a design problem, not a character flaw. When teachers scaffold the process rather than just assigning the product, they remove the conditions that trigger procrastination while simultaneously improving output quality.

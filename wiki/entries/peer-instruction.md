# Peer Instruction

Peer instruction is a classroom teaching method developed by physicist [[eric-mazur]] at Harvard that replaces passive lecturing with cycles of conceptual testing and small-group discussion. Students read assigned material before class; in class, the lecture is interspersed with quick conceptual questions. Students grapple individually for a minute or two, then discuss in small groups to reach a consensus answer. The process is designed to reveal misunderstandings, engage students in deep conceptual reasoning, and leverage the fact that peers often explain better than experts.

## How it works

The method incorporates multiple evidence-based learning principles simultaneously:

- **[[recall-as-learning]] / [[testing-effect]]**: The conceptual questions force retrieval, which strengthens memory and reveals gaps.
- **[[calibration]]**: Students and instructors both get immediate feedback on comprehension. The instructor sees which concepts need more attention; students discover what they don't actually understand.
- **[[curse-of-knowledge]] bypass**: Mazur pairs students who initially gave different answers, so they must articulate their reasoning and convince each other. A student who just learned a concept is often better at explaining it than a professor for whom it's been automatic for decades — the recently-learned student can still access both sides of the confusion.
- **[[elaboration]]**: Explaining your reasoning in your own words to a peer is a powerful form of elaboration.
- **Corrective feedback**: Peers provide immediate feedback, and the eventual class discussion corrects remaining errors.

The process also serves as a gauge for the instructor: if most students get a question right, the class can move on; if most get it wrong, the concept needs more instruction.

## Concepts applied
- [[calibration]] — the primary function; exposes the gap between perceived and actual understanding
- [[curse-of-knowledge]] — addresses the expert-novice gap by leveraging peer explanation
- [[recall-as-learning]] — conceptual questions force active retrieval
- [[testing-effect]] — frequent low-stakes testing strengthens learning
- [[elaboration]] — peer explanation requires restating ideas in your own words

# Reflection as Retrieval

Reflection — the habit of mentally reviewing an experience after it happens — is a powerful form of retrieval practice. When you go home after a challenging day and think through what happened, what you could do differently, and how you might approach it next time, you are retrieving knowledge from memory, connecting it to new experience, and mentally rehearsing future performance. This is not idle rumination; it is a compound learning activity that combines [[recall-as-learning]], [[elaboration]], and visualization.

## Elaboration

Neurosurgeon Mike Ebersold describes this process vividly. After difficult surgeries, he would go home and replay the procedure in his mind — how a suturing went, whether a bigger or smaller bite with the needle would have worked better, whether stitches should be closer together. The next day he would try the adjusted technique and observe the result. Through this cycle of experience, reflection, mental rehearsal, and adjusted practice, he developed novel surgical techniques that became reflexive maneuvers — techniques he could depend on when a patient was losing blood at two hundred cubic centimeters per minute.

The key insight is that reflection is not passive. It involves multiple cognitive activities that each independently strengthen learning:

1. **Retrieval** — pulling the experience back from memory, which triggers the [[testing-effect]]
2. **Elaboration** — connecting the new experience to existing knowledge and prior training
3. **Visualization** — mentally rehearsing alternative approaches or refined techniques
4. **Generation** — generating new strategies or modifications before testing them (the [[generation-effect]])

This makes reflection a naturally occurring form of [[desirable-difficulty]] — it requires effort, and that effort produces durable learning. Ebersold's emphasis on drilling procedures until they become reflex echoes the relationship between retrieval practice and automaticity: "Unless you keep recalling this maneuver, it will not become a reflex."

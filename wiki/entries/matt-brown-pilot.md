# Matt Brown, the Pilot

**Concepts illustrated:** [[mental-models]], [[recall-as-learning]], [[desirable-difficulty]]

Matt Brown was flying a twin-engine Cessna at eleven thousand feet when his right engine lost oil pressure. Alone, at night, carrying freight — he had to decide in real time whether to shut down the engine, how to compensate for asymmetric thrust, and where to land. He succeeded because he had *ready knowledge*: tolerances memorized, procedures drilled, judgment honed through years of practice. He didn't need to look anything up — the knowledge was available from memory when it mattered.

Later, when Matt transitioned to business jets, his training shifted from "fire hose" PowerPoint lectures (7 days of passive absorption, 10 hours/day) to simulator-based active practice (11 days of retrieval, interleaving, and varied scenarios). The simulator training was where real learning happened — spaced, interleaved, and involving the same mental processes he'd use at altitude.

## Why it matters

This is the book's central definition of learning — acquiring knowledge and skills and having them readily available from memory so you can make sense of future problems and opportunities.

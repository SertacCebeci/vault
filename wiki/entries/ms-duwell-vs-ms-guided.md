# Ms. Duwell vs. Ms. Guided

**Concepts illustrated:** [[classroom-procedures]], [[procedural-learning-system]]

The bell rings for third period. In Ms. Duwell's classroom, students sit down and begin writing in their notebooks — a bell ringer, a short retrieval task written on the board. Every group of four has a basket of materials at the center of their collaborative workspace, pre-stocked by Ms. Duwell with plot maps and color-coded Post-it Notes for today's lesson on short stories. The class runs like clockwork.

Across the hall, Ms. Guided's students straggle in. A line forms at the pencil sharpener. One boy knocks another's book off a desk. Two students ask if they're doing anything today. Half the class needs to go to their lockers for notebooks. Ms. Guided is defeated before the lesson begins.

Both teachers are sincere and hardworking. The difference is procedural: Ms. Duwell has taught her students routines — entering the room, starting the bell ringer, accessing materials — and practiced them until they became automatic. Her students' [[procedural-learning-system]] handles the logistics, freeing their [[working-memory]] for learning. Ms. Guided's students must figure out each transition consciously, consuming working memory and creating chaos.

## Why it matters
This opening vignette crystallizes the chapter's thesis: classroom management is not about personality or authority, it is about whether everyday routines have been practiced to the point of procedural automaticity. The same neuroscience that explains how students learn math or reading also explains how they learn to enter a classroom productively.

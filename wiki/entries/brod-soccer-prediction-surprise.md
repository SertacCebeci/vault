# Brod Soccer Prediction and Surprise Study

**Concepts illustrated:** [[prediction-as-learning]]

Researchers (Brod, 2021) asked subjects to predict the correct scores of soccer games, then revealed the actual scores and later tested their memory. A control group was shown the correct scores directly and asked to explain whether those scores matched their expectations — a retrospective reflection rather than an active prediction. The students who made actual predictions beforehand had significantly better memory for the soccer scores than those who only reflected after the fact.

The critical finding was the role of emotional investment. The researchers measured pupil dilation as answers were revealed and found that subjects who had committed to specific predictions showed greater pupil dilation — a physiological marker of surprise — when seeing the results. This emotional engagement was the mechanism: making a prediction creates a personal stake in the outcome, and the surprise or confirmation that follows drives deeper encoding. Random guessing, by contrast, does not produce this effect because the guesser has no emotional investment in the answer.

## Why it matters
This study reveals *why* prediction works at the neurological level: it is not merely a cognitive exercise but an emotional one. Committing to a prediction creates investment; discovering the result triggers surprise; that emotional charge strengthens memory consolidation. This has practical implications — instructors should give students time to genuinely think through and commit to their predictions rather than rushing through the exercise superficially.

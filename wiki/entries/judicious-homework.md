# Judicious Homework

Judicious homework is the practice of assigning short, frequent, well-designed homework that incorporates [[interleaving]] and [[spaced-repetition]] rather than lengthy, repetitive blocked-practice assignments. The quantity is deliberately limited — seven problems instead of forty — but the composition is carefully designed to activate both [[declarative-learning-system|declarative]] and [[procedural-learning-system|procedural]] learning systems.

## How it works

A well-designed homework set might include two problems from today's lesson (building initial declarative links), three problems from the previous lesson (spaced repetition), and two problems from the distant past (longer-interval spacing). This interleaved, spaced structure forces students to discriminate between problem types and strengthens procedural pattern recognition, rather than allowing them to coast through repetitive blocked practice on autopilot.

Biologically secondary material like math, reading, and writing benefits most from homework practice because it requires dramatic neural rewiring. Short daily practice sessions scheduled days apart are more effective than longer sessions — consistent with the principles of [[spaced-repetition]] and [[consolidation]].

Key guidelines include: assign homework at the end of class (not the beginning, to avoid distraction); make it count toward the grade by a small amount; have students begin during the final minutes of class; plan to use key homework ideas the next day; and never use homework as punishment. Parents should allow productive struggle rather than hovering, as this develops [[metacognition|metacognitive]] skills.

## Concepts applied

- [[procedural-learning-system]] — homework provides the repeated practice needed to build procedural links
- [[interleaving]] — mixing problem types from different lessons and time periods
- [[spaced-repetition]] — including problems from progressively more distant past lessons
- [[desirable-difficulty]] — short, challenging, interleaved sets are harder but more effective than long blocked sets
- [[declarative-learning-system]] — starter problems on the current concept reinforce declarative links

# Neuroscience

## Lead

The brain-level implementation layer of learning — synaptic plasticity, hippocampal indexing, neocortical consolidation, neuromodulators (dopamine, acetylcholine, BDNF), the role of sleep and exercise — and how these mechanisms ground the regularities studied in cognitive learning theory.

## Scope

This domain covers brain mechanisms that underlie learning. Core topics:

- Neurons, synapses, and Hebbian plasticity (LTP/LTD).
- Hippocampus as indexer; neocortex as long-term store; the systems-consolidation model.
- The declarative vs. procedural learning systems and their distinct neural pathways.
- Neuromodulators relevant to learning — dopamine (prediction error and reinforcement), acetylcholine (attention and encoding gain), BDNF (plasticity and exercise).
- Sleep stages and consolidation (slow-wave sleep, REM).
- Exercise and learning (BDNF upregulation, hippocampal volume).
- Default-mode and focused-mode networks.
- Neuronal recycling — how reading, math, and other cultural skills repurpose evolutionarily older circuits.

## Out of scope

- Cognitive descriptions that do not reference neural mechanism (route to [[learning-theory]]).
- Clinical neurology / psychiatry — disease, treatment, dysfunction. (May enter the vault later as a separate domain if needed.)
- Philosophy of mind, consciousness, free-will debates.

## Canonical questions

- What is the precise mechanism by which sleep consolidates declarative memory, and how do its effects differ across stages?
- How do dopamine signals during practice modulate the strength of newly formed traces?
- What is the evidence for hippocampal-replay-mediated systems consolidation in humans (vs. rodents)?
- How does aerobic exercise's effect on BDNF translate into measurable learning gains, and over what time scales?
- To what extent are individual differences in learning explained by stable neural-architecture differences vs. modifiable strategies?

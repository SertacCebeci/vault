# Massed Practice

Massed practice is the concentrated, repetitive drilling of a single skill or topic in a single session — what coaches call "practice-practice-practice" and students call "cramming." It is the dominant approach to learning across classrooms, athletic training, and professional development, and it is deeply misguided. The visible improvement during massed practice is real but misleading: scientists distinguish this **momentary strength** (performance during acquisition) from **underlying habit strength** (durable, long-term retention). The techniques that build habit strength — [[spaced-repetition|spacing]], [[interleaving]], and [[varied-practice|variation]] — actively impede momentary strength, which is why massed practice *feels* superior even as it produces inferior long-term results.

## Elaboration

The faith in massed practice rests on a powerful illusion: you can *see* it working. Each repetition brings visible improvement during the session, and that visible progress reinforces belief in the method. But this improvement is largely a short-term phenomenon. Research consistently shows that gains from massed practice fade rapidly — cramming has been likened to binge-and-purge eating, where a lot goes in but most comes right back out.

The mechanism behind the failure is consolidation. Embedding new learning in [[long-term-memory]] requires a process in which memory traces are strengthened, given meaning, and connected to prior knowledge — a process that unfolds over hours and days and benefits from [[sleep-and-learning|sleep]]. Rapid-fire massed practice leans on short-term memory and never gives consolidation time to work. The increased effort required to retrieve learning after a delay — when some forgetting has set in — actually retriggerers consolidation, further strengthening the memory.

Massed practice is pervasive: summer language boot camps, single-weekend professional seminars, textbook chapters that cluster all problems of one type together. Even in studies where spaced or interleaved groups objectively outperform massed groups on final tests, participants often *still believe* they learned better from massed practice. This makes the myth exceptionally hard to dislodge.

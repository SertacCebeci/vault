# Declarative-Procedural Seesaw

The [[declarative-learning-system]] and [[procedural-learning-system]] operate like a seesaw: when one system is actively engaged in learning, the other is relatively de-emphasized. But once both systems have learned the same material, the resulting knowledge is complementary — each way of knowing enhances overall understanding and flexibility with the material.

## Elaboration

The seesaw metaphor captures a key constraint on learning. When a teacher provides step-by-step explanations, students learn through the declarative system. When students practice independently — applying, drilling, making mistakes — they activate the procedural system. Emphasizing one form of instruction necessarily de-emphasizes the other in the moment.

This is why good teaching alternates between the two. The "I do" and "We do" phases of [[direct-instruction]] engage the declarative system through explicit explanation. The "You do" phase and extended practice engage the procedural system through repetition and pattern formation. Students who learn through only one system end up with knowledge that is either flexible but slow (declarative only) or fast but inflexible (procedural only). Learning through both systems is like having two legs instead of one — not only can you stand more stably, you can move forward more easily.

The seesaw has important implications for assessment. Students who have learned primarily through the procedural system may demonstrate competence through correct application while struggling to articulate explanations. Students who have learned primarily declaratively may give excellent verbal explanations while failing to apply knowledge fluently. Neither mode of demonstration alone proves or disproves genuine understanding. See [[verbal-explanation-vs-understanding]].

However, the seesaw is not absolute. [[Dopamine-and-learning]] reveals that unexpected rewards trigger dopamine surges that temporarily *halt* the competitive inhibition, allowing the declarative and procedural systems to work together simultaneously. This cooperative mode amplifies learning, because both pathways can strengthen their links at the same time rather than taking turns. Positive, surprising classroom moments — unexpected praise, a novel activity, a spontaneous reward — create windows of cooperative dual-system learning that are neurologically more productive than either system working alone.

The flip side is equally consequential: a sudden dopamine *crash* (from a punitive response or the withdrawal of an expected reward) intensifies the competitive inhibition, actively undermining the learning process for both systems.

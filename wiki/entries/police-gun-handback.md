# Police Gun Handback

**Concepts illustrated:** [[calibration]], [[mental-models]]

Police officers are trained to disarm assailants at close quarters — a maneuver requiring speed and deftness: striking the assailant's wrist with one hand to break his grip while simultaneously wresting the gun free with the other. In training, officers practiced this move by role-playing with a fellow officer. The training protocol had them hone the maneuver through repetition: take the gun, hand it back, take it again, hand it back.

Until one day, an officer on an actual call successfully disarmed a real assailant — and then, to their mutual astonishment, *handed the gun right back*. The deeply ingrained training sequence (take-hand back-take-hand back) had been practiced so thoroughly that "hand it back" had become part of the [[mental-models|mental model]]. The officer managed to re-seize the gun, but the incident revealed a critical flaw in the training design.

The training regime had violated the cardinal rule: **practice like you play, because you will play like you practice.** The repetitive handback was a training convenience that bore no resemblance to real-world use, yet it became wired into the officer's automatic response — a System 1 pattern that fired in the field exactly as trained.

## Why it matters
This is a cautionary tale about [[simulation-based-training]] design and [[calibration]]. Training that doesn't match real-world conditions doesn't just fail to prepare you — it can actively create dangerous habits. The same principle applies to academic learning: study methods that don't resemble the conditions under which you'll use the knowledge (e.g., studying with the book open when you'll be tested with it closed) create a false [[calibration]] and build the wrong mental models.

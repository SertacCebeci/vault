# Forgetting Curve

The forgetting curve, first plotted by Hermann Ebbinghaus in 1885, describes how rapidly newly learned information slips away without reinforcement. In very short order, we lose roughly 70% of what we've just heard or read. After that initial steep drop, forgetting slows and the remaining 30% or so falls away more gradually. The central challenge of improving how we learn is finding ways to interrupt this process of forgetting.

## Elaboration

The forgetting curve makes vivid why passive strategies like rereading are so ineffective: even a single reading creates a temporary sense of familiarity (an [[illusions-of-competence|illusion of competence]]), but without active intervention the material simply drains away. The metaphor in *Make It Stick* is apt — memory is like a string of cranberries hung on a thread; without a knot (retrieval), the cranberries slip off the other end as fast as you string them.

The curve also explains *why* [[spaced-repetition]] works so well. Each act of retrieval interrupts the forgetting process and resets the curve at a higher baseline. With each subsequent retrieval — especially when spaced to allow some forgetting to occur — the curve flattens further and the memory becomes more resistant to decay. This is the mechanism behind the 1939 Iowa study: once a student took a test on material, forgetting nearly stopped.

The interaction between the forgetting curve and the [[testing-effect]] is what makes spaced retrieval practice the most evidence-backed study strategy: retrieval ties the knot, spacing lets the mortar dry, and each cycle lifts the forgetting curve's floor.

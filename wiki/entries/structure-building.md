# Structure Building

Structure building is the ability to extract the central ideas from new material and organize them into a coherent framework that can support further learning. Learners who do this well separate signal from noise, fit details into an emerging scaffold, and remember the material as an organized whole rather than as a heap of disconnected points.

## Elaboration

Structure building appears to be one of the learning differences that genuinely matters. High structure-builders are better at deciding what is foundational and what is merely incidental. Low structure-builders often hold on to too many details at once, including irrelevant ones, and end up without a workable representation of the material.

A useful analogy is a Lego village. Learning a new subject is like building the town pictured on the box: the streets and major buildings have to be recognized first, and later details deepen those structures. Information that does not fit may be discarded as noise, or occasionally incorporated as a useful substructure. This is why structure building sits very close to [[mental-models]]: it is one of the core skills by which mental models are assembled.

Structure building may also be teachable. When texts include embedded questions that highlight the main ideas, low structure-builders improve substantially. That observation connects the concept to [[reflection-as-retrieval]] and [[elaboration]]: pausing to ask what the big ideas are, how they connect, and what belongs where may itself be a way of learning to build stronger structures.

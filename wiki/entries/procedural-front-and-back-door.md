# Procedural Front Door and Back Door

The [[procedural-learning-system]] has two distinct entry points. The **front door** handles goal-directed activities — conscious intentions that flow from [[working-memory]] in the prefrontal cortex through the basal ganglia. The **back door** handles habitual, unthinking actions — sensory inputs from the back of the brain that flow directly through the basal ganglia to produce conditioned responses.

## Elaboration

The front-door pathway is engaged when you deliberately choose to perform a practiced action. When you tell yourself to flow through a yoga sun salutation, the intention travels from working memory into the basal ganglia, is processed, and returns to the cortex as a smooth sequence of movements. The goal-directed system also handles fluent language production — your procedural system can produce "potato soup" in English without conscious deliberation, or "krumpli leves" in Hungarian if that language is well-practiced.

The back-door pathway links sensory inputs directly to motor outputs without involving conscious awareness. Riding a bike, writing letters, shrugging in response to a question — these are conditioned responses where sensory information enters from visual, auditory, or somatosensory areas and flows through the rear of the basal ganglia back to the cortex. The switching between these two modes is visible when a well-practiced speaker gets distracted: they shift from the smooth habitual back-door pathway to the clumsy goal-directed front-door pathway, stumbling to find their place.

The interaction between the two doors is bidirectional and complex. The conscious goals of the declarative system can be driven non-consciously by the basal ganglia's procedural value function — a system built over years of experience that helps maximize future rewards. When asked why they made a particular decision, people often confabulate a declarative narrative that has little to do with the procedural value function that actually drove the choice.

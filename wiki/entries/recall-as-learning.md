# Recall as Learning

Actively retrieving information from memory — closing the book and trying to remember what you just read — is one of the most powerful learning techniques available. From a neuroscience perspective, retrieval practice pulls out dendritic spines and encourages them to build and strengthen connections with axons, directly building the neural links in [[long-term-memory]]. It builds [[chunking|chunks]], deepens understanding far more than passive re-reading, and simultaneously reveals [[illusions-of-competence]] by exposing what you actually know versus what you only think you know.

## Elaboration

The neuroscience of retrieval practice maps onto the [[declarative-learning-system]]. When a student retrieves information, [[working-memory]] calls for a song the [[hippocampus-as-index|hippocampus]] and neocortex have been learning. If the neocortical links are still weak, the hippocampus chimes in — reminding the neocortex where all the parts of the song are located so she can sing more boldly. Each retrieval strengthens the neocortical connections. Eventually, after enough practice, the neocortex can sing the song on its own — the hippocampal index becomes unnecessary. This is the hallmark of genuine mastery.

Jeff Karpicke's research, published in Science, showed that students who practiced recall learned far more and at a deeper level than those who used concept mapping while reading or who simply re-read the material. Even the students themselves predicted that re-reading would be more effective, showing how powerful the illusion is. Karpicke and cognitive scientist Phillip Grimaldi articulated the centrality of retrieval: all situations in which knowledge is expressed — answering questions, explaining concepts, making inferences, applying knowledge, producing creative ideas — involve retrieval. Learners always draw upon the past in the service of the present.

Testing is itself an extraordinarily powerful learning experience. Comparing one hour of studying versus one hour of test-taking on the same material shows that the hour of testing produces significantly more retention and learning. The evidence against mere repetition goes deep. Endel Tulving's experiments in the mid-1960s showed that people who read paired items six times performed no better on recall than people encountering items for the first time. Subsequent research on rereading texts confirmed that multiple readings in close succession provide no benefit over a single reading.

The most striking classroom evidence: in a Columbia, Illinois middle school 8th grade science class, material that was quizzed with feedback at three points during the semester was recalled at an **A-** level on a test a month later, while material that was reviewed three times but never quizzed came in at **C+**. Same students, same class — the only difference was whether retrieval was practiced.

Retrieval practice is far from mindless memorization — it also builds conceptual understanding. But students often need to be explicitly taught to use it. The technique is counterintuitive: looking away from the material and struggling to recall the key idea feels harder than rereading, which flows easily through [[working-memory]]. That effort is precisely what builds the neural links. Repeated practice in a variety of contexts assists not only with strengthening existing neural connections but also with broadening them to other sets of neurons.

Frequent formative assessment checks — low-stakes tests, homework, and exercises — keep students retrieving all along the way. Plentiful retrieval practice speeds the [[consolidation]] of links in the neocortex and more rapidly frees the hippocampus for new learning. As Pooja Agarwal and Patrice Bain note in *Powerful Teaching*, the focus must shift from getting information *into* students' heads to getting information *out* — retrieval practice is more potent than lecturing, re-reading, or taking notes.

A practical tip: recall material in different physical locations. When you learn in one room, subliminal environmental cues get encoded alongside the material. Recalling in varied locations makes the learning independent of those cues.

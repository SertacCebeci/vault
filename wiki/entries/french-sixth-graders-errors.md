# French Sixth Graders and the Value of Errors

**Concepts illustrated:** [[errorless-learning-myth]], [[desirable-difficulty]], [[working-memory]]

Sixth graders in France were given very difficult anagram problems that none of them could solve. After struggling unsuccessfully, half the kids received a ten-minute lesson teaching them that difficulty is a crucial part of learning, errors are natural and to be expected, and practice helps — just as in learning to ride a bicycle. The other half were simply asked how they had gone about trying to solve the anagrams (a neutral debrief with no reframing of errors).

Both groups then took a difficult test that measured working memory. The children who had been taught that errors are a natural part of learning showed significantly better use of working memory than the control group. They did not expend their working memory capacity agonizing over the difficulty of the task. The theory was further tested in variations of the original study, and the results consistently supported the finding that difficulty can create feelings of incompetence that engender anxiety, which in turn disrupts learning — and that "students do better when given room to struggle with difficulty."

The study received wide publicity in France and inspired a "Festival of Errors" at an elite Paris graduate school, aimed at teaching schoolchildren that making mistakes is a constructive part of learning — not a sign of failure but of effort.

## Why it matters

This study demonstrates that the *framing* of difficulty matters as much as the difficulty itself. Anxiety while taking a test appears to be an undesirable difficulty — it consumes working memory rather than strengthening learning. But when students understand that struggle and errors are expected parts of the learning process, the anxiety diminishes and working memory is freed for productive use. The practical implication: teaching learners *about* desirable difficulty is itself an intervention that improves learning.

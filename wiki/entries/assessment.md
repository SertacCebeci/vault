# Assessment

## Lead

Measurement of learning — what tests detect, what they distort, when feedback helps or harms, and the central learning-science finding that **the act of being tested itself produces learning** (the testing effect). Covers both formative assessment (low-stakes, integrated into instruction) and summative assessment (high-stakes, end-of-period evaluation).

## Scope

This domain covers:

- The testing effect — retrieval as memory operation that strengthens what is retrieved.
- Formative assessment as instruction — exit tickets, low-stakes quizzing, dynamic testing.
- Feedback design — timing, granularity, error-specific vs. correct-answer-only.
- Calibration — alignment between learner confidence and learner accuracy; metacognitive accuracy.
- Test design — surface-level vs. transfer questions, generative vs. recognition formats.
- The Dunning-Kruger pattern — how poor metacognition distorts self-assessment.
- Predictive validity of common assessment instruments.

## Out of scope

- The psychometric foundations of test theory (item response theory, factor analysis as discipline) — covered only as needed to explain assessment claims.
- The act of teaching itself — assessment-as-instruction is the boundary; route lessons-and-courses to [[pedagogy]].
- Learner self-monitoring as a stable trait — that's [[self-regulation]]; assessment treats it as something to be measured.

## Canonical questions

- What is the size of the testing effect across formats and populations, and what conditions maximize it?
- When does corrective feedback help vs. harm? Specifically, when does delayed feedback outperform immediate?
- How well-calibrated are typical learners after a course, and what assessment formats improve calibration as a side-effect?
- What predicts transfer to novel contexts: assessment scores at end-of-course, learning trajectory across the course, or some other signal?

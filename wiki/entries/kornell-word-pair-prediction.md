# Kornell Word-Pair Prediction Study

**Concepts illustrated:** [[prediction-as-learning]], [[generation-effect]]

Researchers at UCLA (Kornell, Jenson, Hayes, and Bjork, 2009) asked subjects to memorize loosely connected word pairs like *whale–mammal*. One group studied each pair for 13 seconds. The other group saw only the first word for 8 seconds, made a prediction about the second word, then saw the full pair for just 5 seconds. Since the pairs were only loosely connected, predictions were almost always wrong — seeing *whale*, a subject might guess *sea* or *ocean* rather than *mammal*.

Despite having less total study time and making mostly incorrect guesses, the prediction group performed dramatically better on a subsequent recall test: 67% accuracy versus 55% for the passive study group. The few seconds spent generating an incorrect prediction were "remarkably, more effective than was spending the same time studying the answer to be recalled later."

## Why it matters
This study cleanly isolates the prediction effect: wrong answers, less study time, yet better retention. It demonstrates that the cognitive act of generating a prediction — activating prior knowledge, committing to an answer — creates a stronger memory trace than passive exposure, even when the prediction fails. This is one of the foundational findings supporting [[prediction-as-learning]] as a classroom strategy.

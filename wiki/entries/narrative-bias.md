# Narrative Bias

Narrative bias is our deep, largely unconscious compulsion to impose coherent stories on ambiguous, arbitrary, or incomplete information. We are driven by discomfort with ambiguity to construct explanatory narratives — fitting events into causal chains, filling gaps with plausible-but-invented details, and gravitating toward stories that best explain our emotions. This narrative hunger shapes both how we remember the past and how we interpret the present, making it a fundamental driver of [[memory-distortion]].

## Elaboration

The power of narrative bias is demonstrated even in trivial contexts. In one study, participants exposed to one side of a phone conversation were more distracted than those who heard both sides — because overhearing half a conversation triggers an irresistible urge to infer the missing half and construct a complete narrative. We strive to fit the events of our lives into a cohesive story: why nobody in my family attended college, why my father never made a fortune, why I'd never want to work for a corporation. We gravitate toward the narratives that best explain our emotions, and in this way, narrative and memory become one.

For learning, narrative bias has a dual nature. On one hand, organizing material into meaningful narratives aids memory — the memories we organize meaningfully are better remembered. Narrative provides a mental framework for imbuing future experiences with meaning. On the other hand, this same mechanism causes us to "remember" details that were implied but never stated, to construct confident-but-false accounts of events, and to resist evidence that contradicts our established story. The psychologists Larry Jacoby, Bob Bjork, and Colleen Kelley found that it is "nearly impossible to avoid basing one's judgments on subjective experience."

The implication for learners: your subjective sense of what happened, what you learned, and how well you understand is always filtered through narrative — and narratives serve emotional coherence, not accuracy.

# Elaboration

Elaboration is the process of giving new material meaning by expressing it in your own words and connecting it to what you already know. Unlike mechanical repetition, which quickly hits the limits of what you can retain, elaboration has no known ceiling — the more you can explain how your new learning relates to prior knowledge, the stronger your grasp becomes and the more connections you create for later retrieval.

## Elaboration

Elaboration works because it forces you to go beyond surface-level encoding. When you relate the principle of heat transfer to the feeling of warming your hands around a cup of cocoa (conduction), the sun pooling in a room (radiation), or the blast of A/C (convection), you're building multiple retrieval routes to the same concept. Each personal connection is another hook that the memory can hang on.

Putting new knowledge into a *larger context* deepens the effect further. The more of the unfolding story of history you know, the more new facts you can absorb. Connecting an abstraction — like angular momentum — to something concrete you already understand (a figure skater pulling in her arms) grounds the learning and makes it stick.

Elaboration is distinct from simple repetition in a critical way: repetition tries to strengthen one pathway through brute force, while elaboration builds a web of pathways. This is why there's no known limit to how much you can learn through elaboration — each new connection creates new routes for retrieval, and new material has more existing knowledge to attach to.

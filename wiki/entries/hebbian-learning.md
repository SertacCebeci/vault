# Hebbian Learning

When a certain set of neurons fires together repeatedly, the connections between them strengthen — they become like a well-practiced choir whose members learn to sing in unison. This principle, often summarized as "neurons that fire together near-simultaneously in time wire together," was first described by Canadian psychologist Donald Hebb and forms the neurobiological foundation of how learning physically occurs in the brain.

## Elaboration

At the cellular level, learning begins when a dendritic spine on one neuron reaches close enough to the axon of a neighboring neuron for a signal to jump across the synapse between them. That signal, passing from neuron to neuron, is what forms thoughts — it is the foundation of learning. When neurons repeatedly co-activate, the synapse between them becomes more efficient: dendritic spines grow, connections stabilize, and signal transmission becomes faster and more reliable.

The sheer scale of neural connection-making is staggering. The brain has roughly 86 billion neurons and around 10^14 (one hundred trillion) synapses. Even learning something relatively simple — a foreign vocabulary word, how to solve 5 x 5 — involves clumps of neurons making dozens, hundreds of thousands, sometimes even millions of connections. Getting a dendritic spine to pop out and make good contact with a neighboring axon is genuinely hard work, and it does not happen in just one place — neurons must connect across multiple sites.

The process is not all-or-nothing. Initial co-activation produces weak, fragile connections (the "learn it" phase of [[learn-it-link-it]]). Repeated co-activation through practice, especially [[recall-as-learning|retrieval practice]], strengthens these connections into durable long-term links. The process is also specific: the neurons that strengthen are the ones being used, which is why [[deliberate-practice]] in a specific domain builds domain-specific expertise rather than general intelligence.

Hebbian learning is the mechanism that underlies [[neuroplasticity]] — the brain's capacity to reorganize itself through experience. It explains why [[spaced-repetition]] works (each retrieval reactivates and strengthens the relevant neural ensemble) and why passive re-reading does not (merely perceiving information without active neural co-firing produces minimal synaptic change).

# The Painting Attribution Interleaving Study

**Concepts illustrated:** [[interleaving]], [[discrimination-learning]], [[massed-practice]]

Researchers expected that massed practice — studying many examples of one painter's works before moving on to the next — would best help students learn to identify each artist's defining style. The logic seemed sound: concentrated exposure to one artist should make their unique characteristics salient. Interleaving, by contrast, seemed likely to be confusing.

The researchers were wrong. Students who studied paintings interleaved across multiple artists outperformed the massed group at matching artworks to their painters on a later test. More strikingly, the interleaving group was also better able to correctly attribute *new* paintings they had never seen during the learning phase. Massed study helped students recognize *commonalities* within one artist's work, but interleaved study helped them perceive the *differences* between artists — and it was these differences that proved more useful for identification.

Despite these results, the students who participated in the experiments persisted in preferring massed practice, convinced it served them better. Even after taking the test and seeing their own performance, they clung to their belief that concentrated viewing of one artist at a time was the superior strategy.

## Why it matters

This study demonstrates two things at once. First, that [[interleaving]] develops [[discrimination-learning|discrimination skills]] — the ability to perceive distinguishing features across categories — which is more powerful than learning to recognize within-category similarities. Second, that the [[illusions-of-competence|illusion of competence]] from massed practice is so strong that learners will deny their own test results rather than abandon their faith in it. The myth of massed practice is hard to exorcise even when you experience the evidence yourself.

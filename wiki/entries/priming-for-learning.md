# Priming for Learning

When you're asked to struggle with a problem *before* being shown how to solve it, the subsequent solution is better learned and more durably remembered. The act of wrestling with the problem — even unsuccessfully — activates related prior knowledge, creates awareness of gaps, and prepares fertile ground for the answer when it arrives. This is a specific application of the [[generation-effect]] and [[desirable-difficulty]]: the difficulty of attempting before instruction primes the mind for deeper encoding.

## Elaboration

The mechanism has two parts. First, as you cast about for a solution, you retrieve related knowledge from memory, strengthening the retrieval routes to that knowledge even before the answer is provided. Second, when you do receive the answer, connections are made to the related material that is fresh in your mind from the effort. Unsuccessful attempts to solve a problem encourage deep processing of the answer when it is later supplied, creating fertile ground for its encoding in a way that simply reading the answer cannot match.

This is why it's better to solve a problem than to memorize a solution. And it's better to attempt a solution and supply the incorrect answer than not to make the attempt at all — provided corrective feedback follows. Even wrong answers prime the mind, because the search process itself activates relevant knowledge networks.

The knot-tying example illustrates this concretely: watching a Boy Scout demonstrate knots in a city park produces weak encoding. But when you've bought a fishing boat and need to attach an anchor line — when you have a real problem demanding a solution — you're far more likely to learn and remember the bowline knot. The need creates the receptivity.

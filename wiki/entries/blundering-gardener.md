# Bonnie Blodgett: The Blundering Gardener

**Concepts illustrated:** [[generation-effect]], [[priming-for-learning]], [[reflection-as-retrieval]], [[elaboration]]

Bonnie Blodgett is a Minnesota writer and self-taught ornamental gardener who came to gardening around middle age with no formal training — just a burning desire to get her hands dirty making beautiful spaces on the corner lot of her St. Paul Victorian home. Her "learning style" might be called leap-before-you-look-because-if-you-look-first-you-probably-won't-like-what-you-see. She publishes garden writing under the name "The Blundering Gardener," a way of telling her voices of doubt to take a hike.

"Blundering means that you get going on your project before you have figured out how to do it in the proper way, before you know what you're getting into. For me, the risk of knowing what you're getting into is that it becomes an overwhelming obstacle to getting started."

Through sustained trial and error, Bonnie backed into complex mastery. She started planting things without knowing Latin nomenclature or plant classification, learning instead from what worked, what failed, and what surprised her. Gradually and despite her inclination to resist, she came to grasp the classical taxonomy of ornamental plants and to appreciate how Linnaeus's schema frames family connections and communicates attributes. The Latin names, she discovered, are actually helpful — "tardiva" connects to "tardy" (a late bloomer), "procumbus" means prostrate, crawling on the ground. Prior knowledge from her high school Latin, French, and English began to reawaken.

By writing about her gardening experiences in her *Garden Letter*, Bonnie engages two potent learning processes beyond the act of gardening itself: she is retrieving the details and story of what she has discovered ([[reflection-as-retrieval]]), and she is elaborating by explaining the experience to her readers ([[elaboration]]).

## Why it matters

Bonnie's story is the chapter's primary example of generative learning in action. She embodies the principle that struggling with a problem — rather than being spoonfed a solution — makes for strong learning. Her sustained commitment to trial-and-error effort led to complex mastery and deep knowledge of plant interrelationships, composting, irrigation, and garden design. The caveat is important: in some settings (like jumping out of airplanes), blundering is not the optimal learning strategy. But where the stakes allow it, getting started before you have it all figured out is itself a desirable difficulty.

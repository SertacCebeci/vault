# Whip-Around

A whip-around is a rapid whole-class retrieval activity where every student responds to an open-ended question in sequence, one at a time, without interruption. It provides universal [[recall-as-learning|retrieval practice]] in under four minutes for a class of thirty, ensures all students participate (not just the confident "race cars"), and reinforces learning through hearing peers' varied responses.

## How it works

The technique has three steps. First, the teacher poses an open-ended question that can elicit a variety of responses — not simple yes/no answers. Second, students get a minute to formulate their answer mentally or in writing, signaling readiness with a thumbs-up. Third, students respond one by one in order (up and down rows, or a list of names), with no stopping for corrections or commentary. If the question has absolute right/wrong answers, the teacher gives an immediate nonverbal thumbs-up or thumbs-down. Corrections are addressed after everyone has responded.

To make the whip-around more challenging, students can be asked to avoid repeating a previous answer — forcing [[generation-effect|generation]] of unique responses.

The minute of preparation before the whip-around provides a [[brain-breaks|brain break]] for hippocampal offloading. The retrieval act itself strengthens [[long-term-memory]] links in the neocortex. Hearing others' responses adds connections and reinforces the material through varied perspectives.

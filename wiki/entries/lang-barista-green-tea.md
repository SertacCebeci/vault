# The Barista Who Learned an Order in One Trial

**Concept illustrated:** [[testing-effect]]

James Lang spent six months ordering the same medium green tea, hot, no honey or lemon, from the same young barista — who every single day asked him the same two clarifying questions, as if seeing him for the first time. She was *receiving* the information from him hundreds of times but never pulling it from her own memory.

One day Lang walked in feeling mischievous and, when asked "Can I help you?", replied "Can you guess?" She blanked, apologized, he reminded her of the order — medium green tea, hot, nothing in it — and a few days later when he walked in she pointed at him and said: "Medium green tea, hot, no honey or lemon."

## Why it matters

One retrieval attempt — even a failed one, followed immediately by the answer — achieved what hundreds of passive exposures had not. The story compresses the entire empirical literature on the testing effect into one vignette: memory is shaped by *what the learner pulls out*, not by how many times information is fed in. Lang uses it as the opening of [[small-teaching]] Ch. 2 for good reason — it previews the gap between **exposure** (rereading notes, listening to lectures) and **retrieval** (a small quiz, a question at the door) that every subsequent model in the chapter exploits.

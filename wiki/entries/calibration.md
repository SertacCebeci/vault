# Calibration

Calibration is the practice of aligning your subjective sense of what you know with objective reality — replacing the "cockpit window" of intuition with the "flight instruments" of evidence-based self-assessment. Because human [[metacognition]] is systematically biased by [[illusions-of-competence]], [[narrative-bias]], the [[dunning-kruger-effect]], and other [[memory-distortion]] mechanisms, learners need external tools and habits to keep their judgment squared with reality.

## Elaboration

Calibration is the practical answer to a central learning problem: if we can't trust our subjective sense of what we know, what can we trust? A useful toolkit includes the following methods:

1. **Testing and retrieval practice** — The single most important calibration tool. Frequent self-testing reveals what you actually know vs. what you think you know. Cumulative quizzing is especially powerful because it consolidates learning across units and reveals decay. [[low-stakes-classroom-quizzing]] serves this function in formal education.

2. **Peer instruction** — [[eric-mazur]]'s model: students grapple with conceptual questions, then discuss in small groups to reach consensus. This reveals misunderstandings to both students and instructors, and leverages the fact that peers can diagnose confusion better than experts (who suffer the [[curse-of-knowledge]]).

3. **Corrective feedback** - From instructors, peers, mentors, or the environment. [[david-dunning]] argues that the path to self-insight runs through other people. In medical practice, morbidity/mortality conferences serve this function. In apprenticeship models, working alongside experienced practitioners provides continuous calibration.

4. **Dynamic testing** - [[dynamic-testing]] treats assessment as a recurring loop: test, discover the weak spots, improve, and retest. This turns calibration from a snapshot into a growth process.

5. **Attention to cues** - Learning to distinguish reliable indicators of learning (ease of retrieval after a delay, ability to explain in your own words) from unreliable ones (familiarity, fluency, ease of retrieval immediately after study).

6. **Simulation** - Training that replicates real-world conditions and demands, exposing gaps between perceived and actual competence. [[simulation-based-training]] in police, medical, and military contexts serves this purpose.

7. **Mistakes in the field** - The most powerful but most costly feedback mechanism. Real consequences calibrate judgment like nothing else - provided you survive the mistake and are receptive to the lesson.

The cardinal rule connecting all these tools: practice like you play, because you will play like you practice.

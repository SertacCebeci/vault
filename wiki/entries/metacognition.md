# Metacognition

Metacognition is the act of monitoring your own thinking — literally "thinking about thinking" (from Greek *meta*, "about"). It is the skill of accurately judging what you know and don't know, how well you're performing, and whether your current learning strategies are working. Metacognition is the master skill that governs all self-directed learning: without it, you cannot allocate effort wisely, choose effective strategies, or recognize when you've gone astray.

## Elaboration

The paradox of metacognition is that the people who need it most are the least likely to have it. The [[dunning-kruger-effect]] shows that incompetent performers systematically overestimate their abilities because they lack the very skill needed to recognize their deficiency. This creates a vicious cycle: poor metacognition leads to poor strategy choices, which leads to poor learning, which reinforces the illusion that everything is fine.

Metacognition operates through both [[system-1-and-system-2]] thinking. System 1 provides quick, intuitive assessments ("I know this" or "this feels easy"), but these snap judgments are deeply susceptible to [[illusions-of-competence]] — fluency from rereading, familiarity mistaken for knowledge, and the [[curse-of-knowledge]] that blinds experts to their own expertise. Accurate metacognition requires engaging System 2: deliberately checking your intuitive judgments against objective evidence, like a pilot scanning cockpit instruments rather than trusting the seat of their pants.

The practical implication is that learners need external [[calibration]] tools — testing, feedback, peer review, simulation — to anchor their self-assessments to reality. Left to subjective judgment alone, humans are reliably misled by their own cognition.

# Learn It, Link It

Learning is a two-phase process of forming and then strengthening neural connections. In the *learn it* phase, neurons begin to find each other and make initial contact — a dendritic spine on one neuron reaches toward the axon of a neighboring neuron and a signal starts jumping across the synapse between them. In the *link it* phase, those fragile initial connections solidify through practice and then extend through varied application, building an increasingly rich neural network that ties into related concepts.

## Elaboration

The framework is grounded in [[hebbian-learning]] and maps onto a four-stage neural progression. In the first stage, neurons begin to find one another as a student is introduced to a new concept — through a teacher's explanation, reading a textbook, or watching a video. In the second stage, connections are made as the student follows along and practices the material. These two stages constitute the *learn it* phase — initial links form in the neocortex, but they are fragile.

In the third stage, as the student works actively with the material, links solidify in [[long-term-memory]] and form the basis of proficiency. In the fourth stage, still more practice in novel and challenging contexts extends the network further, allowing neurons to tie in with other neurons that underpin related concepts. These last two stages constitute the *link it* phase — the neural entanglement grows broader, symbolized by larger, richer sets of interconnected neurons.

The *learn it* phase corresponds to the initial deposit of information by [[working-memory]] into the [[declarative-learning-system]]: the hippocampus receives indexing links and the neocortex receives scattered initial connections. At this stage, the student can follow along in class and feels they understand — the material is active in working memory — but the long-term links are fragile and may never survive without further work.

The *link it* phase is where [[active-learning-declarative|active learning]] operates. Through [[recall-as-learning|retrieval practice]], collaborative exercises like [[think-pair-share]], and varied application, students reinforce and extend the neural links deposited during learn-it. Group work can facilitate the link-it phase, but it is not the only way. Any activity that forces the student to pull information from long-term memory — solo retrieval, [[whip-around|whip-arounds]], problem-solving — drives linking. The critical requirement is cognitive engagement, not physical activity.

The framework also maps directly onto [[direct-instruction]]'s "I do, we do, you do, extend it" stages. The *learn it* phase encompasses "I do" (teacher models) and "We do" (guided practice), while the *link it* phase encompasses "You do" (independent practice) and "Extend it" (student-directed exploration). Teacher-directed instruction and student-directed learning are not opposing approaches but complementary phases of the same neural process. The more [[biologically-primary-and-secondary-knowledge|biologically secondary]] the material, the more students need coaching during the learn-it phase before they can link it independently.

The framework also clarifies why [[worked-example-effect|worked examples]] are valuable but insufficient on their own. Studying examples helps form initial mental templates (the learn-it phase), but students must also practice independently — without looking at the solution — to build durable long-term links (the link-it phase).

# Pedagogy

## Lead

Teaching practice and instructional design — how educators structure courses, lessons, classroom routines, and feedback to convert evidence about learning into effective experiences for actual learners. Covers both the high-leverage micro-moves (retrieval-practice exit tickets, deliberate questioning) and the larger architecture (curriculum sequencing, course design, formative assessment loops).

## Scope

This domain covers the teacher-side practice of designing and running learning experiences:

- Course design and curriculum sequencing.
- Classroom routines and procedures.
- Direct instruction, scaffolding, worked examples.
- Active learning techniques: collaborative learning, discussion, problem-based learning.
- Cognitive Load Theory in instructional design.
- Formative assessment as instruction (not only evaluation).
- Frameworks: Rosenshine's principles, the gradual-release model, Bloom's taxonomy.
- The applied translations of [[learning-theory]] findings into classroom practice (small teaching, retrieval-practice integration).

## Out of scope

- The cognitive-mechanism *why* of why a strategy works (route to [[learning-theory]]).
- Brain-mechanism evidence (route to [[neuroscience]]).
- High-stakes assessment design — psychometrics, test validity (route to [[assessment]]).
- Habit, motivation, procrastination, learner self-management (route to [[self-regulation]]).

## Canonical questions

- Which strategies translate from controlled studies of learning to messy classrooms with high reliability?
- How should an instructor balance time spent on material exposure vs. retrieval practice in a fixed-time course?
- What is the role of struggle vs. scaffolding for novices vs. experts (the "expertise reversal" effect)?
- How does feedback timing and granularity affect long-term learning, and what's the right cost-benefit?
- When does collaborative learning produce gains over individual practice, and when is it active-learning theater?

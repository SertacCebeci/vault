# Columbia, IL Middle School Science Class

**Concept illustrated:** [[testing-effect]]

In 2006, Pooja Agarwal (then with Henry Roediger's Memory Lab at Washington University in St. Louis) partnered with sixth-grade social-studies teacher Patrice Bain at a middle school in Columbia, Illinois, to test whether retrieval practice would move the needle in a real classroom. A year later the team extended the work into 8th-grade science at the same school. Course material — standard middle-school curriculum covering world civilizations and then physical science — was divided into three arms:

1. **Quizzed**: three ungraded retrieval quizzes per topic — one after homework reading, one after class discussion, one before the major exam. Students saw correct answers after each quiz; the teacher left the room during quizzes so she would not cue them.
2. **Restudied**: the same amount of classroom time spent rereading and reviewing key concepts, not retrieving.
3. **Control**: ordinary teaching, no intervention.

Crucially the retrieval practice *replaced* regular class time — it did not add to it.

## Results

- 6th-grade social studies: quizzed material scored a **full letter grade** higher than non-quizzed.
- 8th-grade science: **92% (A-)** on quizzed content vs. **79% (C+)** on non-quizzed.
- Restudy produced **no measurable benefit** over the control condition — in line with Roediger's summary that "mere re-reading does not help."
- The testing effect persisted to the end-of-year exams **eight months later**.

## Why it matters

Perhaps the most striking single data point in the retrieval-practice literature. The study transports the testing effect out of the psychology lab and into a noisy real classroom with ordinary curriculum, and shows that (1) retrieval works, (2) restudy doesn't, and (3) the gains last. It also rebuts the most common teacher objection — that quizzing steals instructional time — because the retrieval arm replaced lecture/discussion minute-for-minute and still won. This reframes the entire purpose of quizzes: testing isn't just a dipstick to measure learning — it *is* a learning tool.

*Note on the city:* [[make-it-stick]] locates the school in Columbia, Illinois; [[small-teaching]] (p. 44) says Columbia, Missouri. The school was in Illinois — Lang's attribution appears to be a minor slip, possibly because Roediger's lab is in St. Louis, Missouri.

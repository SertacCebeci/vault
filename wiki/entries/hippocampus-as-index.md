# Hippocampus as Index

The hippocampus functions as an index to the neocortex, not as a storage site for memories. Like the index of a book — which tells you where information is located without containing the information itself — the hippocampus maintains pointers to where neural links are distributed across the neocortex. Each time a student retrieves information, the hippocampus strengthens links between neocortical sites, gradually enabling [[working-memory]] to access the information directly.

## Elaboration

Two hippocampi sit one on each side of the brain, above each ear, each about the size of a large lima bean. Despite their small size, they play a critical role in new learning. When [[working-memory]] deposits new information into [[long-term-memory]], the hippocampus receives a quick copy of the indexing information — where in the neocortex the various elements of the new learning are stored.

The hippocampus has limited linking capacity, sufficient for events, situations, and experiences spanning at most a few months. This matches the time needed for [[consolidation]] of links in the neocortex. During breaks and [[sleep-and-learning|sleep]], the hippocampus "whispers" its indexing song to the neocortex, helping it know which links to strengthen and which to weaken. Over days, weeks, and months, this repeated hippocampal guidance shapes the neocortical connections into their final, stable form.

Once material is well-learned — once the neocortex can sing the song on its own — the hippocampal index becomes unnecessary. This is the neurological marker of mastery: [[working-memory]] can reach directly into the neocortex without the hippocampal crutch. Conversely, cramming fills the hippocampus with fresh indexing links but leaves the neocortex barely touched. If the hippocampal index fades before the neocortex consolidates, the information is effectively lost.

The hippocampus cannot both learn (face the conductor to receive new indexing information) and teach (turn to Neo to rehearse existing connections) at the same time. This is a key reason why [[brain-breaks]] during instruction are essential — they give the hippocampus time to turn and offload to the neocortex.

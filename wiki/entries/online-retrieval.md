# Online Retrieval Practice

**How to build retrieval practice into online or hybrid course design**, when the instructor cannot be in the room to enforce closed-notebook retrieval. Based on Michelle Miller's recommendations in *Minds Online* (2014) and Flower Darby's *Small Teaching Online* (2019), consolidated in [[small-teaching]] Ch. 2.

The core challenge: students working at home have notes, slides, and readings open. Anything you ask them to "remember" they will instead look up. So online retrieval design has to exploit structural levers rather than honor-system ones.

## Three mechanisms

### 1. Reading Checks (Conditional Release)

At the end of every online reading page or section, embed a short quiz. Configure the LMS so students **cannot advance** to the next section until they pass it. This is sometimes called "conditional release." Miller cites a study in which students reading material with such embedded quizzes outperformed non-quizzed peers on the final exam — and also reported **less** anxiety about the cumulative test, not more, despite the frequent in-line quizzing.

### 2. Frequent Quizzing from a Large Bank

Build a large item bank and require weekly quizzes. If the bank is large enough you can allow multiple retakes — each retake is another retrieval rep, so retakes amplify rather than dilute the benefit. A small bank plus retakes becomes a cheating surface; a large bank plus retakes becomes a retrieval engine.

Short-answer produces more learning (cf. [[roediger-butler-art-history]]) but multiple-choice is often the realistic choice online because it grades itself. Miller's pragmatic principle: "the best quiz is the one that students will actually do."

Use timers. A generous but bounded window reduces the incentive to flip through materials and increases the share of items answered from memory.

### 3. Spacing Due Dates

Spread different kinds of work across different days of the week: quiz Monday, discussion post Wednesday, homework problem Friday, essay draft Sunday. This yields two benefits for the price of one: regular retrieval practice and [[interleaving]] of the cognitive demands. It also flattens the weekly workload in a way students reliably prefer once they experience it.

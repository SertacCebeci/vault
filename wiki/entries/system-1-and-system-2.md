# System 1 and System 2

Daniel Kahneman's framework from *Thinking, Fast and Slow* describes two modes of cognitive processing. System 1 is automatic, unconscious, intuitive, and immediate — it draws on senses and memories to size up situations in the blink of an eye. System 2 is controlled, conscious, analytical, and slow — it considers choices, makes decisions, and exerts self-control. The interplay between these systems is central to understanding why our judgment goes wrong and how we can correct it.

## Elaboration

System 1 is powerful because it draws on accumulated experience and deep emotions. It gives us the survival reflex in moments of danger and the deftness earned through thousands of hours of [[deliberate-practice]]. A running back dodging tackles, a cop reading a dangerous situation, a neurosurgeon recognizing a complication — these are System 1 at its best. System 1 is what [[mental-models]] become once they're deeply ingrained: fast, fluent, and largely invisible to conscious awareness.

But System 1 is also susceptible to illusion. Its very speed and automaticity mean it can be hijacked by perceptual illusions, [[narrative-bias]], emotional reasoning, and familiarity effects. When System 1's conclusions arise from misperception or illusion, they can steer you badly — and because System 1 operates below conscious awareness, you may not realize you've been misled. This is the root of [[illusions-of-competence]]: the feeling of knowing that System 1 provides is often mistaken for actual knowledge.

System 2's job is to check System 1 — like a pilot scanning cockpit instruments rather than trusting the seat-of-the-pants sensation of level flight. Learning when to trust your intuition (System 1) and when to question it (engaging System 2) is a core aspect of [[metacognition]] and a skill that separates experts from novices. The [[calibration]] tools recommended in learning science are essentially System 2 instruments: testing, feedback, peer review, and simulation, each designed to override System 1's unreliable self-assessment.

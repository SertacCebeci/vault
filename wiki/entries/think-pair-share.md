# Think-Pair-Share

Think-pair-share is a collaborative learning technique developed in 1981 by Frank Lyman at the University of Maryland. The teacher poses an open-ended question, students *think* quietly for a moment, then *pair* up to share and refine their ideas with a partner, and finally *share* their best answer with the class. Each phase serves a distinct neuroscientific purpose within the [[declarative-learning-system]].

## How it works

The *think* phase is the most often overlooked but is neuroscientifically critical. During this quiet moment, students are not just reflecting on the teacher's question — they are giving their [[hippocampus-as-index|hippocampus]] a "brain break" to begin offloading to the neocortex. The pause allows students to begin making sense of what was just taught, initiating [[consolidation]].

The *pair* phase provides [[recall-as-learning|retrieval practice]] in a low-stakes, supportive context. Students must articulate what they know — pulling ideas from [[long-term-memory]] rather than just holding them in [[working-memory]]. Hearing a partner's perspective adds new connections, and verbalizing one's own understanding strengthens existing ones.

The *share* phase extends learning to the whole class. Choosing a shy student who clearly has the right answer can build confidence. The technique can be enhanced by having students jot down their initial response before pairing (think-*write*-pair-share), adding accountability.

The transition time to and from think-pair-share activities naturally provides [[brain-breaks]] — time for the hippocampus to rehearse with the neocortex. The collaborative activity itself also assists with offloading by allowing students to think in a more relaxed, team-oriented way.

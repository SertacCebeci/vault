# Roediger & Butler Art-History Retrieval Study

**Concept illustrated:** [[testing-effect]] — specifically, that the *format* of retrieval shapes the size of the effect.

In a 2007 experiment, Henry Roediger and Andrew Butler had students watch three 30-minute art-history lectures with slideshows over a 3-day period. After each lecture, students were randomly assigned to one of four conditions:

- **(a)** Short-answer test on the lecture
- **(b)** Multiple-choice test on the lecture
- **(c)** Restudy of the key facts
- **(d)** No further activity — they walked out the door (i.e., what happens after most college lectures)

Thirty days later — a realistic interval mimicking the gap between class and exam — all students took a short-answer final test.

## Results

| Condition | 30-day recall |
|---|---|
| (a) Short-answer | **47%** |
| (b) Multiple-choice | 36% |
| (c) Restudy | 36% |
| (d) No activity | 20% |

## Why it matters

Three takeaways:

1. **Retrieval beats restudy** — even the multiple-choice group, which barely outperformed the restudy group, is essentially matching focused rereading with a tiny intervention. Group (d) performance (20%) shows what happens by default.
2. **Production beats recognition.** Short-answer questions force the learner to *generate* an answer in their own words, not merely pick one from a list. They produce nearly 50% more durable memory than multiple-choice retrieval.
3. **Test-format alignment matters.** Part of the short-answer advantage may be that the final was itself short-answer — the practice mirrored the assessment, reinforcing [[small-teaching]]'s principle of aligning practice with assessments.

The study is the empirical spine behind Lang's recommendation that closing questions and opening questions be written, short-answer, and focused on the concepts the instructor most wants remembered.

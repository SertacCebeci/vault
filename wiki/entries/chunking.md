# Chunking

Chunking is the process of binding pieces of information together through meaning and use into a single, compact neural pattern — a "chunk" — that can be accessed as one unit in [[working-memory]]. It is the fundamental mechanism by which the brain compresses information and builds expertise. A chunk is essentially a network of neurons that fire together so you can think or act smoothly and effectively without attending to every underlying detail.

## Elaboration

Chunks are built through three steps: **focused attention** (undivided concentration on the material), **understanding** (grasping the basic idea so it creates broad-encompassing memory traces that link to other chunks), and **context** (seeing not just how but *when* to use the chunk, fitting it into the bigger picture). Understanding alone doesn't create a chunk — you must also practice it yourself. This is why merely looking at a worked solution creates an [[illusions-of-competence|illusion of competence]]; only doing it yourself builds the neural pattern.

The path to expertise is built chunk by chunk. Small chunks join into larger ones. A chess master has thousands of chess-position chunks; a musician has thousands of musical-phrase chunks. The bigger and more practiced your chunked mental library, the more easily you can solve problems — your [[focused-and-diffuse-modes|diffuse mode]] can more easily skip to the right solution by metaphorically listening to whispers from your chunk library. A well-developed chunk occupies only one slot in [[working-memory]], freeing the remaining slots for other thinking. This is analogous to compressing a file into a ZIP — the information is still there but takes up less active space.

Two ways to build understanding converge: **bottom-up chunking** (practice and repetition strengthens each chunk) and **top-down big-picture processing** (understanding where chunks fit in the overall framework). Context is where these meet.

Background knowledge is the critical variable for chunk size. The more prior knowledge you have in a domain, the larger and more elaborate your chunks become. Neuroscientists describe this as increasing the "size of the balls" that working memory juggles — the number of arms on the attentional octopus stays the same, but each arm holds more. This is why domain-specific practice (geometry, French, piano) increases *apparent* working memory capacity for that domain without changing general capacity. A student who seems like a [[race-car-and-hiker-brains|hiker]] in an unfamiliar subject may process like a race car once sufficient chunks are in place. This relationship between chunking and background knowledge is the mechanism underlying the [[long-term-memory-boosts-working-memory]] effect.

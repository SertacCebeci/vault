# Sobel's Quiz Evolution

**Concepts illustrated:** [[testing-effect]], [[low-stakes-classroom-quizzing]]

Andrew Sobel, a political economics professor at Washington University in St. Louis, faced a growing attendance problem in his 160-student introductory course. By midsemester, 25-35% of students were absent on any given day. His first attempt at a fix backfired badly: he replaced the midterm and final with nine unannounced pop quizzes. The logic was sound — frequent retrieval practice should boost learning, and the stakes would motivate attendance. But a third or more of the students bailed out over the semester. Those who stayed either thrived (earning A-pluses) or collapsed (earning Cs), with almost nothing in between. Teaching reviews were brutal.

Sobel scrapped the format and returned to lectures with a midterm and final. But a couple of years later, after hearing a presentation on the learning benefits of testing, he tried again — differently. This time he announced nine scheduled quizzes on the syllabus, was explicit about dates, and eliminated the midterm and final entirely. No surprises, no gotchas.

The result was transformative. Enrollment actually *increased*. Students showed up for quiz days and sometimes skipped non-quiz days, but they showed up. The quality of class discussion rose sharply. By end of semester, students were writing paragraph-length and even full-page essays on quiz questions at a level Sobel usually saw only in upper-division courses. Five years in, he was sold: "The quality of discussions in class has gone way up. I see that big a difference in their written work, just by going from three exams to nine quizzes." Enrollment grew to 185 and counting.

## Why it matters

This story shows that the [[testing-effect]] is real but implementation matters enormously. The *same* underlying principle — frequent retrieval practice — failed as surprise pop quizzes but succeeded as transparent, scheduled, low-stakes quizzes. The difference was student buy-in: when students understood the structure and felt it was fair, they embraced the practice rather than resenting it. It's a powerful lesson for anyone designing [[low-stakes-classroom-quizzing]] interventions.

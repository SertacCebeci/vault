# Jot Recall

A family of lightweight classroom techniques that embed [[recall-as-learning|retrieval practice]] into everyday teaching. The core action is simple: students pause, look away from their materials, and jot down the key ideas from memory. This forces genuine retrieval from [[long-term-memory]] rather than passive re-exposure through [[working-memory]], and it gives teachers an immediate formative check on whether students are actually learning.

## How it works

All jot recall variants follow the same core loop: teach or read, then pause, look away from the material, and write down the key ideas from memory. This forces [[recall-as-learning|retrieval]], which strengthens neural links far more than rereading or highlighting. The technique is framed with an upbeat tone — it is about checking whether material has moved from working memory into long-term memory, not about testing for grades.

Four variants are described:

- **Jot Notes**: During essential sections of a lesson, pause and ask students to take a fresh piece of paper and briefly jot down the most important ideas covered — without looking at their notes. The teacher circulates to check comprehension. If time permits, students gather in groups of three or four to compare and discuss what they found to be the key ideas.
- **Jot Sketches**: Students create drawings that represent their understanding of the material. Allowing creativity increases interest and deepens understanding. Drawing pictures in place of words is also effective with emergent learners still developing writing skills — a form of dual coding.
- **Jot Reading Materials**: During quiet reading in class, students pause at the end of each page, look away, and see if they can recall the key idea without looking back. They jot it down, then continue. This can also be done at home.
- **Jot Previous Materials**: Students jot what they remember from the previous day, week, or month. This variant explicitly combines retrieval practice with [[spaced-repetition]] — the time interval between learning and retrieval is what makes it a spaced exercise.

## Concepts applied

- [[recall-as-learning]] — every jot variant forces active retrieval rather than passive re-exposure
- [[testing-effect]] — the act of retrieval itself strengthens memory, even without formal grading; Pooja Agarwal and Patrice Bain's *Powerful Teaching* calls this "no-stakes testing"
- [[working-memory]] — the technique exposes whether material has moved from working memory to long-term memory
- [[spaced-repetition]] — the "jot previous materials" variant explicitly introduces spacing between learning and retrieval
- [[learn-it-link-it]] — jot recall drives students from the "learn it" phase into the "link it" phase by requiring active retrieval

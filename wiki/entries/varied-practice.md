# Varied Practice

Varied practice means practicing the same skill under different conditions, contexts, or parameters rather than repeating it identically each time. While [[interleaving]] mixes different skills or problem types, varied practice varies the *conditions* of a single skill — tossing beanbags at different distances, solving anagrams in different letter arrangements, practicing hockey passes from different positions on the ice. The result is a broader, more flexible mental schema that transfers better to novel situations.

## Elaboration

The beanbag toss study is the canonical demonstration. Eight-year-olds practiced tossing beanbags into buckets. Half practiced only the target distance (three feet). The other half practiced at two and four feet — never at three. After twelve weeks, on a test at three feet, the varied-practice group significantly outperformed the group that had practiced only the target distance. The children who *never practiced the test condition* were better at it than those who *only* practiced it.

The mechanism appears to be that varied practice forces learners to develop a broader understanding of the relationships between conditions and the movements or strategies required to succeed. You build a flexible "movement vocabulary" — or in cognitive tasks, a flexible problem-solving schema — rather than a narrow, rigid procedure tuned to one specific condition. Neuroimaging studies support this: motor skills learned through varied practice are consolidated in brain areas associated with higher-order, more cognitively demanding motor learning, while massed practice is consolidated in areas for simpler motor skills. The inference is that varied practice encodes learning in a richer, more flexible neural representation.

This principle extends beyond motor skills. In a verbal learning study, students who practiced multiple anagram arrangements for the same word outperformed those who practiced the same anagram repeatedly — even when tested on the exact anagram the latter group had drilled. The same logic applies to identifying tree species, differentiating legal case principles, or mastering software tools.

It is important to distinguish varied practice from **blocked practice**, where a learner moves through stations performing a different drill at each one in a fixed sequence. Blocked practice can look varied but lacks the randomization that forces genuine adaptation. True varied practice requires shuffling the conditions.

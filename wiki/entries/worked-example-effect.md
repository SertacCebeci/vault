# Worked-Example Effect

Being exposed to and working with sample problems can be invaluable in allowing students to form mental templates that enable them to understand and solve a wide variety of problems. Research by educational psychologist John Sweller and colleagues established that studying worked examples reduces extraneous [[working-memory]] load and helps learners build initial schemas for problem-solving.

## Elaboration

Worked examples are valuable because they support the "learn it" phase of [[learn-it-link-it]]: they give students a scaffold for understanding the structure and logic of a problem type. A student who studies a well-presented algebraic solution forms an initial neural template — a pattern of what the problem looks like, what steps are involved, and how the pieces fit together.

However, worked examples are necessary but not sufficient. The critical distinction is between understanding an example (which happens in [[working-memory]]) and being able to reproduce the solution independently (which requires [[long-term-memory]] links built through practice). Students who study examples but never work problems on their own — like Katina following the textbook solution with her finger while writing the homework — remain trapped in the "learn it" phase. The example problems are not the issue; the problem is that the student never progresses to independent retrieval.

This means teachers should use worked examples strategically: present them to build initial understanding, then transition students to independent practice with the examples put away. The sequence is example → attempt → retrieve, not example → copy → repeat.

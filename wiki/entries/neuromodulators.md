# Neuromodulators

Neuromodulators are chemicals that influence how neurons respond to other neurons — they carry information not about the content of an experience but about its importance and value. Three neuromodulators are particularly relevant to learning: **acetylcholine**, **dopamine**, and **serotonin**. They profoundly influence attention, motivation, social behavior, and emotion — all of which affect learning.

## Elaboration

**Acetylcholine** neurons form neuromodulatory connections to the cortex that are important for focused learning and close attention. They project widely and activate circuits that control synaptic plasticity, leading to new [[long-term-memory]] formation. Acetylcholine is essentially the chemical substrate of the [[focused-and-diffuse-modes|focused mode]].

**Dopamine** neurons, found in the basal ganglia and brain stem, control reward learning and are released when we receive an unexpected reward. Critically, dopamine is in the business of predicting *future* rewards — it can motivate you to do something unrewarding now for a much better reward later. This connects directly to [[procrastination]]: promising yourself a reward after a study session taps into your dopamine system. Loss of dopamine leads to lack of motivation (anhedonia) and, severely, to Parkinson's disease. Addictive drugs artificially hijack dopamine, creating false signals of wonderful events.

**Serotonin** is a diffuse neuromodulatory system that strongly affects social life. In primate hierarchies, higher-ranking individuals have higher serotonin levels. It is closely linked to risk-taking behavior and mood. Prozac treats depression by raising serotonin.

Emotions — mediated by these neuromodulators and integrated in the amygdala and hippocampus — are not separate from cognition. They are intertwined with perception, attention, learning, and memory. Keeping your amygdala happy is essential for effective learning.

# Write to Learn

"Write to learn" is a classroom technique where students reflect on a recent class topic in a brief writing assignment, expressing the main ideas in their own words and relating them to other concepts covered in class or to knowledge from outside class. It leverages multiple cognitive activities simultaneously — retrieval, elaboration, and generation — making it a potent compound learning strategy.

## How it works

After the presentation of a key concept during a lecture, the instructor asks students to write a brief summary in their own words, restating the concept, generating examples, and connecting it to other material. This is distinct from copying: students who write summaries from memory engage in active retrieval ([[reflection-as-retrieval]]), express ideas in their own words ([[elaboration]]), and produce original examples ([[generation-effect]]). The writing also creates additional [[retrieval-cues]] by connecting the concept to the student's own language and prior knowledge.

A study of over eight hundred college students in introductory psychology classes tested this directly. For some key concepts, students wrote summaries in their own words; for other concepts, they were shown slides and spent a few minutes copying key ideas and examples verbatim. On exams, students scored approximately half a letter grade better on the concepts they had written about in their own words. In follow-up tests approximately two months later, the benefits had dropped somewhat but remained robust — demonstrating that the learning gains from writing are durable, not just momentary.

## Concepts applied

- [[reflection-as-retrieval]] — writing after a lecture is a form of reflective retrieval practice
- [[generation-effect]] — producing your own summaries and examples strengthens memory more than copying
- [[elaboration]] — restating ideas in your own words and connecting to prior knowledge
- [[retrieval-cues]] — writing creates new cues by linking concepts to personal language and examples

# Terry and Maxwell's Equations

**Concepts illustrated:** [[procedural-learning-system]], [[declarative-procedural-seesaw]]

When coauthor Terry Sejnowski was a college physics major, he memorized Maxwell's equations — elegant formulas derived from experiments with batteries and magnets. He could recite them perfectly. But did he understand electricity and magnetism? Not really. It was only when he had to apply the equations to real problems — designing a radio antenna, for example — that he learned their consequences. By using [[procedural-learning-system|procedural learning]] to solve many different problems, Terry formed intuitions about what the equations really meant, how to use them, and how to solve new problems quickly.

## Why it matters

This example crystallizes the distinction between declarative and procedural understanding. Memorizing equations is a declarative achievement — fast to acquire but limited in utility. Genuine understanding emerged only through repeated problem-solving practice that engaged the procedural system. The story illustrates why textbook chapters (declarative explanations) and end-of-chapter problems (procedural practice) are both essential for mastery.

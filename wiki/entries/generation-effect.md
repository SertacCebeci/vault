# Generation Effect

The generation effect is the finding that even a modest effort to produce an answer — rather than passively receiving it — strengthens memory of that answer. When learners see a word pair like *foot-s___e* and fill in "shoe," they later recall the pair better than learners who simply read *foot-shoe* intact. The small act of generating the answer, even when the answer is nearly obvious, creates a stronger memory trace than passive exposure.

## Elaboration

The generation effect is a close relative of the [[testing-effect]], but it highlights something subtle: the benefit comes not just from full retrieval, but from *any* degree of active production during learning. This is why supply-format tests (essay, short-answer, flashcards) tend to be more effective than recognition-format tests (multiple choice, true/false) — the more the learner must generate, the greater the cognitive effort, and the stronger the resulting memory.

An intriguing finding from the research: the generation effect was *greater* when the practice retrieval was delayed by twenty intervening word pairs than when it came immediately after first studying the pair. The greater effort required by delayed recall solidified the memory further. This interaction with spacing and delay connects the generation effect directly to [[desirable-difficulty]] — the harder the generation, the better the learning.

The generation effect has practical implications beyond test format. Trying to solve a problem before being taught the solution, writing a summary from memory before rereading, or attempting to explain a concept to someone else are all forms of generation that leverage this effect. Each one forces the learner to actively construct rather than passively receive.

A crucial finding: even unsuccessful generation helps. When you're asked to supply an answer to something new and you get it wrong, the power of generation to aid learning is even more evident. As you cast about for a solution, retrieving related knowledge from memory, you strengthen the route to a gap in your learning even before the answer is provided. When you then receive the correct answer, connections are made to the related material that is fresh in your mind from the effort. It's better to solve a problem than to memorize a solution, and it's better to attempt a solution and supply the incorrect answer than not to make the attempt at all — provided corrective feedback is given. This connects directly to [[priming-for-learning]] and the [[errorless-learning-myth|debunking of errorless learning]].

# High-Structure Teaching

High-structure teaching replaces the traditional lecture-plus-high-stakes-exam format with daily and weekly low-stakes exercises that keep students actively retrieving, elaborating, and calibrating throughout the term. The approach closes achievement gaps by providing the practice scaffolding that poorly prepared students lack from their prior schooling — building the habits of effective learning into the course itself rather than assuming students already have them.

## How it works

A high-structure class embeds [[desirable-difficulty]] into every class meeting through a combination of tools:

1. **Frequent retrieval** — Daily or near-daily low-stakes quizzes, free-recall exercises, or testing-group activities force students to practice retrieval rather than passively absorb lectures. These leverage the [[testing-effect]] to consolidate learning continuously.

2. **Elaborative synthesis** — Weekly summary sheets or learning paragraphs require students to connect concepts, illustrate relationships, and express understanding in their own words — a structured form of [[elaboration]].

3. **Transparent metacognition** — The instructor explicitly teaches students about [[desirable-difficulty]], the [[testing-effect]], and [[illusions-of-competence]], giving them a vocabulary for understanding why effective learning feels harder and a framework for interpreting setbacks as growth rather than failure ([[growth-mindset]]).

4. **Calibration through Bloom's taxonomy** — Answer keys are structured by levels of Bloom's taxonomy (knowledge, comprehension, application, analysis, synthesis, evaluation), so students can see where their answers fall and what deeper mastery looks like. This turns each test into a [[calibration]] tool.

5. **Low-stakes consequences** — Practice exercises count toward the grade at very low stakes. Even minimal consequences improve performance compared to exercises with no grade impact, because students take them more seriously.

In experiments comparing high-structure biology classes to low-structure classes (traditional lecturing with midterm and final), high-structure classes significantly reduced student failure rates and narrowed the gap between poorly prepared and well-prepared students — while simultaneously producing higher-level answers on Bloom's taxonomy.

## Concepts applied

- [[desirable-difficulty]] — the course structure ensures students regularly encounter productive struggle
- [[testing-effect]] — frequent quizzing provides the retrieval events that strengthen memory
- [[calibration]] — Bloom's taxonomy-linked answer keys and free recall reveal actual vs. perceived mastery
- [[growth-mindset]] — teaching students that struggle builds ability changes how they interpret difficulty
- [[illusions-of-competence]] — giving students the vocabulary to recognize and combat their own illusions
- [[elaboration]] — summary sheets and learning paragraphs build layers of meaning

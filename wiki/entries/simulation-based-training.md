# Simulation-Based Training

Simulation-based training uses realistic scenarios — video-based, role-played, or virtual — to replicate the demands and changeable conditions of real-world performance. Unlike traditional instruction (lecture, textbook, static drills), simulation reveals the gap between perceived readiness and actual performance by confronting learners with dynamic situations that require judgment, adaptation, and reflexive action. It serves both as a learning tool and a [[calibration]] instrument.

## How it works

Simulation works by activating multiple learning mechanisms simultaneously:

- **[[calibration]]**: Simulation exposes what you don't know under realistic conditions. Lt. Catherine Johnson of the Minneapolis Police describes how a simulated traffic-stop ambush permanently changed her real-world behavior — she now always checks car trunks on stops, because the simulation revealed a blind spot she didn't know she had.
- **[[mental-models]]**: Simulation builds and tests the bundled perceptions-and-actions that constitute expert performance. The officer doesn't just learn a technique; she builds the mental model of "traffic stop with concealed threat" that will fire automatically in the field.
- **[[desirable-difficulty]]**: The stress, time pressure, and ambiguity of realistic simulation create productive difficulty that strengthens learning beyond what calm, orderly instruction can achieve.
- **Feedback loops**: Simulations typically end with debriefs where an instructor reviews the learner's decisions, providing corrective feedback that targets specific judgment errors.

The cardinal rule of simulation design: **practice like you play, because you will play like you practice.** If the training environment differs from the real-world environment in important ways, the mental models built will be miscalibrated. The cautionary tale is the [[police-gun-handback]] — a training convenience (handing the gun back to your partner after each repetition) that created a dangerous automatic behavior in the field.

Simulation is used extensively in police training (video-based firearms scenarios, role-played domestic calls), medical training (surgical simulation, patient-actor encounters), military training (flight simulators, war games), and increasingly in corporate settings.

# Closing Questions

**A retrieval routine for the last few minutes of class.** Before students leave, ask them — in writing — to recall and apply the key concepts from the session. Favor writing over oral because writing forces every student to commit to a specific answer (see the "participation from all students" argument in [[rogerson-chemistry-retrieval]]).

## The Faculty Feedback Form (Fishman & Wahesh 2020)

A simple three-question template that students complete at the end of class:

1. **What is one thing I learned from our class readings?**
2. **What is one thing I learned from today's class?**
3. **What am I unclear about or would like to know more about?**

Fishman and Wahesh add a fourth variable question each week plus a 1-100 self-rating of weekly understanding. The three-question version alone delivers retrieval (for two concepts), reflection, and a formative-assessment signal to the instructor — in under five minutes.

## Why it works

- **Retrieval practice on exactly the right material** — the concepts just covered, while working memory still holds them and before the [[forgetting-curve]] has done its work. See [[roediger-butler-art-history]] for the 30-day retention data on this kind of end-of-lecture intervention.
- **Principle of alignment.** Design closing questions to mirror the format of your high-stakes assessments. Short-answer closing questions are most defensible on the research (see [[memory-is-residue-of-thought]]) but multiple-choice closing questions are still valuable if your final is multiple-choice.
- **Feedback loop for the instructor.** The "unclear" question surfaces confusions you can address at the next session's opening question. Loop back.

## Related forms

- **One-sentence summaries** of the day's material.
- **Problem-solving prompts** tied to the day's concepts — especially valuable in STEM, where retrieval *in use* outperforms retrieval in recall.
- **Minute paper** variant: "most important thing you learned" + "biggest remaining question."
- Rogerson's two-copy question: students write twice, keep one, turn in one — the instructor reviews without grading.

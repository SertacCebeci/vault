# Small Teaching: Everyday Lessons from the Science of Learning

## Summary
*Small Teaching* argues that instructors can significantly improve student learning through small, manageable modifications to their courses — brief classroom activities, one-time interventions, and minor design tweaks — rather than requiring wholesale transformation of their teaching. Lang draws on cognitive psychology, neuroscience, and educational research to identify principles that meet three criteria: grounded in learning science, demonstrated to work in real classrooms, and personally observed by the author. The book is organized into three parts reflecting a progression from foundational cognitive processes (Knowledge: predicting, retrieving, interleaving) through deeper comprehension (Understanding: connecting, practicing, explaining) to affective and motivational dimensions (Inspiration: belonging, motivating, learning). Each chapter follows a consistent structure — opening anecdote, theory, four to five detailed implementation models, guiding principles, and quick tips — making the strategies immediately actionable for busy faculty across disciplines and institutional contexts. The second edition (2021) substantially revises two chapters, broadening "Self-Explaining" into "Explaining" and replacing "Growing" (growth mindset) with "Belonging" (asset-based teaching and community).

## Key ideas
- Factual knowledge is the prerequisite for higher-order thinking, not its opposite → [[knowledge-as-foundation]]
- Prediction before instruction enhances learning even when predictions are wrong → [[prediction-as-learning]]
- Activating prior knowledge before new material creates cognitive hooks for deeper encoding → [[prior-knowledge-activation]]
- The brain is a prediction machine; learning is fundamentally prediction-error-correction (Dehaene) → [[prediction-as-learning]]
- Emotional investment in predictions (surprise/confirmation) strengthens memory consolidation → [[prediction-as-learning]]
- Retrieval practice beats restudy — effortful recall is the learning event, not merely a measurement of it → [[testing-effect]]
- "Memory is the residue of thought" — retrieval format shapes retention because it shapes what the learner thinks about → [[memory-is-residue-of-thought]]
- Covert retrieval (silent thinking) produces essentially the same benefits as overt retrieval if pauses are respected → [[covert-retrieval]]
- Practice retrieval format should mirror assessment format; misalignment predicts failure on the final → [[practice-assessment-alignment]]
- Retrieval fits naturally in opening and closing minutes of class, and in online course design via conditional release, large quiz banks, and spaced due dates → [[opening-questions]], [[closing-questions]], [[online-retrieval]]

## Notable claims
- Kornell et al. (2009): predictors recalled 67% vs. 55% for passive studiers, despite less study time and mostly wrong predictions (Ch. 1, p. 21)
- Elizabeth Bjork pretesting: students performed ~10% better on final exam questions from pretested topics vs. non-pretested topics (Ch. 1, p. 22)
- Brod (2021): pupil dilation during prediction reveal shows emotional engagement as a mechanism for prediction's learning benefit (Ch. 1, pp. 38-39)
- Small teaching activities can raise student performance by the equivalent of a full letter grade (Introduction, p. 9)
- Agarwal & Bain middle-school quizzing study: full letter grade improvement (A- vs C+) on quizzed vs non-quizzed material; effect lasted 8 months (Ch. 2, pp. 44-45) → [[columbia-il-science-quizzing]]
- Roediger & Butler 2007 art-history study: short-answer 47% / multiple-choice 36% / restudy 36% / no activity 20% recall at 30 days (Ch. 2, p. 46) → [[roediger-butler-art-history]]
- Rogerson introductory chemistry: end-of-class short-answer question reduced fail/withdraw rate from 35% to 17% (Ch. 2, pp. 47-48) → [[rogerson-chemistry-retrieval]]
- Pyc, Agarwal, Roediger (2014): covert retrieval is as effective as overt retrieval for later retention (Ch. 2, p. 53)

## Questions raised
- How does prediction interact with retrieval practice — are they additive or redundant?
- Does the prediction benefit plateau with repeated use, or does it remain effective across a semester?
- How does the emotional mechanism (surprise/investment) interact with test anxiety?

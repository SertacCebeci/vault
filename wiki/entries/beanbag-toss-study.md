# The Beanbag Toss Study

**Concepts illustrated:** [[varied-practice]], [[desirable-difficulty]], [[transfer]]

A group of eight-year-olds in gym class practiced tossing beanbags into buckets. Half the children practiced tossing into a bucket three feet away — the exact distance they would later be tested on. The other half mixed it up, tossing into buckets at two feet and four feet away, but never at three feet.

After twelve weeks of practice, all the children were tested on the three-foot toss. The result defied intuition: the children who had *never once practiced* the three-foot toss significantly outperformed the children who had *only* practiced it.

## Why it matters

This study is the chapter's opening hook and one of the cleanest demonstrations that varied practice builds a more flexible, transferable skill than constant repetition of the target task. The varied-distance children developed a broader understanding of the relationship between distance, force, and arc — a flexible "movement vocabulary" — rather than a narrow motor program tuned to one specific condition. It powerfully illustrates that what feels like the most direct path to mastery (practicing the exact thing you'll be tested on) can be inferior to a more varied approach that never touches the target condition at all.

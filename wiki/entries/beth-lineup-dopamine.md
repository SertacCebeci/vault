# Beth's Lineup Procedure — Dopamine Contrast

**Concepts illustrated:** [[dopamine-and-learning]], [[classroom-procedures]], [[procedural-learning-system]]

Fourth-graders are sitting at their desks while the teacher shuffles papers. There is no expectation of reward. The teacher asks students to line up by their assigned numbers. As they begin lining up, the teacher provides unexpected positive reinforcement with an infectiously upbeat voice: "Isn't it great to get out of our seats and get our blood flowing? Let's add a stretch to the sky or give yourself a hug." The cheerfulness is unexpected — and rewarding. Dopamine surges into the brain areas where the new lineup procedure is being encoded, strengthening the synaptic connections. Students learn that lining up feels good, *and they also more easily learn how to line up*.

The contrast comes from Ms. Guided's approach. She expects students to line up correctly on the first attempt — it seems simple to her, because the procedure already exists in her mind. When students fumble (it is confusing for them; they have no procedural memory yet), she begins yelling. The students' dopamine levels crash from ho-hum to negative. Dopamine neurons stop firing. The neural links being formed begin to *disconnect* rather than strengthen. Students learn that Ms. Guided is associated with negative feelings, and lining up becomes affiliated with negative emotions — so they drag their feet even more in the future.

## Why it matters
This is the chapter's most vivid demonstration of how dopamine operates as a learning mechanism, not just a feel-good chemical. The same physical task (lining up) produces opposite neural outcomes depending on whether the teacher's response triggers a dopamine surge or a dopamine crash. It makes the abstract neuroscience of [[dopamine-and-learning]] concrete and actionable for teachers.

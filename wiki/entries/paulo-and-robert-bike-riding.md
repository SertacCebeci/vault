# Paulo and Robert Learn to Ride a Bike

**Concepts illustrated:** [[biologically-primary-and-secondary-knowledge]], [[race-car-and-hiker-brains]]

Paulo is a born athlete — backflips come as naturally as breathing. When his big brother Robert was still teetering on a new bike, Paulo hopped on the same far-too-large bicycle and rode off immediately as if he had been cycling his whole life. But both brothers learned to ride. Robert fell more, skinned his knees and elbows, and endured nerve-racking moments, yet he persisted until he got it. He never once told himself he did not have "the biking gene."

The puzzle is why students accept different speeds of learning for physical skills like bike riding but give up on themselves when they struggle with academic subjects. In the classroom, students who learn reading or math more slowly often conclude they lack talent for the subject, even though they would never draw that conclusion about a motor skill.

## Why it matters

The vignette sets up the chapter's central argument: learning physical skills (biologically primary) feels natural even when it takes time, but learning academic subjects (biologically secondary) triggers self-doubt because the brain was not evolved for it. The different reactions to struggle in primary versus secondary domains reveal how the evolutionary heritage of the brain shapes students' self-perceptions as learners.

# Cal Poly Batting Practice Experiment

**Concepts illustrated:** [[desirable-difficulty]], [[interleaving]], [[massed-practice]], [[discrimination-learning]], [[illusions-of-competence]]

The California Polytechnic State University baseball team — all highly experienced hitters — agreed to take extra batting practice twice a week for six weeks, following two different regimens, to see which produced better results.

One group practiced hitting forty-five pitches in a standard (massed) way: three sets of fifteen, each set consisting of one pitch type. Fifteen fastballs, then fifteen curveballs, then fifteen changeups. For each set, as the batter saw more of that type, he got gratifyingly better at anticipating the balls, timing his swings, and connecting. Learning seemed easy.

The other group received a more difficult regimen: the same three pitch types were randomly interspersed across the block of forty-five throws. For each pitch, the batter had no idea what type to expect. At the end of forty-five pitches, he was still struggling somewhat to connect with the ball. These players didn't seem to be developing the proficiency their teammates were showing.

At the end of six weeks, assessment showed that those who had practiced on randomly interspersed pitches displayed markedly better hitting than the massed-practice group. These results are especially striking because the players were already skilled hitters prior to the experiment — bringing their performance to an even higher level is strong evidence of the training regimen's effectiveness.

## Why it matters

This study perfectly crystallizes the desirable difficulty paradox: the massed group *felt* they were learning better (and would have said so if asked), while the interleaved group felt frustrated and slow. Yet the interleaved group built the skill that actually matters in baseball — hitting a curveball when you don't know it's coming. It's one skill to hit a curveball when you know a curveball will be thrown; it's a different skill to hit a curveball when you don't know what's coming. The massed group practiced the former; the interleaved group practiced the latter. This illustrates [[discrimination-learning]] in a motor-skill domain.

# Top-down and Bottom-up Attention

Attention flows through two distinct neural pathways. Top-down attention is voluntary, originating in the prefrontal cortex and propagating backward through the brain — it is the student choosing to pay attention through willpower. Bottom-up attention is involuntary, originating in sensory processing areas at the back of the brain and propagating forward — it is the environment capturing attention through motion, sound, or surprise.

## Elaboration

Top-down attention is effortful and finite. When a scene stays static for too long, attention naturally drifts and the student must force it back through willpower — a depleting resource. Bottom-up attention is automatic and effortless: motion, unexpected sounds, humor, and visual novelty pull focus without requiring the student to try.

Skilled online teaching exploits bottom-up processes to reduce the willpower burden on students. Small editing techniques — switching camera position, transitioning between full-body and half-body shots, inserting an animated arrow, adding a brief sound effect — periodically recapture attention through involuntary channels. The key is restraint: enough sensory variation to refresh attention without overwhelming the learning content. A talking head frozen in a corner of the screen provides no bottom-up stimulus; a well-edited video with sporadic visual changes sustains attention naturally.

This framework explains why [[brain-breaks]] work in synchronous settings (they break the static scene) and why humor functions as a learning tool — an unexpected joke triggers bottom-up attention and a [[dopamine-and-learning]] boost simultaneously.

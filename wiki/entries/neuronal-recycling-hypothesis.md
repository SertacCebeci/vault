# Neuronal Recycling Hypothesis

When humans learn [[biologically-primary-and-secondary-knowledge|biologically secondary]] skills like reading or mathematics, the brain does not grow entirely new circuits. Instead, it repurposes — "recycles" — neural regions that originally evolved for related primary functions. Letters and ideographic characters consistently invade the occipitotemporal cortex (which evolved to detect objects and scenes), while number representations invade the bilateral intraparietal cortex (which codes for a rudimentary sense of quantity). This happens regardless of culture or writing system.

## Elaboration

The hypothesis was developed by neuroscientist [[stanislas-dehaene]]. Its central insight is that the brain is plastic but not super-plastic — new skills cannot colonize any arbitrary brain region. Instead, the brain selects areas with functions most similar, from an evolutionary perspective, to the new ability being acquired. Reading recycles face and object recognition circuits because letters are, at a neural level, visual objects. Arithmetic recycles quantity-estimation circuits because formal math extends the approximate number sense that even infants possess.

This constrained recycling explains several phenomena. It explains why certain types of brain damage can be repaired (nearby circuits can take over related functions) while others cannot (too much rewiring would be needed). It explains why learning to read is hard — the brain must painstakingly retrain a region evolved for a different purpose. And it explains the cross-cultural consistency of where reading and math "land" in the brain: the architecture of recycling is universal because the underlying primary circuits are universal.

The hypothesis also reinforces why [[direct-instruction]] matters for secondary learning. Unlike primary abilities that wire themselves through immersion, secondary abilities require guided practice to coax the neural recycling process along. The brain will not spontaneously repurpose the occipitotemporal cortex for reading just by being surrounded by books.

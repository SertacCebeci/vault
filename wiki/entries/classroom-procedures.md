# Classroom Procedures

Classroom procedures are everyday routines — entering the room, transitioning between activities, lining up, collecting materials — that are explicitly taught and practiced until they become automatic habits stored in students' [[procedural-learning-system]]. When procedures are habitual, they free [[working-memory]] for learning and create a safe, predictable classroom environment that reduces stress.

## How it works

Teaching procedures follows the same [[learn-it-link-it]] sequence used for academic content, mapped onto the [[direct-instruction]] framework:

1. **Tell** — Use direct instruction to get the procedure into the declarative system. Show and tell students exactly what you expect ("I do").
2. **Show and practice** — Practice the procedure together, providing specific praise for correct execution ("We do"). This is where [[dopamine-and-learning]] matters most: unexpected positive reinforcement during practice triggers dopamine surges that accelerate both the feel-good association and the synaptic consolidation.
3. **Reinforce to automaticity** — Have students repeat the procedure until it becomes habitual ("You do"). The [[procedural-learning-system]] takes over, and students perform the routine without conscious thought.
4. **Reteach as needed** — When students forget, reteach rather than punish. Punitive responses trigger dopamine crashes that impair the consolidation in progress.

Consistency is paramount. Inconsistent enforcement prevents the procedural system from consolidating the routine, because the basal ganglia need consistent repetition to build strong procedural links.

Common procedures worth teaching explicitly: entering the classroom, taking attendance, participating in discussion, asking for help, using technology, putting headers on papers, making up missed work, using hand signals for needs, using free time, leaving the classroom.

A key insight is that students cannot see what is in the teacher's mind. What seems obvious to an experienced teacher (the [[curse-of-knowledge]]) must be made explicit and practiced for students who lack the procedural memory.

## Concepts applied
- [[procedural-learning-system]] — procedures are habits built through the basal ganglia pathway
- [[direct-instruction]] — the tell-show-practice sequence gets information into declarative memory first, then transfers to procedural through repetition
- [[dopamine-and-learning]] — unexpected praise during practice accelerates habit formation; punitive responses impair it
- [[learn-it-link-it]] — I do + We do = learn it; You do + extend = link it
- [[working-memory]] — automatic procedures free WM capacity for academic learning
- [[curse-of-knowledge]] — teachers must make implicit expectations explicit
- [[collaborative-learning]] — group work requires its own set of taught procedures (roles, behavioral expectations, transition cues)
- [[socioemotional-learning]] — social procedures (turn-taking, boundary-setting) are taught the same way as academic routines

# Eustress and Learning

Moderate, transient stress — known as eustress — enhances cognition, working memory, and learning. Unlike chronic stress (distress), which damages health and impairs brain function, eustress is a short-lived physiological response over which the learner has some control, such as the tension of preparing for a test or delivering a presentation.

## Elaboration

Transient stress triggers the release of adrenaline and cortisol. In moderate amounts, these glucocorticoids strengthen connections between neurons — the metaphor is greasing a pan so information flows more easily. The relationship between stress hormones and memory performance follows an inverted-U curve: too little stress produces boredom and sleepiness (no hormonal boost), optimal moderate stress produces peak memory performance, and too much stress causes neural connections to burn and stick, blocking information flow entirely.

The practical implication is counterintuitive: stress is not the enemy of learning. Students who study for a nerve-racking test may learn more efficiently than those lounging around "studying" at leisure, precisely because the transient stress sharpens their neural machinery. Information learned under moderate eustress — such as a memorable presentation — can persist for years. The uncommon-sense insight is to use eustress strategically rather than demonizing all stress in the classroom.

# Loftus Car Crash Study

**Concepts illustrated:** [[memory-distortion]], [[narrative-bias]]

In a classic experiment on the suggestibility of memory, participants watched a video of a car running a stop sign and colliding with another car. Afterward, they were asked to estimate the speed of the vehicles — but the question was framed differently for different groups. Those asked to judge the speed when the vehicles "contacted" each other gave an average estimate of thirty-two miles per hour. Those asked about when the vehicles "smashed" into each other estimated forty-one miles per hour — a 28% increase from a single word change. The actual speed limit was thirty miles per hour.

A single verb — "contacted" vs. "smashed" — altered not just the speed estimate but the entire reconstructed memory of the event. The "smashed" framing activated a narrative of violent collision, which reshaped what participants believed they had seen. The legal system has long recognized the danger of "leading questions," but this study showed that suggestibility is far more subtle than most people realize — even neutral-seeming word choices can systematically distort recall.

The study also connects to the broader problem of self-generated misinformation: when witnesses struggling to recall are instructed to let their minds roam freely and generate whatever comes to mind, their guesses can later be retrieved as actual memories. This is one reason testimony after hypnotic interviews is barred in most jurisdictions.

## Why it matters
If a single word can shift a speed estimate by 28%, imagine how much our own internal framing — the stories we tell ourselves about what we learned, how well we studied, how much we understand — distorts our self-assessment. The Loftus study is evidence that [[memory-distortion]] isn't a rare glitch but a pervasive feature of human cognition, reinforcing the need for objective [[calibration]] tools rather than subjective judgment.

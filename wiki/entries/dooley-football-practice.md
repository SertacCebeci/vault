# Coach Dooley's Football Training Cycle

**Concepts illustrated:** [[interleaving]], [[spaced-repetition]], [[varied-practice]], [[reflection-as-retrieval]]

Vince Dooley, head coach of the University of Georgia Bulldogs from 1964 to 1988 (201 wins, 77 losses), organized his team's training around a weekly cycle geared to each Saturday's game. In that short period, players had to learn the opponent's game, study and practice offensive and defensive strategies, break these down into individual movements, knit the parts into a whole, and run the plays until they became automatic.

The practice schedule was a natural embodiment of every major learning principle. Each day's practice began with position-specific fundamentals (spaced, since these recurred daily). Players then worked in small groups on multi-position maneuvers, gradually assembling into full team play — speeded up and slowed down, rehearsed mentally and physically. Special plays were practiced only on Thursdays (always a week between sessions) and run in a varied sequence. Players took their playbooks home and mentally rehearsed plays — walking through steps, leaning their bodies as if running routes, simulating reactions to opponents' adjustments.

The Saturday quarterback going into the game — mentally running through plays, anticipating reactions, visualizing adjustments — was doing the same thing as the neurosurgeon [[mike-ebersold|Ebersold]] rehearsing what's about to unfold in the operating room.

## Why it matters

Dooley's regime demonstrates that elite practitioners often arrive at evidence-based learning strategies through intuition and trial-and-error, long before the science catches up. His weekly cycle naturally combines spacing (daily return to fundamentals, weekly return to special plays), interleaving (mixing individual, small group, and team practice across positions and scenarios), variation (changing conditions and opponents weekly), and mental rehearsal (a form of retrieval practice). It shows these principles are not just classroom techniques — they are universal features of how complex mastery is built.

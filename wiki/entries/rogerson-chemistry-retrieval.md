# Rogerson's End-of-Class Chemistry Questions

**Concept illustrated:** [[testing-effect]] applied via [[closing-questions]].

Brian Rogerson taught introductory chemistry at Richard Stockton College over five semesters. In three of them he lectured conventionally. In the other two he made **one change**: the last ten minutes of each 75-minute class period were given over to a short-answer question about the material just covered — "Give two reasons that K is more reactive than Li," or the like. Students wrote the answer twice, kept one copy, and turned in the other. Rogerson reviewed the answers before next class (without grading them) and addressed common errors at the start of the next session.

## Results

- Fail-or-withdraw rate across the **three non-intervention semesters: 35%**.
- Fail-or-withdraw rate across the **two intervention semesters: 17%**.
- The reduction came from students who would have earned Ds and Fs now scoring Cs and Ds; A/B rates were roughly unchanged.

## Why it matters

This is not a lab experiment — it is an instructor varying one practice across semesters of a real college course. The intervention was about **10 minutes per class**, ungraded, in the form of short-answer questions that required thinking, not repetition. It roughly halved the course's failure rate.

The result also gestures at an equity dimension: the students who benefited most were the ones closest to failing — precisely the population that most urgently needs the time-cheap interventions [[small-teaching]] argues for.

# Barb's First Online Videos

**Concepts illustrated:** [[instructor-presence]], [[procedural-learning-system]]

When Barbara Oakley began recording videos for her massive open online course "Learning How to Learn," the results were far from polished. Watching her early footage, viewers might have thought she was staring down the barrel of a shotgun — stiff, awkward, and visibly uncomfortable on camera. Yet she persisted, and the course went on to become one of the most enrolled MOOCs in history.

The transformation happened through the same procedural learning mechanisms she teaches: hour by hour, expertise did not seem to change much, but over weeks and months the improvements were dramatic. Video-making skills, like piloting or bicycle-riding, require accumulated practice hours before the procedural system internalizes the patterns. The first step was simply making a bad video.

## Why it matters
This example demolishes the perfectionism barrier that prevents many teachers from creating online content. The quality of [[instructor-presence]] matters less than its existence — even a "cheesy amateur video" can earn student respect because the teacher is stepping into the students' digital world. The procedural system learns camera presence the same way it learns any skill: through practice, not through planning.

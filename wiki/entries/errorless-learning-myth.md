# The Myth of Errorless Learning

In the 1950s-60s, [[b-f-skinner]] advocated "errorless learning" in education — the idea that learners should be spoonfed material in small bites and immediately quizzed while the answer is still fresh, ensuring they never make mistakes. Modern research has thoroughly debunked this approach. Errors are not the enemy of learning; they are an integral part of it. When learners commit errors and receive corrective feedback, the errors are not learned — instead, the struggle and correction produce stronger and more durable retention than passive, error-free reception ever could.

## Elaboration

The errorless learning paradigm reflected a belief that errors are counterproductive and result from faulty instruction. This gave rise to techniques where learners were tested immediately after exposure, when the material was still on the tip of the tongue — guaranteeing correct answers but producing only short-term memory retrieval with no lasting benefit.

The evidence now shows that strategies highly likely to result in errors — like attempting to solve a problem before being taught, or taking a test on material not yet mastered — produce stronger learning and retention than passive instruction, provided corrective feedback is given. This is the [[generation-effect]] and [[priming-for-learning]] at work: the effort of the attempt, even when wrong, creates deeper processing.

Yet fear of failure persists and actively harms learning. Students with high fear of making errors on tests expend a significant portion of their [[working-memory]] capacity monitoring their own performance ("How am I doing? Am I making mistakes?"), leaving less capacity for actually solving the problems. A study of French sixth graders showed that teaching children that errors are a natural part of learning — not a sign of failure but of effort — significantly improved their working memory use on difficult tasks.

The cultural dimension matters: in Western achievement-oriented cultures, many learners view errors as indicators of low ability rather than as stepping stones. Carol Dweck's work on growth mindset and Anders Ericsson's work on deliberate practice both reinforce that failure is an essential experience on the path to mastery. It's not the failure that's desirable — it's the dauntless effort despite the risks, and the discovery of what works and what doesn't that sometimes only failure can reveal.

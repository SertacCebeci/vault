# Uncommon Sense Teaching

## Lead

Oakley, Sejnowski, and Rogowsky bridging neuroscience and K-12 / higher-ed classroom practice. The central argument: many commonsense intuitions about teaching are wrong; understanding how the brain actually learns — through neural-link formation, working- and long-term-memory dynamics, and the declarative/procedural pathways — lets teachers make small but powerful adjustments to instruction.

## Summary

*Uncommon Sense Teaching* bridges neuroscience and classroom practice, written by the creators of the massively popular "Learning How to Learn" MOOC ([[barbara-oakley]] and [[terrence-sejnowski]]) together with education professor Beth Rogowsky. The book translates brain science into practical, teacher-facing strategies for K-12 and higher education.

The central argument is that many commonsense intuitions about teaching are wrong, and that understanding how the brain actually learns — through neural link formation, the interplay of [[working-memory]] and [[long-term-memory]], and the two distinct learning pathways (declarative and procedural) — allows teachers to make small but powerful adjustments to their instruction. The book is organized around helping teachers understand *why* certain techniques work, not just *what* to do, with practical "Now You Try!" exercises and teaching tips throughout.

The authors emphasize inclusivity through the lens of working memory capacity differences (the "race-car" vs. "hiker" brain metaphor) and argue that both types of learners need different scaffolding. The book covers memory building, active learning through both declarative and procedural pathways, procrastination remedies, brain evolution, collaborative learning, online teaching, and lesson planning.

## Key ideas

### Chapter 1: Building Memory
- [[learn-it-link-it]] — the two-phase framework for how neural connections form and strengthen during learning; four-panel illustration showing neurons connecting (learn it) then strengthening and extending (link it); simplified dot-and-circle diagram
- [[hebbian-learning]] — "neurons that fire together near-simultaneously in time wire together" as the mechanism underlying link formation; choir analogy; 86 billion neurons, 10^14 synapses, millions of connections even for simple learning
- [[working-memory]] as "master trickster" — octopus/slanted-shelf metaphor; four-ball capacity ("quadropus"); students confuse having material in working memory with having it in [[long-term-memory]], leading to [[illusions-of-competence]]; rereading and highlighting explained as strategies that exploit WM's false-friend quality
- [[long-term-memory]] — neural links in neocortex; quadrillion-byte capacity; music-streaming analogy (bottleneck is access, not storage); 10^5 synapses dedicated per second; varied practice for reinforcing links
- [[recall-as-learning]] reframed as the primary tool for moving information from working memory to long-term memory; builds understanding far better than rereading, highlighting, or concept mapping; must be explicitly taught — students cannot intuit its value; Pooja Agarwal & Patrice Bain's *Powerful Teaching* cited for "no-stakes testing" framing
- [[worked-example-effect]] — John Sweller's research: sample problems help form mental templates, but students must also practice independently to build long-term links
- [[jot-recall]] — practical classroom retrieval techniques (jot notes, jot sketches, jot reading materials, jot previous materials)
- "Test anxiety" reframed as reaching into empty long-term memory — a memory-system diagnosis, not an emotional disorder

## Notable claims
- Children give up on their studies not because they lack a [[growth-mindset]] or haven't been taught in their preferred [[learning-styles-myth|learning style]], but because they honestly don't understand how to learn difficult topics (Preface, p. 10)
- Neuroscience gives "far more direct insight into the foundations of learning and education than any other discipline" — the book positions neuroscience as the primary lens, with psychology as an indirect but connected pathway (Preface, p. 10)
- Teachers often don't know foundational research insights such as [[recall-as-learning|retrieval practice]] and the value of teaching through both declarative and procedural pathways (Preface, p. 10)
- "Test anxiety" is sometimes code for "I reach into long-term memory and nothing's there" — the real problem is that students never transferred material from working memory to long-term memory (Ch. 1)
- Retrieval practice builds understanding of material far better than rereading, underlining, highlighting, or concept mapping (Ch. 1)
- The brain's information storage capacity is around a quadrillion bytes — the bottleneck is getting information in and out, not storage space (Ch. 1)
- Even learning something simple involves clumps of neurons making hundreds of thousands or millions of connections — learning is genuinely hard work at the neural level (Ch. 1)
- Students naturally gravitate toward ineffective study strategies because working memory's temporary hold feels indistinguishable from genuine long-term storage (Ch. 1)
- In all probability, no one has ever taught struggling students how to study effectively — the problem is systemic, not personal (Ch. 1)

### Chapter 2: Teaching Inclusively
- [[race-car-and-hiker-brains]] — central metaphor for working memory capacity differences; race cars get to answers fast, hikers process slowly but deeply; most learners are on a continuum
- [[working-memory]] deepened — central executive (octopus at front of brain), focus of attention (parietal lobe), phonological loop, visuospatial sketchpad; capacity ranges from 3 to 6+ items; develops with age (doubles between 4 and 14); cannot be trained generally but domain-specific practice increases apparent capacity
- [[long-term-memory-boosts-working-memory]] — LTM links extend apparent WM capacity through [[chunking]]; the "bigger balls" metaphor; education changes LTM, not WM, and this is what matters
- [[expertise-reversal-effect]] — too much guidance impedes experts; scaffolding must be added and removed appropriately
- [[scaffolded-instruction]] — differentiated strategies for hiker learners (step-by-step, written instructions, skeleton outlines) and race-car learners (complex problems, peer collaboration, sponge activities)
- Santiago Ramón y Cajal as archetypal hiker learner — poor WM, expelled from schools, became father of modern neuroscience; attributed success to his slower, more flexible thinking
- Susan Gathercole & Tracy Alloway — research quantifying WM capacity variation in children (3 of 30 seven-year-olds at four-year-old level, 3 at near-adult level)
- Music and studying: WM capacity determines tolerance — larger capacity allows studying with music; most should avoid music during math
- Note-taking: lesser-capacity students struggle to simultaneously take notes and comprehend; skeleton outlines, structure cues, and retrieve-taking help
- Practice has the most significant positive effect on struggling students — builds LTM through procedural pathway, creating automaticity

### Chapter 3: Active Learning — The Declarative Pathway
- [[active-learning-declarative]] — neuroscience-grounded definition: active learning supports creation and consolidation of neural links, not just physical activity; Freeman meta-analysis (1.5x failure rate in lecture vs. active); the critical caveat that the amount of "active" time varied 10-100% — breaks alone may be sufficient
- [[declarative-learning-system]] — the three-part neural architecture: [[working-memory]] (conductor) → [[hippocampus-as-index|hippocampus]] (fast indexer) + neocortex (vast permanent store); declarative vs. procedural memory distinction introduced
- [[hippocampus-as-index]] — hippocampus as book index, not storage; limited linking capacity (few months); dual-direction constraint (can't learn and rehearse simultaneously)
- [[consolidation]] deepened — three-stage diagram (disorganized → hippocampus-guided → stable neocortical); semanticization; sleep as synapse sealant; Erin Wamsley on wakeful rest; cramming's double failure
- [[brain-breaks]] — 20-40 second pauses for hippocampal offloading; age-plus-one attention rule; interval training analogy for learning
- [[bdnf-and-exercise]] — exercise produces BDNF for dendritic spine growth; neurogenesis; stress buffering; recess elimination as counterproductive
- [[recall-as-learning]] deepened — retrieval pulls out dendritic spines; Karpicke & Grimaldi on retrieval pervading all knowledge expression; retrieval speeds neocortical consolidation; Agarwal & Bain's Powerful Teaching; Natalie Wexler on content vs. comprehension skills
- [[learn-it-link-it]] deepened — link-it phase = active learning; group work is one mechanism but not the only one
- Teaching techniques: [[think-pair-share]] (Frank Lyman, 1981), [[whip-around]], error analysis with "pairing and repairing"

### Chapter 4: Remedies for Procrastination
- [[procrastination]] deepened — insular cortex as pain-processing center activating at mere anticipation of unpleasant tasks; the pain is real but temporary (dissipates once work begins); differential vulnerability for race-car vs. hiker students
- [[focused-and-diffuse-modes]] deepened — focused mode as WM actively connecting neurons; diffuse mode as WM stepping aside for random subconscious connections; frustration as the signal to switch modes; the "paper airplane" analogy (effort to launch, then it soars)
- [[pomodoro-technique]] deepened — classroom implementation with age-adjusted timing (age + 1 minute); break quality matters (phones overwrite hippocampal contents); gamification (Forest app); teacher modeling; applies to grading too
- [[race-car-and-hiker-brains]] deepened — race cars can be worst procrastinators due to early-years ease; school-transition vulnerability; hikers learn discipline earlier but need more practice time
- [[hard-start-technique]] — test-taking strategy leveraging diffuse-mode background processing: start with hardest question, switch to easy ones, return later with progress
- [[intermediate-task-scaffolding]] — breaking assignments into staged deadlines with partner review and feedback loops; WWII essay example; rubric design principles (criteria, explicit expectations, rating scales); teacher-as-coach model
- Francesco Cirillo — inventor of Pomodoro Technique (1980s), named after tomato-shaped timer
- Deep learning requires creative new neural connections — not simple neighbor-linking but wild testing of different connection patterns; this takes time, which procrastination eliminates
- Motivation teaser: "commonsense" motivational approach of making learning fun can backfire; detailed treatment deferred to Ch. 7

### Chapter 5: How Human Brains Evolved — and Why This Matters for Your Teaching
- [[biologically-primary-and-secondary-knowledge]] — David Geary's framework: primary abilities (face recognition, language) are pre-wired by evolution; secondary abilities (reading, math) require deliberate neural repurposing. The more secondary the material, the more [[direct-instruction]] students need.
- [[neuronal-recycling-hypothesis]] — Stanislas Dehaene's theory: secondary learning repurposes brain regions evolved for related primary functions (letters → occipitotemporal cortex, numbers → intraparietal cortex); constrained plasticity across all cultures
- [[brain-development-timeline]] — neuropil formation peaking at age 2, then pruning moving back-to-front through childhood/adolescence; prefrontal cortex (planning/judgment) matures last; environment shapes pruning intensity
- [[direct-instruction]] — "I do, we do, you do, extend it" model explicitly mapped onto [[learn-it-link-it]] (I do + we do = learn it; you do + extend it = link it); not the same as lecture; Barak Rosenshine's principles of effective instruction; evidence that countries using more direct instruction show higher achievement scores
- The teacher-directed/student-directed continuum: direct instruction leads to student autonomy, not away from it; student-directed approaches too early overload WM and frustrate novices
- [[desirable-difficulty]] — Robert Bjork cited for the argument that secondary material requires productive struggle; [[deliberate-practice]] converges
- [[curse-of-knowledge]] deepened as "curse of expertise" — Roman Hardgrave's two corrective steps for teachers
- [[scaffolded-instruction]] connected to the primary/secondary framework: premature scaffold removal leaves neocortical links unstable; too much scaffolding lets students off the hook

### Chapter 6: Active Learning — The Procedural Pathway
- [[procedural-learning-system]] — the brain's second learning pathway via basal ganglia; nonconscious, slow to learn, fast to deploy; 100ms cortex-basal ganglia-cortex loop; stores sequences, habits, pattern recognition, and intuitions
- [[declarative-procedural-seesaw]] — the two systems act like a seesaw: engaging one de-emphasizes the other during learning, but knowledge from both systems is complementary; learning through both produces flexible, fast, powerful knowledge
- [[procedural-front-and-back-door]] — front door (goal-directed, from prefrontal cortex) and back door (habitual, from sensory cortex); basal ganglia switch between modes; value function drives non-conscious decision-making
- Michael Ullman's declarative-procedural theory of language learning — both systems can learn the same material in complementary ways; each excels at different information types
- Dyslexia reframed as a procedural-system deficit — not just a reading problem but a fundamental issue with automatizing skills; some compensate via exceptional declarative memory
- Race-car/hiker connection: race-car learners lean declarative, hikers lean procedural; ADHD/dyslexia predispose toward declarative reliance; lesser WM predisposes toward procedural
- [[interleaving]] deepened as a procedural-learning strategy — builds pattern discrimination; similar categories benefit most; caution against premature interleaving (Christodoulou); concept attainment as related technique
- [[desirable-difficulty]] deepened — peg-leg metaphor; gesture-based encoding for deeper neural activity; neural link density metaphor
- [[schemas]] introduced — "neural shelves" integrating procedural and declarative knowledge; settle in neocortex; iceberg metaphor (vocabulary = tip, application = depth); enable [[transfer]]; may underpin Bloom's taxonomy
- [[spaced-repetition]] deepened — supports both declarative and procedural learning; allows cross-system link connection; imprecise spacing guidelines; intensive courses may be counterproductive
- [[verbal-explanation-vs-understanding]] — Dunbar's Newtonian motion study; David Geary: "deep conceptual understanding and explicit statements are not the same thing"; procedural learners may struggle to verbalize knowledge they genuinely possess
- [[judicious-homework]] — short, frequent, interleaved assignments; 7 problems instead of 40; 2 current + 3 recent + 2 distant past; five practical guidelines; homework as procedural practice ground
- [[concept-attainment]] — teaching method using yes/no examples to build schemas through guided discovery; related to interleaving
- Handwriting over typing for learning — Audrey van der Meer research; writing by hand creates more sensorimotor activation and deeper encoding than typing
- The value of teaching students about their two learning pathways — "explanation" (declarative) and "practice" (procedural)

### Chapter 7: Building Community Through Habits
- [[procedural-learning-system]] extended to classroom management — everyday routines (entering, lining up, transitioning) can be taught as procedures and practiced to automaticity, freeing [[working-memory]] for learning
- [[dopamine-and-learning]] — unexpected rewards trigger dopamine surges that strengthen synaptic connections being formed; expected rewards drive motivation but not learning; dopamine temporarily halts the [[declarative-procedural-seesaw]], enabling cooperative dual-system learning; dopamine crash from punitive responses actively signals neurons to disconnect
- [[temporal-discounting]] — brains discount delayed rewards; steeper discounting in teenagers linked to the striatum/basal ganglia; explains why distant grades are weak motivators
- [[classroom-procedures]] — teach procedures using the same I do/we do/you do framework as academic content; consistency is paramount for procedural consolidation
- Schemas and motivation: students often dislike subjects because they lack schemas, not because the subject is inherently uninteresting; practice builds schemas → schemas make learning easier → competence becomes rewarding (Wang & Morris: "what interests us takes time to develop")
- Doug Lemov (Teach Like a Champion 2.0) quoted on the paradox of classroom culture: ignoring behavior leads to talking about nothing else; intentional culture fades into the background
- Harry and Rosemary Wong (The First Days of School) cited for first-day planning: anticipate and address students' questions before they arise
- Teaching tip: impostor syndrome is common even among top students; discussing it openly normalizes the experience; overly cocky low-ability students may need real-world feedback to recalibrate
- Student resistance has multiple causes (bad days, peer pressure, past negative experiences); proactive measures include specific praise, normalizing errors, circulating to check understanding before cold-calling

### Chapter 8: Linking Learners — The Power of Collaborative Learning
- [[eustress-and-learning]] — chronic stress (distress) damages health; transient stress (eustress) enhances cognition, working memory, and learning. The relationship follows an inverted-U curve: moderate glucocorticoids grease neural connections, excessive stress causes them to burn and stick.
- [[socioemotional-learning]] — SEL develops self-awareness, self-management, decision-making, relationship skills, and social awareness; partly biologically primary but sophisticated social skills are secondary and need explicit instruction. Neural basis spans prefrontal cortex, amygdala, hippocampus, and basal ganglia.
- [[social-buffering]] — supportive group members reduce stress hormones to the optimal "happy middle"; obstructive members can increase stress. This is the primary neurochemical argument for collaborative learning.
- [[collaborative-learning]] — defined by common goal, equal workload distribution, and close contact; five components from cooperative learning: positive interdependence, individual accountability, face-to-face interaction, social skills, group processing.
- [[collaborative-learning-procedures]] — standard procedures for group formation (teacher-assigned groups of 3-4), activity execution (six steps including countdown timers and "three before me"), behavioral expectations, and defined roles (previewer, clarifier, connector, summarizer).
- [[collaboration-clinics]] — periodic ten-minute sessions for resolving group dysfunction; process-focused reflection, brainstorming strategies, no names.
- Competition reframed — moderate occasional competition is eustress; excessive competition is distress. Don't eliminate all competition.
- Empathy + wisdom — empathy without boundaries enables manipulation and codependency. Teaching boundaries alongside empathy is essential.
- [[brainwriting]] — individual-first brainstorming then group augmentation; compensates for larger groups suppressing creativity (Suparna Rajaram research).
- Group size trade-off — larger groups produce bigger accomplishments; smaller groups (2-3) produce more creative results.

### Chapter 9: Online Teaching with Personality and Flair
- [[multimedia-learning-theory]] — Richard Mayer's dual-channel theory: combining visual and verbal information leverages [[working-memory]]'s hearing and seeing components for more efficient learning. Practical principles: speak clearly at 150-185 wpm, introduce complex material gradually, eliminate extraneous on-screen material, don't read long text aloud (interference, not reinforcement).
- Synchronous vs. asynchronous teaching — two primary modes of online instruction with complementary strengths. Synchronous provides real-time accountability and interaction but causes "Zoom fatigue" (mental exhaustion from degraded audio/video signals and incomplete body language). Asynchronous provides self-pacing and reusability but loses [[instructor-presence]] unless designed carefully.
- [[instructor-presence]] — one of the strongest predictors of student engagement and course completion online. Even amateur videos with the teacher's face build connection; instructor personality and humor matter more than production quality. Aaron Johnson: "the courses with the lowest student absenteeism are the courses where teachers are the most present."
- [[top-down-and-bottom-up-attention]] — two attentional pathways: top-down (voluntary, prefrontal cortex, willpower-dependent) and bottom-up (involuntary, sensory-driven, effortless). Good online video design uses sporadic bottom-up attention grabbers (motion, sound, humor, visual surprise) to sustain engagement without depleting students' willpower.
- Synchronous teaching rules: set ground rules co-created with students; the sixty-second rule (hook students in the first minute); the five-minute rule (explicit instruction < 5 min before active response); the nowhere-to-hide rule (breakout groups, polls, cold-calling after every brief instruction segment). Doug Lemov's cold-calling research: it increases comfort with participation, not discomfort.
- Asynchronous video design: 3-12 minute videos with hook, explanation, and wrap-up; "the first step to making a good video is making a bad video"; scripts or half-scripts improve quality and enable captions; instructor should be visible for at least 1-2 minutes per video.
- [[flipped-classroom-online]] — hybrid model mapping [[learn-it-link-it]] across the week: Monday (asynchronous, "I do"), Wednesday (synchronous, "We do"), Friday (student-directed, "You do"). Race-car students can jump ahead to Friday; hiker students get extra reinforcement on Wednesday.
- [[richard-mayer]] — educational psychologist whose decades of multimedia research underpin the chapter's design principles.
- Online [[classroom-procedures]] — ground rules (mute on entry, cameras on, no pajamas), question procedures (chat room > hand raise), Simon Says for platform familiarization, co-created rules for buy-in.
- Online collaboration tools (Padlet, Kahoot!, Quizlet, GoNoodle, PeerWise) and discussion forums as asynchronous [[recall-as-learning|retrieval practice]] vehicles; embedded quiz questions in videos for "just in time" assessment.
- Audio quality is "51% of the video" — good microphone matters more than good camera; voice coaching for instructors whose procedurally-learned voice habits may be grating on camera.

## Questions raised
- How does the race-car/hiker distinction relate to the working-memory creativity finding from [[learning-how-to-learn]]? (Ch. 2 notes that tiredness/lessened WM may heighten creative insight — direct connection)
- How does teacher-directed vs. student-directed instruction interact with WM capacity? (Ch. 2 notes lesser-capacity students do worse with student-directed learning)
- How do habits and motivation interact with the procedural system? (Answered in Ch. 7: dopamine from unexpected rewards accelerates procedural consolidation; expected rewards drive motivation via temporal discounting)
- How does collaborative learning leverage the dopamine/procedural framework? (Answered in Ch. 8: social buffering moderates stress hormones; collaborative learning builds procedural social skills; competition provides eustress-driven dopamine; bullying produces dopamine that reinforces antisocial behavior)

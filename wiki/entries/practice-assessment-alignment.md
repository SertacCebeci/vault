# Practice-Assessment Alignment

**Whatever cognitive task you ask students to perform on high-stakes assessments, they should have practiced that exact task in low-stakes contexts.** The principle sounds obvious and is pervasively violated. Instructors lecture all semester, assign multiple-choice quizzes, then give a final exam of essays on synthesis — and wonder why performance collapses. The practice did not rehearse the retrieval format the final demanded.

## What it connects

This insight sits at the intersection of two wiki threads:

- [[testing-effect]] — tells us *that* retrieval practice strengthens memory.
- [[transfer]] — tells us that cognitive skills tend to be *context-bound* until specifically practiced across contexts.

Together they yield a design rule: the *form* of retrieval practice shapes the *form* of memory available at test time. Retrieval in multiple-choice form strengthens multiple-choice retrieval; retrieval via writing strengthens retrieval via writing. The empirical spine is [[roediger-butler-art-history]], where short-answer practice produced 47% recall on a short-answer final while multiple-choice practice produced only 36%.

## How to apply it

Work backwards from your summative assessment:

- **If the final is short-answer / essay** → opening and closing questions should be written short-answer, even if informal. Short multiple-choice warm-ups will not substitute.
- **If the final is multiple-choice** → classroom polling (clickers, raised hands on A/B/C/D options) is the matching practice, and short-answer practice alone will under-prepare students for the format.
- **If the final is problem-solving** (math, chemistry, coding) → the practice retrieval should itself be *problem-solving under retrieval conditions*, not recognition of definitions. This is where [[rogerson-chemistry-retrieval]]'s end-of-class problem questions land.
- **If the final is essay synthesis** → practice should include repeated short acts of synthesis, not just factual retrieval. Don't expect transfer from one kind of retrieval to a different one.

## The contrapositive

If you *can't* align practice to assessment in a given unit, consider whether the assessment is testing what you actually taught. Misalignment usually means one of two things: either the assessment is testing a skill the course never rehearsed (unfair), or the course is spending time on skills the assessment doesn't measure (inefficient).

# Reconsolidation

Reconsolidation is the process by which retrieving a memory from [[long-term-memory]] makes it *pliable* again — modifiable, updatable, and available for strengthening. When you recall something after a lapse of time, the act of retrieval both strengthens the memory traces and reopens them for revision, enabling you to connect the recalled material to more recent learning and to reinforce the [[retrieval-cues]] that help you find it later. This is the mechanism through which [[recall-as-learning|retrieval practice]] modifies and strengthens learning.

## Elaboration

Reconsolidation explains why effortful retrieval is so much more powerful than re-reading or passive review. When you retrieve a memory from short-term memory (as in rapid-fire massed practice), little mental effort is required, little reconsolidation occurs, and the underlying representation is barely strengthened. But when you retrieve after some forgetting has set in — as happens with [[spaced-repetition|spaced practice]] — you must reconstruct the knowledge from long-term storage. This focused, effortful recall makes the most salient aspects clearer, and the consequent reconsolidation reinforces meaning, strengthens connections to prior knowledge, bolsters cues and retrieval routes, and weakens competing routes.

The jump school analogy is vivid: on day two, when Mia is put on the spot to execute a parachute landing fall, she struggles to recall the correct posture from the day before. The effort to reconstruct what she learned is ragged, but in making it, critical elements of the maneuver come clearer and are reconsolidated for stronger memory. By contrast, if she had practiced the same fall fifty times in a row on day one, she would have been leaning on short-term memory — showing gratifying improvement quickly, but with little reconsolidation occurring.

The paradox is central to [[desirable-difficulty]]: the more effort required to retrieve (or, in effect, relearn) something, the more effective the reconsolidation will be in shaping permanent knowledge. The more you've forgotten, the more powerful relearning becomes.

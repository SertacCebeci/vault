# Beth's Literary Devices Scavenger Hunt

**Concepts illustrated:** [[interleaving]], [[procedural-learning-system]]

Coauthor Beth Rogowsky used direct instruction to teach literary devices — similes, metaphors, hyperbole, personification, onomatopoeia, alliteration. After presenting each device with definitions and examples ("I do"), she had students create their own examples to share with the class ("We do"). Students could easily recite definitions and give examples when it came time for the test ("You do"). But they often could not demonstrate true conceptual understanding: they could not generate original examples when terms appeared randomly, nor spot them scattered through a text — even when the examples were strikingly obvious.

Beth realized students needed more practice, not more memorization. She had them perform a scavenger hunt for literary devices each time they read a new piece of literature. The devices were interleaved throughout the reading, making the learning slow and frustrating at first. But as students progressed, they became faster and more accurate at spotting devices in context. Months later, Beth could see the devices appearing masterfully in her students' own writing. They could even explain how and why they used each device purposefully.

## Why it matters

This example demonstrates the gap between declarative knowledge (reciting definitions) and procedural mastery (recognizing and deploying devices in context). Blocked practice — studying each device separately — produced students who could parrot definitions but not apply them. Interleaved practice forced students to discriminate between similar devices in varied contexts, building the procedural pattern recognition that declarative study alone could not achieve.

# Surgical Residents Microsurgery Spacing Study

**Concepts illustrated:** [[spaced-repetition]], [[massed-practice]], [[desirable-difficulty]]

Thirty-eight surgical residents took a series of four short lessons in microsurgery — specifically, how to reattach tiny blood vessels. Each lesson included instruction followed by practice. Half the residents completed all four lessons in a single day, which is the normal in-service training schedule. The other half completed the same four lessons spaced a week apart.

One month after their last session, all residents were tested. Those whose lessons had been spaced a week apart outperformed their colleagues on every measure: elapsed time to complete a surgery, number of hand movements, and success at reattaching the severed, pulsating aortas of live rats. The difference was dramatic — the residents who had crammed all four sessions into a single day not only scored lower on all measures, but 16 percent of them damaged the rats' vessels beyond repair and were unable to complete their surgeries. None of the spaced group failed in this way.

## Why it matters

This illustration brings the spaced-practice research out of the classroom and into a domain where the stakes are life and death. The one-day format is how most professional continuing education actually works — compressed seminars, weekend workshops, intensive boot camps. Yet the evidence shows this massed format produces dramatically inferior skill retention compared to the same content spread over time. When the skill in question is reattaching blood vessels, the cost of massed practice isn't just a lower test score — it's a failed surgery.

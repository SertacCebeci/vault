# Active Learning (Declarative Pathway)

Active learning is instruction that engages students in the process of learning through activities and discussion, as opposed to passively listening to an expert. From a neuroscience perspective, well-done active learning supports the creation and [[consolidation]] of neural links in [[long-term-memory]] that underlie both basic factual knowledge and higher-order conceptual understanding. It is often the mechanism of the *link it* phase in [[learn-it-link-it]].

## Elaboration

A common misconception is that "active" means physically active — building models, making crafts, doing hands-on projects. But draping papier-mache around a balloon to make a Grecian urn teaches nothing about Greek culture. Genuine active learning means cognitively active: students are retrieving, connecting, and consolidating information in their [[declarative-learning-system]].

A meta-analysis by Scott Freeman and colleagues found that students in traditional lecture classes were 1.5 times as likely to fail compared to those in active learning courses, with grades improving by 6 percent. However, a critical nuance is buried in this finding: the amount of "active" time varied between 10 and 100 percent of class time, and researchers did not measure the actual amount. The paper could equally have been titled "break up your instruction with a few active breaks, and your students will do better." Even brief pauses for retrieval or collaboration can produce significant gains because they allow the hippocampus to offload information to the neocortex.

Active learning is not synonymous with all learning. Not all learning requires activity; sometimes direct instruction is essential, especially for novice learners with lesser [[working-memory]] capacity. The key is that active learning integrates retrieval, [[brain-breaks]], and collaboration to build robust neural links — it is the complement to instruction, not a replacement for it.

Factual knowledge matters more than is often assumed. Students need a foundation of facts and definitions — a "knowledge party" of accumulated associations — before higher-order thinking can flourish. Active learning that skips over this foundational content produces superficial understanding, as when students actively discuss the Civil War without retaining basic facts.

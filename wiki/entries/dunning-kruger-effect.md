# Dunning-Kruger Effect

The Dunning-Kruger effect is the finding that incompetent people systematically overestimate their own competence, precisely because they lack the skills needed to recognize their deficiency. Named after psychologists [[david-dunning]] and Justin Kruger, whose landmark 1999 paper was titled "Unskilled and Unaware of It." The effect is not simply that unskilled people are overconfident — it is that the same skill required to perform well in a domain is the skill required to recognize good performance.

## Elaboration

In Dunning and Kruger's experiments, students who scored at the 12th percentile on a logic test rated their own logical reasoning ability at the 68th percentile. More troublingly, when shown other students' superior answers alongside their own, bottom-quartile performers did not revise their self-assessment downward — they actually raised their already-inflated estimates. The incompetence that led to poor performance also prevented them from recognizing competence in others.

However, the effect is not irreversible. In a crucial third experiment, bottom-quartile students who received ten minutes of training in logic (the domain being tested) became dramatically more accurate at estimating their own performance. Students who received training in an unrelated domain showed no improvement. This demonstrates that [[metacognition]] is domain-specific and trainable — you can learn to calibrate your self-assessment, but only by building competence in the relevant area.

The Dunning-Kruger effect has three reinforcing mechanisms: (1) people rarely receive honest negative feedback in everyday life, (2) failure can always be attributed to external causes while success requires everything to go right, and (3) some people are poor at reading others' performance, making comparative judgment difficult.

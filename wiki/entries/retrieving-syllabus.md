# The Retrieving Syllabus

**Use the syllabus as a durable, in-the-room retrieval cue all semester.** Most syllabi appear on day one, get skimmed for the due dates, and are then buried in a folder until grade-dispute season. Lang argues for a **living syllabus**: a document students bring to every class and use periodically for retrieval practice.

## How to do it

1. **Write a detailed course-schedule section.** For every class period, include not just a topic title but a sentence or two describing what that day will actually cover and how it fits the larger unit. The syllabus then functions as a map of the course — and maps support retrieval.
2. **Require the syllabus in class every day.**
3. **Point at a past date.** At the opening or closing of a session, ask students to pull out the syllabus, find a specific earlier date, and write what they remember about the concepts covered that day. Informal (in their notebooks) or formal (a collected writing exercise) or oral (a class whip-around).

## Why it works

- **Targeted retrieval practice.** Pointing at a specific past class date is a much sharper retrieval cue than "what did we cover earlier?" It is the difference between "what did you do last week?" and "what did you do Tuesday afternoon?" — the second is easier to answer because the cue is more specific.
- **Reinforces the course's conceptual map.** Each retrieval episode re-links individual sessions to the larger structure, reducing the "bag of unconnected topics" feeling students often have near the end of a semester.
- **Effectively free.** No new preparation, no new handout, no new platform. It exploits a document every course already has.

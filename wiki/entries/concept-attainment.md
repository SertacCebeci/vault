# Concept Attainment

Concept attainment is a teaching methodology in which the teacher presents a series of *yes* examples and *no* examples of a concept, and students figure out the common attributes and generate a potential definition. The teacher then provides further examples to test and solidify the students' hypothesis. Students develop their own understanding of the concept through guided discovery rather than being told the definition upfront.

## How it works

The method leverages both the [[declarative-learning-system]] and the [[procedural-learning-system]]. Students form hypotheses declaratively as they analyze examples, but the repeated discrimination between yes and no examples engages procedural pattern recognition — the same mechanism that makes [[interleaving]] effective. The progressive refinement of the definition through more challenging examples builds [[schemas]] from the ground up.

The sequence typically follows four steps: (1) present yes/no examples and have students identify common attributes; (2) provide a template for students to record observations; (3) add more challenging examples to test the emerging definition; (4) have students apply their learning to a new task, such as generating their own examples.

Concept attainment is related to interleaving because students practice discriminating between items that do and do not belong to a category. It can be used at the start of a lesson to hook students before moving to [[direct-instruction]], scaffolding the transition from inductive to explicit learning.

## Concepts applied

- [[procedural-learning-system]] — repeated discrimination between yes/no examples builds procedural pattern recognition
- [[interleaving]] — concept attainment is structurally similar to interleaving, forcing comparison between similar categories
- [[schemas]] — students build internal schemas by identifying key attributes across varied examples
- [[direct-instruction]] — concept attainment can precede or supplement direct instruction

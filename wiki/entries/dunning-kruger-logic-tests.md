# Dunning-Kruger Logic Tests

**Concepts illustrated:** [[dunning-kruger-effect]], [[metacognition]], [[calibration]]

[[david-dunning]] and Justin Kruger conducted a series of three experiments that reveal the depth of metacognitive failure in incompetent performers:

**Experiment 1:** Students took a logic test and rated their own performance. The least competent students were the most out of touch: those who scored at the 12th percentile rated their general logical reasoning ability at the 68th percentile — a five-fold overestimation.

**Experiment 2:** After the initial test and self-rating, students were shown other students' answers alongside their own and asked to re-estimate how many questions they'd answered correctly. Bottom-quartile students did not revise their self-assessment downward after seeing superior work. They actually *raised* their already-inflated estimates — exposure to competence made them more, not less, confident.

**Experiment 3:** Students took a new logic test, again overestimating their performance. Then half received ten minutes of training in logic (specifically, how to evaluate syllogisms). The other half received training in an unrelated task. All were asked to re-estimate their performance. The bottom-quartile students who received logic training became dramatically more accurate — they could now recognize their errors and calibrate their self-assessment. Those who received unrelated training held firm to their mistaken conviction that they'd performed well.

## Why it matters
The three-experiment sequence demonstrates both the problem and the solution. The problem: incompetence is self-masking — the very skill you lack is the skill you'd need to recognize the lack. The solution: domain-specific training improves [[metacognition]] — you can learn to calibrate, but only by building competence in the relevant area, not through generic confidence-boosting or exposure to others' work. This is why [[calibration]] tools must be domain-specific: testing yourself in the material you're learning, not just general self-reflection.

# Fuller's Line-Learning Process

**Concepts illustrated:** [[recall-as-learning]], [[spaced-repetition]], [[interleaving]], [[generation-effect]], [[elaboration]]

Nathaniel Fuller, a professional actor at the Guthrie Theater in Minneapolis, has refined a line-learning process that seamlessly blends every major desirable difficulty. When learning a new role, he covers a page of the script with a blank sheet and attempts to speak his lines from memory, using the other characters' lines as cues. He checks his accuracy, corrects errors, and moves on — a direct application of retrieval practice with immediate feedback.

He spaces his practice deliberately: when returns begin to diminish, he stops for the day and returns the next morning, trusting that the material will come back a little better each time. As he progresses through the script, he constantly moves between familiar and new material, stitching scenes together by working backward from the end to the beginning — practicing the less familiar final scenes first, then linking them to earlier ones. This interleaving of old and new keeps him retrieving across the full arc of the play.

Fuller also records every other character's lines into a handheld recorder, voiced in character. He tucks the recorder in his hand, plays the other characters' lines, and speaks his own from memory — adding physicality, movement, and emotional context. At home he imagines his living room as the stage, walking through blocking and reacting to imaginary props. This multi-layered encoding — visual (the script layout), auditory (the recorder), kinesthetic (the blocking), emotional (the character's motivations) — is a rich form of elaboration that builds multiple retrieval cues.

## Why it matters

Fuller's process is the most complete real-world integration of the book's principles in a single practitioner. He uses retrieval practice, spacing, interleaving, generation, and elaboration not as separate techniques but as an organic workflow. His case also reinforces a key point from Chapter 7: he does not have an exceptional memory — he has exceptional methods. The struggle of retrieval is what makes the learning stick.

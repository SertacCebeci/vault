# Transfer

Transfer is the ability to apply knowledge learned in one context to novel, different situations. Concepts and problem-solving methods learned in one field can share deep structural similarities with those in another. The more robust a learner's [[schemas]], the easier transfer becomes — but the more dissimilar the new material, the harder transfer is.

## Elaboration

Transfer happens because chunks encode not just specific content but underlying *patterns*. When you grasp one chunk, you may find it can be related to similar chunks not only in the same field but in very different fields. The more chunks and schemas you have in your mental library, the more easily you can find these cross-domain connections. This is closely related to [[interleaving]] between subjects — when you interleave practice across different fields, you strengthen the ability to recognize structural similarities and apply knowledge flexibly.

The connection to [[metaphor-and-analogy]] is direct: analogy is essentially the mechanism of transfer, mapping structure from a familiar domain to an unfamiliar one. And [[einstellung]] is what blocks transfer — existing rigid patterns in one domain prevent you from seeing how a different domain's approach might apply.

There is a "curse of specificity" in learning: improvements usually involve only precisely what the student has learned, with little to no gain in tasks that might seem nearly identical. The cure is training on a large set of varied samples. [[Interleaving]] assists with transfer because it forces learners to practice discriminating between contexts and selecting the appropriate approach. Retrieval practice may also assist with transfer — even far transfer to quite different domains — possibly by strengthening the underlying schema structure.

Transfer can be surprisingly difficult even for experts. Master teachers asked to transfer face-to-face teaching skills to online instruction during the COVID pandemic often struggled. Students should not be judged too harshly for struggling to transfer new knowledge to varied scenarios. The best approach is to introduce students to as many different uses of the content as possible, enlarging their [[schemas]] and extending their transfer range.

Richard Feynman was inspired for his Nobel Prize-winning work by watching someone throw a dinner plate in a cafeteria. This kind of serendipitous transfer becomes more likely the richer your chunk library across diverse fields.

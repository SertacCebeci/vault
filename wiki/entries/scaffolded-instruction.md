# Scaffolded Instruction

Scaffolded instruction is temporary, targeted support that teachers provide to help students with lesser [[working-memory]] capacity or less background knowledge reach the same learning outcomes as their peers. Just as physical scaffolding supports workers climbing a building and is removed once the structure is complete, instructional scaffolding is added, adjusted, and withdrawn as learners develop competence.

## How it works

Differentiation — teaching the same content using approaches matched to individual learners — sits at the core of scaffolding. Different [[working-memory]] capacities and background knowledge levels mean that a one-size-fits-all approach inevitably fails: it overloads hikers and under-challenges race cars. Effective scaffolding addresses both ends of the spectrum.

The neurological rationale deepens through the lens of [[biologically-primary-and-secondary-knowledge]]. Secondary material requires the brain to repurpose circuits evolved for other purposes — a difficult neural task that benefits from external guidance. The more secondary the material, the more scaffolding students need. [[direct-instruction]]'s "I do, we do, you do" progression is scaffolding in action: the teacher initially takes full responsibility for the cognitive work, then gradually transfers it to the student.

**For hiker-type learners** (lesser WM capacity):
- Meet individually or in small groups to reteach concepts
- Allow more time for tasks and skill practice
- Break problems into steps with examples at each stage
- Use criteria that match *and then extend* skill levels
- Provide instructions one at a time with step checks
- Write instructions on the board as external WM support
- Offer skeleton outlines and structure cues for note-taking
- Take mini-breaks during new material for note review
- Use mnemonics to compress information into fewer WM slots

**For race-car learners** (larger WM capacity):
- Go beyond simple factual questions to concept connections
- Provide opportunities for peer collaboration and challenge
- Create complex, multilayered problems (not more of the same)
- Offer meaningful "sponge activities" for extra time
- Accelerate learning with individualized software

The [[expertise-reversal-effect]] governs the timing: scaffolding that helps a novice becomes redundant processing for a developing expert, so support must be progressively withdrawn as long-term memory links strengthen.

A word of caution: when scaffolds are removed too early, newly learned information has not been solidly reinforced in the neocortex. Students may freeze on homework or exams because they needed more time in the "we do" phase. Conversely, providing too much scaffolding can let students off the hook — they learn that admitting defeat gets their work done for them. The balance involves guiding students toward answers without simply giving answers.

# Brain Breaks

Brain breaks are brief pauses during instruction that allow the [[hippocampus-as-index|hippocampus]] to turn away from incoming information and begin offloading — rehearsing its indexing links to the neocortex. Even seconds-long rest periods during a learning experience trigger memory-related neural activity that predicts later test performance. These breaks are not wasted time; they are essential infrastructure for [[consolidation]].

## Elaboration

The hippocampus faces a fundamental constraint: it cannot simultaneously receive new information from [[working-memory]] and rehearse existing information to the neocortex. During instruction, the hippocampus is oriented toward the "conductor" (working memory), absorbing indexing links. During a brain break, it can turn to face "Neo" (the neocortex) and begin strengthening the connections it has just indexed.

Brain breaks can be remarkably short — as little as twenty to forty seconds. They occur naturally when students transition to collaborative activities like [[think-pair-share]] or when they pause to think on their own. A study found that fifteen minutes of eyes-closed rest after learning enhanced memory far more than immediately moving to the next task. But even sub-minute pauses provide consolidation time.

A good rule of thumb for attention spans is the student's age plus one minute — a seven-year-old can focus for about eight minutes before needing an interlude. But this varies with material difficulty and student age.

The dual demand on teachers — make the mind work hard through retrieval AND let the mind rest for hippocampal offloading — mirrors interval training in athletics. Just as muscles and neurons are both excitable tissues that develop through alternation of intense effort and recovery, learning develops through hard mental work paired with mental rest. This is sometimes called the "meathead theory of brain function."

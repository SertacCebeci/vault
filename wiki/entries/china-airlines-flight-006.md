# China Airlines Flight 006

**Concepts illustrated:** [[system-1-and-system-2]], [[metacognition]], [[illusions-of-competence]]

On a winter day in 1985, China Airlines Flight 006 — a Boeing 747 carrying passengers from Taipei to Los Angeles — was cruising at 41,000 feet over the Pacific when engine number 4 lost power. Rather than following the prescribed procedure (taking manual control and descending below 30,000 feet to restart the engine), the captain kept the autopilot engaged at altitude and attempted a restart. The asymmetrical thrust from the missing outboard engine began rolling the plane. The captain was aware of deceleration but not of the extent of the bank — his vestibular reflex (System 1) told him the plane was flying level, even as it wasn't.

As the bank increased, the plane descended through 37,000 feet into clouds, obscuring the horizon. The captain disengaged the autopilot, but by then the plane had rolled beyond 45 degrees, inverted, and entered an uncontrolled dive. The crew were confused — they understood erratic behavior but didn't realize they'd overturned. They could no longer distinguish thrust from individual engines and concluded all engines had quit. The dive was evident from flight gauges, but the angle was so extreme the crew decided the gauges had failed. At 11,000 feet they broke through clouds and were astonished to see the earth rushing toward them. Both pilots hauled back on the stick, managing to level off. They diverted to San Francisco. Inspection revealed the forces had bent the wings permanently upward, broken two landing gear struts, and torn away stabilizer sections — five times normal gravity.

The incident illustrates "spatial disorientation": the captain's System 1 (sensory/vestibular perception of level flight) overrode System 2 (instrument readings), and when things spiraled further out of control, he trusted his constructed narrative of what was happening over the objective data from his instruments. The NTSB report concluded that the captain let himself become preoccupied with the engine restart rather than monitoring all instruments — a failure to engage systematic System 2 scanning.

## Why it matters
This is a life-or-death demonstration of the same mechanism that makes students overconfident in their learning: System 1 provides a confident, intuitive sense of the situation that feels trustworthy — but is wrong. The prescription is the same in cockpits and classrooms: develop the habit of checking objective instruments ([[calibration]]) rather than trusting your subjective sense of how things are going.

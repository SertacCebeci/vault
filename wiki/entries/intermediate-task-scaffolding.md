# Intermediate-Task Scaffolding

Intermediate-task scaffolding is a teaching strategy that combats student [[procrastination]] by breaking large assignments into a sequence of smaller tasks with separate deadlines, each building toward the final product. Rather than assigning one distant due date and hoping students manage their time, the teacher structures the process itself.

## How it works

A complex assignment — an essay, research project, or portfolio — is decomposed into intermediate stages, each with its own deadline, feedback loop, and often a collaborative component. For a multi-week essay, the stages might progress from thesis drafting through research citations, outlining, body paragraphs, revision, and final submission.

Each stage accomplishes several things simultaneously: it forces students to start early (bypassing procrastination), provides opportunities for formative feedback, builds the assignment incrementally so the final product is higher quality, and models a life skill — breaking large projects into manageable tasks. The technique also leverages [[consolidation]] by spreading work over days, giving the brain time to form and strengthen neural links between study sessions.

Pairing intermediate tasks with partner review adds an accountability layer and activates retrieval practice, since explaining work to a peer requires getting information *out* of one's head. Combining intermediate tasks with the [[pomodoro-technique]] — setting a timer for each in-class work session — creates a powerful double defense against procrastination.

Rubrics presented at the start help students understand expectations before they begin. Examples and non-examples of the finished product allow students to calibrate their own work. Over time, teachers can transfer ownership of the decomposition to students, having them break assignments into their own intermediate tasks.

## Concepts applied
- [[procrastination]] — the core problem this technique addresses by removing the "distant deadline" trigger
- [[learn-it-link-it]] — staged work allows time for the link-it phase between sessions
- [[consolidation]] — spacing work over days enables overnight consolidation
- [[pomodoro-technique]] — can be combined with intermediate tasks during in-class work sessions

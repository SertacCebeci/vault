# Retrieval Cues

Durable learning requires not just storing knowledge in [[long-term-memory]], but building effective *retrieval cues* — the mental triggers that allow you to locate and activate that knowledge when you need it. Most of what we've learned is not accessible to us at any given moment; retrieval capacity is severely limited compared to storage capacity. How readily you can recall knowledge depends on context, recency of use, and the number and vividness of cues linked to it. When we say we've "forgotten" something, it often means the cues have been lost or reassigned — not that the knowledge itself has been erased.

## Elaboration

This distinction — between losing the knowledge and losing the route to it — is one of the most important in learning science. If you've moved several times, you may be unable to recall a previous address from twenty years ago. But given a multiple-choice test, you'd probably pick it out easily: the knowledge still abides, like an uncleaned closet of your mind. Context can unleash long-buried memories, as Proust illustrated with the taste of a madeleine bringing back an entire lost childhood.

There is a paradox in how cues work: sometimes forgetting old cues is *essential* for new learning. When paratroopers transition from military to smoke jumping, they must unlearn one set of deeply practiced procedures and adopt another. Having trained at the army's jump school is cited as a distinct *disadvantage* for smoke jumping — you must forget the cues to one complex body of learning in order to acquire a new one. Similarly, when learning Italian after French, you must suppress the French cue for "to be" (être) so the Italian cue (essere) can take its place. It's not the knowledge that's forgotten — a refresher course in French would quickly reactivate it — but the cues have been repurposed.

The practical implication: the task of learning is not just committing knowledge to memory. Being able to *retrieve it when you need it* is just as important. This is why [[varied-practice]], [[interleaving]], and practicing in diverse contexts are so valuable — they multiply the cues attached to knowledge, making it retrievable from more situations. And it's why [[spaced-repetition]] works: periodic retrieval keeps cues active and strengthens the routes to stored knowledge.
